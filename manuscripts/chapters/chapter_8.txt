Chapter 8 - Letting Stones Slip



The fifteenth flash signaled a brief escape from the monotony of endless paperwork.

Haldric was mentally drained. Whether it was from Praew's request this morning or his endless office duties, he couldn’t tell. 

Another day of work done. Only a million more to go, he thought, as he finally reached home.

Haldric rubbed his hand against the rough stone of the doorframe before stepping inside. He was told it was a ritual his father used to do for good luck.

The house, carved directly from the cavern wall, had a way of swallowing sound, softening it into something warmer than the harsh streets outside. The dim glow from a single embedded SolarStone flickered in the kitchen. The fading of light made their small home feel almost alive, shifting like breathing rock.

Both his parents had been Hunters, but they were killed fifteen years ago fighting the mythical beast Fendire. Since the age of five, his Grayparents on his mother’s side had taken him in. Their eldest child had died in a mining accident, and Graypa had volunteered for the Sterilization Program after Haldric’s mother was born.

After losing both children, his Grayparents had become a little… well, overprotective. They didn’t even try to hide their excitement when he failed the Hunter’s test. They’d always feared he would suffer the same fate as their daughter.

Ironically, that was one of the only times they ever talked about his mother.

Then came their voices.

“Haldric! Oh, Haldric, you’re home!” Graymother Wintrid’s voice, always soft yet bright, carried from the kitchen. He barely had time to step forward before she shuffled into view, thin hands wiping on a threadbare cloth, face lighting up like he was a hero returning from war.

Graypa Cenrick wasn’t far behind, moving slower but still wearing that same eager smile. His back was bent from years of work, his hands rough from a life that had never once been easy, but his eyes still held warmth.

“Are you alright, lad? No injuries, sprains, or broken bones?”

“Graypa, I sit at a desk sorting paper. The greatest risk at my job is a paper cut. I’m fine.”

Wintrid ignored him, already fussing, hands fluttering near his shoulders and arms, checking him over like he was made of fragile glass.

“You must be starving! Come, sit. We got you something special today.” She turned away, too fast, too eager. It was only when he noticed her thin wrist trembling slightly as she reached for a small cloth bundle that a flicker of unease settled in his gut.

The unease vanished the moment the scent hit him like a slap. She set the bundle on the table, unfolded the fabric, and revealed a small pile of rice with thin cuts of tenderized meat. Glorious, juicy, mouthwatering meat.

Not the criminally thin slices of dried beast intestine he had grown used to. Not the rice they mashed with water to stretch their portions. Real meat. With real rice.

Haldric hesitated. Too long. “They’re selling whole meals for supplementary slips now?”

He glanced at his Grayma just in time to see her shift uncomfortably.

“Are you two crazy?!” he asked, eyes still locked on the meat. “How much did this cost?”

Wintrid’s smile faltered.

“Wait.” His voice came out sharper than intended. “Didn’t they just cut back on our supplementary slips? How did you even—”

His voice trailed off as he watched Wintrid’s fingers twitch against the cloth. Cenrick looked away.

The answer settled before either of them could speak. Stone me, he thought. This food wasn’t from their supplementary slips like they always claimed. 

They hadn’t finished all their rations.

Haldric’s stomach twisted, guilt settling in his chest like a slow-building storm. They were already too thin. How had he never seen it until now? The way Wintrid’s cheeks looked hollower than before. The tremor in Cenrick’s fingers. And still, they had starved themselves—for him? 

How long? How many meals had they skipped, just in case he ever came home hungry?

“Don’t do this.” His voice was quieter now, but still firm. “I get food at work. I don’t need this.”

Wintrid’s hands clutched at her apron. Cenrick sighed, rubbing a hand across his wrinkled forehead. “We just wanted to make sure, lad. You’re workin’ hard, and—”

“You need your strength!” Haldric snapped, fists clenching at his sides. Frustration bled into something deeper. He swallowed, forcing his voice down. “You’re all I have. You need your strength so we can all stay together as long as possible. I’ve already lost my parents. I don’t want to lose you too.”

Wintrid’s fingers trembled slightly against the cloth before she let out a quiet, soft laugh. Cenrick exhaled through his nose, a tired but warm sound.

Haldric pulled them both into a quick, firm hug.

Wintrid’s arms wrapped around him, thin but still warm, still gentle. Cenrick chuckled, clapping his back once before stepping away. “Alright, alright, before you start makin’ your old Graypa cry, off to bed with you.”

Haldric smirked, rolling his eyes as he turned for the stairs.

The house felt quiet again.

He sat on his small bed, staring at the ceiling and the faint cracks in the stone where moisture had settled. He thought of Praew. Of what she had said about the food shortage. Of the quiet acceptance in his Grayparents’ voices.

He thought of how Grayma Wintrid had smiled despite everything.

And for the first time since starting his job, he felt uneasy. 

It took too long to fall asleep. It felt like his eyes had only just closed when the giant SolarStone on the First Floor outer ring triggered, flooding his room with the harsher light of day.

The next two days flew by in a blur of whispered questions and dead ends. Praew and Junya spent them digging for answers wherever they could. At one point, Praew met with Joel, Junya’s parents’ friend, to hear his account firsthand, hoping something—anything—useful would slip through. But Joel was tight-lipped, his nerves barely concealed, and most of what he said left her with more questions than answers. 

When she pressed him on what paperwork was required to access the mines, he shifted uncomfortably and muttered something under his breath.

An investigation letter. Sealed with The Prime Archon’s stamp.

Not even Haldric could help them with that. So now what?

“Focus, Praew,” Instructor Stone barked from the front of the room. “Unless you want the whole class squatting for the rest of the session instead.”

She straightened fast, pulse thudding. Right, right. Let’s focus, she thought. Sitting was much more comfortable.

Godric didn’t miss a beat.

“All common and Noble CoreStones deteriorate with use,” he said, pacing between rows. “As you use it, a small crack appears. The more it’s used, the more that crack grows, always in a perfect spiral. Once the crack reaches the center, the stone crumbles into a grainy sand. We call this crumbling. The same thing happens to Beast Stones.”

He scanned the class, watching for flickers of recognition.

“I’m sure most of you have seen a cracked SolarStone, maybe even watched one crumble. But none of you have seen what happens next up on the surface. Something we call reverse crumbling.”

The class erupted into chatter, voices overlapping

Reverse? Praew blinked. How could that even be possible?

She raised her hand, but her words came out before he called on her.

“Instructor… uhhh, Stone,” she said, still embarrassed to call him that. “If we can uncrumble stones, why doesn’t the city ever have enough Sifaraliths? Why do Hunters compete with other cities? Why do they even worry about inventory? Isn’t most of the surface covered in the sand of crumbled Sifaraliths? Something doesn’t add up.”

“You’re right,” Godric said. “That’s because we can’t uncrumble them. Not normally.”

He stepped to the front of the class, voice dropping into something heavier.

“There are only two things that can reverse the process. One is Terravoris—one of the Ten Beasts of Legend. It eats metals, gems, stones, and Sifaralith sand. When it eats enough, it somehow reforms stones inside its body.”

A few students exchanged uneasy glances.

“You can find stones in its droppings. The waste protects them from being triggered. But once the feces dries and blows away, the stones remain behind.”

“That’s revolting,” Junya muttered.

“Yes,” Godric said flatly. “That’s what it means to be a Stone Hunter. Hunters who follow Terravoris and fight each other over its droppings.”

“So they’re just feces Hunters?” Wannii asked.

Someone made a noise that might’ve been a laugh, but it cut off fast. No one wanted to be the loudest person in the room.

“Make no mistake,” Godric said. “It can be just as dangerous as being a Beast Hunter. If you ever injure a Beast of Legend, they can mark you. A tattoo appears. Each one is unique to the beast that marked you.”

He paused, his tone shifting.

“Since your other teacher took it upon herself to reveal my real name, there’s no point in hiding any longer.”

Instructor Stone—no, Silverwing Godric, Mythical Beast Hunter, rolled up his right sleeve. A glyph tattoo was etched into his skin. The ink shimmered with an impossible, ruby-like color that could only be found on the surface of a SurgeStone, as if one had been melted down and turned to ink.

 Two distinct rows of inked marks covered his right forearm, curving perfectly in tandem, mirroring the powerful bite of some fierce animal. Each tooth imprint appeared precise and sharp, arranged like a crescent. The design gave a feeling of strength and permanence, as if the jaws had clamped down on skin and never quite let go.

His left arm was not to be outdone. Rolling up his other sleeve revealed a pair of sapphire-blue horns curving gracefully upward. They mirrored the formidable shape of a Tarnox’s horns, yet held an elegance no Tarnox from any picture book Praew had read could ever match. The rich, gemstone hue shimmered subtly beneath the skin, giving the inked design an almost regal quality.

The classroom was silent in worship and shock. All eyes were fixed on his arms.

Two!? He bolting faced two Beasts of Legend and survived!? Praew couldn’t even breathe in his presence—especially when he began unbuttoning his shirt, turned around, and lowered it to his waist.

A massive silver and yellow glyph of a wing stretched across his back, from just above his left hip to his right shoulder. The detail was stunning. The wing shimmered as if the surface of the ink caught light with every breath.

Praew recognized it instantly.

“Isn’t that the wing of Boltrax?” she asked.

Godric buttoned his shirt without answering.

No wonder they call him Silverwing Godric.

“We call these territory marks,” Godric said. “Because they let other Beasts of Legend know… this is their prey to be hunted.”

He let the weight of that settle before continuing.

“These glyphs act as a form of tracking. They can find you anywhere, anytime. The tattoo doesn’t disappear. Not even if the beast dies.”

The room was stunned silent as Godric continued.

“There are only two ways to remove one. 

“First, you die. It’ll disappear from your body the moment you do. Second… is highly unlikely, so don’t count on it.”

He crossed his arms.

“It’s theorized that Beasts of Legend can only mark so many Hunters at once. There are rare reports of a mark vanishing, but only if someone else becomes a higher priority. Someone who angers them more than you did. But don’t get hopeful. Hunters die all the time. The beast just replaces them without even trying.”

No one spoke, not because they had no questions, but because they were terrified of the answers.

“Your only guaranteed way to hide is to be underground. A cave. A tunnel. Deep enough to lose them.”

He let that sit.

“Terravoris is the exception. Make too much noise, get in his way, or catch him on a bad day, and a dark brown mark appears on the back of your hand. Congratulations. You’re a Mythical Stone Hunter. Now try not to die.”

No one moved or  interrupted.

“Terravoris holds a grudge worse than any other beast. He can find you anywhere. Even underground. Other beasts might ignore you if it’s inconvenient. Terravoris and the ones he commands will track you to the other side of the planet just for being too loud.”

His voice dropped further.

“That’s why no one marked by Terravoris is ever allowed back into the city. They could bring disaster to us all and…”

His eyes swept the class.

“It’s been too long since someone interrupted me. Are you all paying attention?”

“Sir,” Bpen said slowly. “You just hit us with a boulder. I think I can speak for everyone when I say we need time to process this. You just confirmed you’re one of only two mythical Beast Hunters in the city, you faced three Beasts of Legend and lived, plus you met Srevlis. And don’t forget the Tarnox that got into the city… sir.”

“Oh, would you all quit that bolting look in your eyes?” Godric snapped. “I can already see it. You’re all itching to get your first mark. Well, I’m telling you now, that’s bolting stupid. There’s no glory in death. I did what needed to be done. That’s all I’m trying to teach you. Do what needs to be done.”

The room stayed quiet.

“Now, if you’re all done processing, we still need to go over the other way reverse crumbling can happen. And sometimes, it’s even more dangerous than Terravoris.”

He resumed pacing.

“As you know, nature can be Embedded with Nature Stones. We believe a sentient tornado is Embedded with a powerful TerraStone, much like the beast Terravoris. We call it Haiyana. It’s a giant force of nature.”

He glanced at the walls like he could feel it even now.

“It seems to choose what it pulls in. Two Hunters could be hit—only one might die, while the other walks away untouched. If it chooses you, your death is certain. It sucks in Sifaralith sand and spits out CoreStones.”

He let the words hang a moment before continuing.

“There’s no use chasing it. Haiyana is completely unpredictable. We can’t predict its trajectory, nor where the stones will land. They could be launched to the other side of the planet, the ocean, or two feet in front of it.”

He met their eyes again.

“If you see it in the distance, don’t bother running. Use every TerraStone you have to dig as deep as possible and hope it doesn’t feel like taking your life.”

A long silence followed.

“That’s all for today’s lecture. Any questions?”

Praew had one, but so did everyone else. Every hand in the room went up. Godric pointed to a guy in the back, someone Praew had never bothered to learn the name of.

“Yeah, uhh… Instructor Stone Godric, sir?”

Godric sighed. “Spit it out, kid. You’re holding up the class.”

The student sat up straighter. “Sorry, sir. Uhh… What's an ocean?”

All of the other hands dropped in unison. Praew’s included.

Godric gave them all a flat stare, clearly annoyed “Class dismissed,” he barked.

Praew’s early morning trek to class felt blissfully serene, although admittedly, she felt a little guilty for enjoying the silence.  

     Not once had Mai Azadi shown up since their argument. She was probably taking a different route to avoid her. She did tell Mai to stay away. She just didn’t expect someone who thought it was okay to stalk her friends to actually listen. 

     Still, Praew had been wanting to talk to her. About—well, everything. And soon, she just might get her chance.

     Ghost Operation was scheduled for tomorrow morning, but not for her. Mai had pulled her from the class so she wouldn’t have to cross a line she didn’t believe in. It wouldn’t be treasonous if she stopped by, would it? If she went early enough, she could probably catch Mai alone.

But first, she just had to survive another Shadow Recon class.



***



That proved to be more difficult than she thought. It was week two and she couldn’t even take a single step without triggering the SolarStones in the first silent movement course. She didn’t even bother attempting to test out last week, choosing to spend the time investigating with Junya over making a complete fool of herself. 

     Everyone somehow passed but Junya and her. Surprising, but fair. That’s what they got for skipping. What she found unfair, however, was that somehow Junya crossed the path today perfectly, like it was nothing.

     That’s just not fair! Praew almost yelled but stopped herself. No one has seen Jorpen since his outburst in this class last week. So no one else dared to defy their instructor’s no speaking rule.

     Praew’s right eye started to twitch. She patted it three times before throwing away the bad omen. Praew believed if her right eye twitched, she was going to get in an argument or something really bad was going to happen.

     As the class concluded, Praew stepped outside the room, waved goodbye to Junya and Bpen, and waited for Wannii. She’d noticed that Wannii has been cold and distant the last few days. Praew occasionally caught Wannii glaring at her, rolling her eyes and shooting dirty looks. Praew was going to get to the bottom of it today.

     Concentrating on the ground as she practiced silent step, Wannii stumbled in the doorframe. Clearly surprised to see Praew waiting for her. Wannii brushed a hand through her short black hair, slicking it back slightly. Her recent haircut was cute. It rested only halfway down the ears revealing piercings Praew hadn’t noticed before. With her small nose, scrunched brow, and naturally intense expression, Wannii always looked like she was scowling. Honestly these days, she probably was.

“Hey, Wannii! I love the haircut. It’s so cute!”

“Thanks,” Wannii said, arms folding across her chest, voice flat and uninviting.

Really? That’s it? Ugh, this woman.

“Of course!” Praew pushed on, forcing lightness into her voice. “Have you always had your ears pierced? I have six piercings myself.”

     “Yeah,” Wannii said with an exaggerated sigh.

     That was on purpose. It had to be. Girl, if you’re jealous just say so. You obviously have a problem with me—“So just spit it out already!”

  Wannii stopped mid-step.

  Great. Not again, Praew.

  “Fine!” Wannii snapped, raising her voice to match the tension in the air. “Stay away from Junya! You’re way too dangerous!”

  “What?”

  “You know what she’s looking into! I know she told you! And the funny thing is—I was glad she did! You’re so smart, logical, considerate. Obviously, you’d tell her to stop poking her nose around an obvious trap before she gets herself killed, right?”

  “Wannii, I—” Praew didn’t get the chance to finish.

  “No! No, you did not. Instead, you thought it was a good idea to encourage her and tell her story to someone from The Prime Archon’s office?! You might as well kill her yourself!”

  “ No!” Praew argued. “He’s my best friend. We can trust him!”

  “You… can trust him.” Wannii clarified “We don’t even know him. And you really expect me to be okay with you putting my best friend’s life in the hands of someone who works for the very place that executes inquisitive people like her? Haven’t you heard the saying? ‘What faith is owed to a stranger when betrayal may protect them from danger?’”

     “He wouldn’t do that,” Praew’s fists tightened until her knuckles went white. She was sick of being scolded. By Haldric, Mai, Godric, and now Wannii too? The irony wasn’t lost on her. Wannii found her dangerous… just like Praew felt about Mai.

  Her head dropped. She couldn’t blame Wannii. What they were doing was irresponsible, stupid, and dangerous… and Wannii was just worried about Junya. Wait, I may not know Junya as long, but I’m worried about her too!

  “Is that so bad?” Praew asked calmly, lifting her head and locking eyes with Wannii. “Maybe you’re right. But I think it’s better not to pretend. We both know she’ll never give up. It’s her parents we’re talking about. Can you honestly say you wouldn’t do the same?”

  She stepped forward, voice unwavering. “Do you really think scolding her and ignoring her tears will stop her? If you do, then I’ve severely overestimated your intelligence. Hide behind your words all you want, Wannii. But no shadow you cast will ever stretch far enough to hide your cowardice. If you really cared about Junya, you would never leave her side. At least with me, if we get caught, I won’t let her suffer alone again.”

  Wannii looked around the hallway, then leaned in, her voice quieter now, but no less sharp. “Do you even know what she’s planning, Praew? Junya plans to sneak into the mines at night and investigate the area alone. She’s making sure no one goes with her so no one else gets implicated. I only found out because she forgot to hide a few maps. In her room”

We had to identify the triggers of a set of weak Sifaraliths, one by one, and place them gently into a Hunter pouch without setting off a chain reaction. The pouches were embedded with an EchoStone. If even one trigger fired inside, the bag would scream out loud: You failed.

     “I turned to see Junya’s face, frozen, hollow. She looked like she hadn’t slept in days. And I’d been so caught up in the test, I didn’t even notice. Some friend, right?”

     Wannii’s voice cracked, but she pushed on.

     “Of course she made a mistake. I would’ve too, if I were mourning and still trying to hold it together.

     “But Stormhaven doesn’t care. They already took her parents, her home, her belongings. Now they were taking her dream of reaching the surface.

     “I just stood there, final Sifaralith still in hand. If I placed it in the correct pouch, I would become a Hunter. But who knows when—or if—I’d be there when she needed me most.

“I wasn’t going to let them take away her only friend too.

“So I made my choice. I let the final stone slip. It landed in the wrong pouch, and it screamed for me too.”

  Her eyes burned. Wannii had the look of someone who stood by their decision with no regret. “I would give my life for her without a second thought. You don’t know me.”

     Wannii stepped closer now, fists trembling. “You think I haven’t tried it your way? Junya’s been doing this for a while now—taking stupid risks, sneaking around, skipping classes, stealing from the Archon and not clueing me in until after the fact. She says it’s to protect me, but really? She just doesn’t want anyone stopping her. I tried talking her down. Backing off. Discouraging her. And it was working. Zephyrs take you, it was working!”

  Her whole body shook now. “Then you came and ruined it. I wanted to believe things could still change. But I know now—I can’t stop her. So I’m asking you… can you at least make sure she doesn’t go alone?”

  Praew took a breath, then slowly extended the back of her hand.

  “Let’s make sure together, Wannii. We’ll both keep an eye on her. Make sure she knows how dangerous this is, and that she can’t be impulsive.”

     Praew smiled. “Junya’s lucky to have a friend like you.”

    “Thanks,” Wannii said. “She’s lucky to have you too.”

    “You both do,” Praew replied, still smiling as they walked forward and turned the corner.

     Wannii hesitated, about to say something else—but froze.

     Praew froze too. “Zephyrs take me, she whispered.”

    Godric was waiting in the hallway, arms crossed, leaning against the wall.

    “Well,” he muttered, pushing off the wall and stepping toward them. “That was quite the argument you two were having near a government-run facility.”

     His eyes narrowed. “Now, I didn’t hear what you bolting emotional wrecks were screaming about, but it sounded like something I should’ve. Would either of you care to fill me in?”

This is bad, I should have been more cautious.

    “Oh, nothing,” Praew said, holding Godric’s gaze without flinching. “Just arguing about whose Hunter Exam was harder.”

“Yeah,” Wannii added flatly.

 “I see,” he muttered. “Just be mindful of where you decide to have your mental breakdowns. You’re dismissed.”

Praew shared a look with Wannii. They both started walking quickly down the hall—

“Dangerous habit… letting stones slip.” Godric’s voice cut through the silence behind them.  “It’s not uncommon for one to end up missing.”

     Praew and Wannii shared a wide-eyed look, fear rising like static between them. Then they bolted in opposite directions, not daring to speak.
