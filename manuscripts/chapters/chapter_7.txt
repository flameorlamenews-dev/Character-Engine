Chapter 7 -  Stormhaven Mysteries





Junya sat with her knees pulled up, thumb tracing invisible lines across the seam of her pants. Praew joined her sitting on the stone furniture in the living room. Junya hadn’t spoken in a while, not since the food shortage came up again. Praew waited, watching the tension gather in her shoulders like a storm.

Then, finally, a breath.

“I was looking into my parents’ deaths. Something about it just felt… off.”

“Off how?” Praew asked, leaning in.

“Well, for starters,” Junya said, fingers fidgeting faster now, “they just didn’t come home. No warning, no message. I skipped school the next day and searched all three Floors of the city.”

Praew blinked. “Wait—you actually skipped general education?”

Junya gave a small, guilty smile. “Yeah. I know. Pretty bold, right?”

“How did you not get caught?”

“Oh, that part was easy,” Junya said with a weak laugh. “I stole an administrative uniform. No one questions someone who looks like they belong.”

Praew raised an eyebrow but didn’t interrupt.

“I spent two days searching,” Junya continued. “Eventually I found the mine entrance on the Third Floor, but they wouldn’t let me in. Said I needed clearance papers. Whatever, I didn’t have any.”

Junya sighed.

“Luckily, I knew a few of my parents’ friends who worked down there. I went to four different homes. None of them had heard about a cave-in. Nothing. When they asked their supervisors, they were told my parents were just home sick.”

Praew’s expression darkened. “Sick?”

Junya nodded. “For ten days, that was the story. Then… administration showed up. But even after that, my parents’ friends still hadn’t heard anything. That’s when Joel—one of their closest friends—told me something weird.”

She shifted forward, voice low now. “He worked the same shaft. He said there was no cave-in. No rescue efforts. No bodies. Just a sudden order to block off the shaft completely. The last thing he remembered was hearing my dad complain about getting new orders to dig straight down. Then a foreman burst into the area, cursing under his breath, and ran toward that area.”

Praew sat still, jaw clenched.

“After that? Everything went quiet,” Junya said. “The shaft was sealed. The rest of the crew was told my parents were sick and sent home. But no one ever saw them again.”

She looked down at her hands. “Joel told me, ‘Why would they say your parents are home sick if they’re trapped in a mine?’ He said it didn’t add up.”

Praew nodded slowly. “Because it doesn’t.”

“Joel said he might know someone who could help,” Junya added. “His wife didn’t want him to tell me anything. She was scared. They argued—right in front of me. But Joel told her I had a right to know the truth and ‘that man’ was likely the only one who could give me that.”

“Note,” Praew said flatly.

“Note,” Junya repeated, nodding slowly. “He didn’t know anything about my parents—not really. Said he’d heard nothing specific about the mines. I felt like the whole trip was a waste. Just another dead end.”

She paused, arms crossed now, eyes low.

“I was about to leave when he said something… strange. Out of nowhere. Told me I was right to question everything. Said even our ration slips are a lie. And that digging down on the Third Floor is just as dangerous as doing it on the First or Second. Whatever that means.”

“Did you ask for a clearer answer?” Praew asked, her annoyance with this Note person starting to show.

“Of course I did,” Junya muttered. “But he just laughed and said he won’t tell me more until I figure out what any of that actually means first.”

She cleared her throat.

Praew could tell Junya was trying not to crumble again, trying to hold back her tears and frustration, even though her voice kept cracking.

She exhaled shakily. “So yeah. That’s where I’m at. A reason to believe the food shortage might be fake, one sealed shaft, zero answers… and a lot of questions.”

“That doesn’t tell me a lot,” Praew said, standing up and stretching. “I guess we will just have to get more answers together.”

Junya began to tear up again. “Thank you,” she choked out. “When I told Wannii, she… well, she keeps acting like I’m crazy and keeps telling me to forget about Note, everything I heard. She told me to let my parents rest. She’s been my friend since early childhood. My parents even secretly fed her before. How can she be so heartless?”

“I don’t know,” Praew said thoughtfully. “It may seem that way, but don’t you think she’s saying all that because she knows it’s probably true? Secrets like this are dangerous. She’s probably just trying to keep you safe.”

“Yeah,” Junya said, a little sheepish now. “You’re probably right.”

Praew placed a hand on Junya’s shoulder. “Wannii’s a good friend. Me, not so much. I’m too reckless to give you common sense. I know someone close who works in government administration. I think it’s time I visit an old friend to see what we can dig up for you.”

Junya nodded, forcing a brave smile as Praew walked out the door and headed home. 

Praew had the next day off. That meant she could drop by the ration station near The Prime Archon’s office—the one Haldric would go to for breakfast. And honestly, she probably should. It had been seven days since they’d failed the Hunter test. Time to see how he was holding up.

The following day, Praew was already seated, waiting for Haldric at the ration station. It was nearly identical to the one she normally visited, just larger. Rows of identical stone tables filled the space, all lined with the same slumped shoulders and lowered gazes. The SolarStones overhead gave off a weak, even light—just enough to see your food, not enough to feel awake.

The air hung heavy with the stench of overcooked rice and chewy intestines. Nutrient-packed, sure, but lifeless. The kind of meal you only enjoyed if you didn’t have to eat it nearly every day. A thick, flavorless mush that made teeth optional—except for the strips of beast intestine, which made them work overtime. It was the most confusing meal Praew had ever eaten. Was it a drink, a soup, or a punishment?

She spotted Haldric before he noticed her. He looked better than she expected. Getting betrayed by his best friend and being denied the chance to ever see the surface could have broken him.

But the smile he gave her the moment their eyes met…

Some smiles just couldn’t be faked.

Haldric got his food and made his way over, the wooden tray balanced neatly in one hand. He dropped into the seat across from her at the stone table with practiced ease.

Praew’s eyes drifted to his tray. 

It wasn’t just smaller than hers. It was barely enough to count as a full meal.

Her brows drew together. She was still in classes, technically. Was she still getting double portions compared to everyone else? Administration was notoriously known for their generous rations. It was probably part of why they were so hated by the rest of the city.

But if this was what Haldric got… she couldn’t imagine what everyone else was living on.

Her appetite flickered.

“What seems to be the problem, constable?” Haldric asked, his voice light with amusement. “Am I under arrest?”

Praew chuckled softly. “Not this time,” she said. “I just wanted to check on you.”

“Right,” Haldric replied. “Well, I’m not giving you my ration slips, if that’s what you’re after, so don’t bother.”

Praew didn’t join Haldric’s laughter this time. She pushed at her rice with the edge of her utensils, sifting through it as if it might rearrange itself into something better. Her fingers moved absently, the food forgotten.

Then she finally spoke.

“Haldric, have you noticed anything… odd at your job?”

He glanced at her, pausing mid-bite.

“Odd?” he repeated. “I work in administration for The bolting Prime Archon. Every document I go through seems odd to me. You’re gonna have to be more specific.”

He looked around the room, probably to make sure no one had heard him use The Prime’s name in vain.

Praew sighed, setting down her utensils and rubbing her temple.

“I mean… look at us. Look at everyone.”

She gestured at his tray, at the thinning portions he’d somehow grown used to.

Haldric didn’t need to look. He wasn’t stupid. It was already impossible to ignore. The quiet fatigue hanging over the cafeteria wasn’t just exhaustion—it was hunger.

Praew continued.

“Something doesn’t add up about the food shortage. If our Hunters are having this much trouble getting food, why not accept more Hunters?”

“I don’t know what to tell you,” Haldric said. His eyes darted around the room again before he leaned in slightly.

“I’ve been thinking the same thing. But I haven’t heard anything out of the ordinary.”

“I don’t care if I eat less. I can handle it. But my parents? They’re older. They need more. And they’re getting weaker.”

She didn’t look at Haldric as she spoke. She didn’t have to. He had to have seen it too. His Grayparents, a generation older than her parents, were likely faring even worse. Like the thinner faces and heavier movements she saw in her own parents.

She stared down at her tray, pushing a lump of rice to the side with the edge of her utensils.

“They used to fuss over me when I got home. Always asking if I ate enough, if I had clean clothes, if I was sleeping okay.”

She set the utensils down gently.

“But lately… they just smile. Say they’re proud of me. And then they sit back down like even that took too much energy.”

She trailed off. The words stuck somewhere she couldn’t reach.

They were too tired to even fuss. She felt her eyes start to water.

She came here for Junya. But perhaps this had always been on her mind.

There was a strange power in voicing her worries. It was like dislodging a single stone, only to feel the weight of the whole mountain shift behind it.

“Okay, I get it,” Haldric said. “I’ll do some digging and see what I can find. Just… don’t do anything stupid.”

“What? Me?” Praew said with feigned innocence, pushing down her emotions and forcing a smile.

“I thought I was supposed to be the impulsive and emotional one,” Haldric said, eyeing her. “There’s something you’re not telling me, and that worries me.”

“I—” Praew looked away, the word catching in her throat.

Haldric didn’t let up.

“I may not be as smart as you, but I can tell when something’s off. What is it? What’s going on?”

Praew took a deep breath, steadying herself before meeting his eyes.

“I was going to tell you anyway,” she said. “I have a friend who needs you to look into a suspicious cave-in and the mysterious disappearance of two individuals.”

Haldric choked on his drink, tray already empty. When had he finished it?

“Praew, could you please stop being so nosy? Why are you always getting involved in everyone else’s problems?” He threw his hands slightly. “This sounds even more dangerous than the food shortage!”

“Come on,” she said, leaning in. “Don’t tell me you already forgot. A Hunter never ignores—”

“We’re not Hunters, Praew!” Haldric snapped, voice rising.

Yeah… it still bothered him. That was a stupid thing to say. She made a mental note.

“You’re right. I’m sorry,” Praew said quietly. She took a bite of rice, letting the silence settle before swallowing and continuing.

“It’s too bad, though. She needed someone who could hunt down what happened to her parents. But you’re right, Haldric. It would be way too dangerous. Someone would have to risk their life becoming her hero.”

She took another bite of rice, chewed slowly, then swallowed.

“Such a shame…” She tilted her head, then continued.

“She’s so pretty. Seeing her cry nearly broke me.”

“I know what you’re doing, Praew,” Haldric said flatly.

“Is it working?” she asked, adding a wink.

“Stone me. You’re suggesting I just… snoop around, digging through sensitive or classified incident reports?”

Praew smirked. “I prefer to call it an investigation.”

Haldric shook his head. “This is a terrible idea.”

“But you’re considering it,” Praew said, that look on his face making it obvious he was.

She leaned forward, her expression serious. Haldric tapped his fingers against the edge of his tray. He was mulling it over. It wasn’t just curiosity anymore. Like her, he would need to learn the truth by any means necessary.

“Alright,” he said finally. “You handle your end. Train, ask questions, see if you hear anything. I’ll start looking into storage and incident reports.”

Praew grinned. “And every six days, we compare notes.”

Haldric rolled his eyes. “You can just say a week, Praew.”

She gasped, putting on her most innocent voice. “I just didn’t want you to get confused!”

Her smirk widened, mischievous.

Haldric frowned. “What was that supposed to mean?”

Praew’s smile only grew. “I mean, after all… I remember a certain someone mixing up the seven planets with the six-day week once.” She tapped her chin dramatically. “Remind me, who was that again?”

Haldric stiffened. “Oh stone me, I was a child!"

“Of course you were!” Praew beamed, her voice dripping with false sympathy. “And an actual grown adult would have to be incredibly stupid to assume a week was seven days.”

Haldric scowled, but Praew was already laughing.

Her laughter cut through the dull murmur of the cafeteria.

Haldric didn’t answer. Just watched her for a moment, shaking his head as her laughter spilled out, unburdened by the world around them.

She caught the look in his eyes before he turned away. Like he was trying not to smile. Like he almost forgot to guard whatever it was he was holding back.

“Just don’t do anything too reckless,” he said, leveling his gaze.

She smirked, the last trace of laughter still warming her cheeks. “No promises.”

  Haldric rolled his eyes. “We meet in three weeks. I need time to actually look into things. What are their names, and when did they disappear?”



***



After going over the details of Junya’s case, Praew and Haldric said their goodbyes. She knew she’d made the right choice going to him. When he put his mind to something, no one was more capable.

Praew found herself in a rare good mood as she walked home. She was happy. Happy to see her best friend again, happy to have made progress helping Junya, and happy to be spending the rest of the day with her parents, enjoying some much-needed, stress-free relaxation.

She walked inside—and cursed in front of her parents.

Not a light one, like “Zephyrs take me.” No. A full-throated, blasphemous belt of a slur involving a mother’s private part falling on the floor.

Her parents gasped. She ignored them.

Because sitting in her living room, casually chatting with them, was Mai Azadi. Smiling. Dagger at her hip. 

The worst person Praew could imagine had somehow found where she lived.

A psychopathic assassin who was inches away from her parents.

“What are you doing here?” Praew yelled, eyes widening as she inched forward, trying to position herself between Mai and her parents without drawing attention to it.

“Oh, nothing. I just had time to kill,” Mai said casually.

Was that a threat or a joke? Praew couldn’t tell. She glanced around for anything she could use as a weapon, but there was nothing. Everything not lodged in stone was either too bulky or too flimsy to stop a blade.

“Oh yeah?” Praew said, now fully between them. A plan began to form. “Well, that’s perfect. There are some things I’ve been just dying to know. How about we go for a walk?”

“Well, actually—” Mai began.

“Mae, Papa, we’ll be back,” Praew called over her shoulder, not letting Mai finish.

Praew ‘clumsily’ bumped Mai’s side. She didn’t notice, right? Praew used her other hand to grab Mai’s before she had a chance to notice anything missing. 

She ran as hard as she could, Mai in tow, until they reached an abandoned home carved entirely from stone. It hadn’t been repurposed yet. Thin cracks ran through the walls and ceiling, as if the structure itself had grown tired of holding on. The floor was littered with gravel-like dust, loose debris shifting beneath her boots.

Praew slammed the door shut behind them, panting. The sudden impact stirred the air, sending tiny motes of dust spiraling into the light.

She looked up, hand on her hips as she tried to catch her breath. A single SolarStone glowed overhead, fractured with a spiral-shaped crack. The beam it cast broke apart, splitting across the ruined walls. It looked ready to crumble—just like her life right now. Spiraling, splintering, falling apart.

She glanced over at Mai. That bolting monster wasn’t even a little out of breath. Mai just smiled beneath the cracked SolarStone overhead. The shadow hiding her eyes painted her more menacingly than Praew could have ever imagined.

“Wow, that was fun,” Mai said brightly. “Nothing like a good midday jog! Thank you for carrying my dagger for me, by the way. Made the run easier. Very thoughtful of you. Now, how do you plan to return it? My hand? My throat? Ooh, my wrist? I love guessing games. Do I get a prize if I—”

“Zephyrs take me!” Praew snapped, brandishing the dagger. “Shut your bolting mouth unless I ask you a question!”

Mai tilted her head. “Oh, interrogation. Hmm. No.” She stepped forward. “You’re holding the dagger wrong. And your stance is awful.”

Then, without hesitation, she adjusted Praew’s arms like a teacher correcting a child.

Was this girl serious right now?

“How did you find my home?” Praew demanded.

“Oh, I just followed you yesterday,” Mai said, like it was the most normal thing in the world. “You almost fooled me, too. For a moment, I thought Junya’s place was yours. I’m glad I waited until one of you left to be sure. Rookie mistake, am I right?”

Praew said nothing at first. She just stared, jaw slack. That must be what this was about. Mai must’ve overheard them. So she is here to kill me.

Her voice came out flat. “Why? If you planned to kill me from the start, why stalk me? Why come to my house? You could’ve done it any time. Why bring my parents into it?”

“Kill you?” Mai laughed. “Silly. I’m not here to kill you. I’m here to keep you!”

Praew must have made a face because even Mai looked flustered.

“I mean, not like that! I meant your friendship. I’m here to keep your friendship! I feel like we got off on the wrong foot yesterday and I—”

Praew laughed bitterly, lowering the knife. Who was she kidding? If Mai wanted her dead, Praew couldn’t stop her.

“My friendship?” Praew snapped. “You can’t be serious. We were never friends, Mai. And I would never be friends with a psychopathic murderer like you. I’d rather die than kill someone. You get off on blood. What makes you think we could ever be friends?”

Mai opened her mouth to speak, but Praew wasn’t finished.

“No! Don’t even bother answering that. You’re sick. And I want you nowhere near my parents.”

She tossed the dagger at Mai’s feet.

“Just say away from me.”

The assassin dropped her head. For once, the smile vanished.

Praew turned to leave.

“What do you know?” Mai’s voice cracked.

Praew froze. She turned back. Tears ran down Mai’s face.

Was I too harsh? But… my parents. I have to keep them safe. And … Mai was far from stable.

Mai raised her voice, nearly shouting. “What do you know?! I am Stormhaven’s best assassin. The only person to pass all the Shadow Intelligence tests on the first day. But that’s all I want to be seen as, a ‘person’.”

Her voice caught. She swallowed hard, fists clenched.

 “I’m only the best because I was raised to be the best. While you sat in classrooms, I trained to kill. You think I’m good at it because I like it?”

She stepped forward.

“I stay positive because if I don’t… if I don’t—” She shook her head, hard. “I would have killed myself long ago. Do you know what they promise us if we keep a flawless kill record for five years? We’re allowed to make a friend. That’s it. After five years of being… being perfect. Of killing whoever they pointed to. And only if we’ve proven we’re so deadly that no one can distract us, we’re allowed one friend. I’m the first to ever earn that.”

Her voice broke.

“Yeah, I’m awful at it. I don’t know what I’m doing. Yeah, maybe I am sick. But don’t pretend you know me. If we’re being honest, let’s really be honest.”

She pointed at Praew.

“This is really about that man you tried to save. The one I killed.”

“Yeah, well maybe it is!” Praew snapped back “Look, I’m sorry to hear about your messed-up life, but that doesn’t change the fact that you killed an innocent man. Right in front of me! I still feel his blood on me. I still saw his eyes when I slept last night.  How could you do that?”

“You don’t know everything, Praew!” Mai shouted, arms flailing.

Then her voice dropped low. Too low. Drained.

“That man? His wife and daughter were reported sick. No one had seen them in weeks. Meanwhile, he was flaunting his rations. Sharing meat with coworkers. Smiling while they ate.”

Praew’s stomach twisted.

“Eventually people got suspicious. His neighbors reported a smell. When they broke in… they found his wife and four-year-old daughter gutted. Partially cooked. He’d been eating them. Feeding them to others. Without telling them.”

Praew nearly collapsed. Her hand reached out, steadying herself against the wall. Her legs wouldn’t stop shaking.

“That’s the kind of man you mourn.” Mai shook her head. “That man had the audacity to beg for his life, saying his wife and daughter needed him. But I went to his house myself. I saw their remains… You condemn me but protect people like that?”

Mai bent down, picked up the dagger, and sheathed it.

She walked past Praew toward the door.

Her voice, barely more than a whisper, sliced through the air behind her.

“You don’t have to like what I do. But before you judge someone… don’t ever assume you know the whole story.”

Without another word, she was gone. Praew stared at the fissures in the wall as Mai’s words echoed in her head. 

“You condemn me but protect people like that?” 

Why had she been so hard on Mai?

Praew wasn’t stupid. She knew executioners existed. Knew what their job meant. And yet, she had acted like Mai was the only monster in the room. Everyone in her class had killed someone that day. Junya. The others. So why had she only cared when it was Mai? Was it really that simple? Just because she saw it?

She leaned her head back, jaw tight. That man said he was innocent, and she believed him without question. All because she didn’t want to hurt him.

And she still hadn’t thanked Mai. Not for saving her life. Not for failing her from the class so she wouldn’t be forced to kill anyone else. Instead, she called her a psychopath.

Zephyrs take me… way to show appreciation, Praew.

She did not move. Just sat in that cold, dim room, letting the guilt twist in her chest until the fifteenth flash, the hour most people got off work.

When she finally returned home, she was too exhausted to explain anything to her parents. After successfully reassuring them she was fine, she went straight to bed. Praew had a feeling she wouldn’t see that man in her dreams again.
