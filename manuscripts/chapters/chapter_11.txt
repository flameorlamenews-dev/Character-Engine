Chapter 11 - Fired



"Once again, I stand as the final wall between two opposing ideals."



The first thing Haldric noticed was the air—crisper, richer, almost electric in his lungs. He took a slow breath, deeper than usual, and the difference was immediate. His chest didn’t burn anymore. His muscles no longer ached. 

He blinked, adjusting to the dim lighting.

He was in a bed—stone-carved, embedded into the wall like all Hunter bunks. The room smelled of rock-dust and sweat, the unmistakable scent of those who lived their lives in battle. His vision was clearer than it had ever been, sharper.

The blinding pain from the surface light was gone. He could see perfectly again. And standing just a few paces away, waiting patiently, was The Prime Archon.

Haldric sat up immediately. “Sir?”

The Prime Archon seemed troubled at first. Then his lips curled into a smile. “Ahh, you’re awake!”

Haldric swung his legs over the edge of the bed, straightening his posture. “How long was I out?”

“Not for terribly long. Your body was in dire need of recuperation.” The Prime Archon folded his hands behind his back. “Shall we take a walk?”

Haldric hesitated for only a second. “Of course, sir.”

He stood, testing his weight. His balance felt different. His steps were lighter. His strength more… present. As if his entire body had been recalibrated overnight.

The Prime Archon watched Haldric with a smirk.

“What’s so funny?” Haldric asked.

The Prime turned away, as if to hide his amusement. “Come,” he said, nodding toward the hall. “Let’s talk.”



The tunnels were quieter than usual at this hour. The faint hum of distant movement echoed through the stone, but for the most part, it was just the two of them.

The Prime Archon led him through the winding pathways, slow and deliberate. “Tell me, Haldric. How do you feel?”

Haldric considered. “Different.”

The Prime Archon hummed. “Good. That’s how it should be.”

He gestured around them, as if motioning to the walls, the stone, the very air itself.

“Your body has acclimated. Your lungs have expanded. Your muscles are more efficient. Your stamina, speed, and strength are all enhanced. Every Hunter experiences this after spending time near the surface. It’s an evolution—a natural mutation.”

Haldric frowned slightly. “Mutation?”

The Prime Archon nodded. “A refinement. A necessary adaptation. The surface is harsh, but in return, it strengthens us. Hunters—true Hunters—are not the same as other people. They aren’t just trained for this life, Haldric. They’re built for it.”

Something shifted in The Prime’s voice. His usual stoicism softened into a kind smile—one Haldric hadn’t seen in twelve years. Not since he was a child playing with Asher. But that was before Mr. Rivers became The Prime Archon.

“When I acclimated, the hardest part was getting used to my new strength,” The Prime said with a chuckle. “You don’t want to know how many doors and tables I accidentally destroyed.”

Haldric stiffened slightly.

He had never questioned The Prime Archon’s strength before. He was feared, respected, but in an administrative sense, in his control, his tactics, his mind. But now, standing beside him, hearing the weight behind his words, Haldric was seeing him in an entirely different way. The Prime Archon was enhanced. Haldric exhaled, steadying himself. He wasn’t sure what to say to that, but he had the distinct feeling that today would change everything.

At first, neither of them spoke. Then, The Prime Archon finally broke the silence. “How have you been, Haldric? Since failing the test?”

Haldric blinked. That was unexpected. No one had asked him that before. He let out a slow breath, thinking.

“If I’m being honest, sir… I hated Asher. And for some reason, I hated you too.” He sighed. “But then I realized, if Asher didn’t betray me, neither of us would have succeeded. And you know what? I’m glad one of us made it.”

The words surprised even him. Saying it out loud felt… final.

The Prime Archon hummed, nodding. Then, quietly:

“No, you were right to hate me.”

Haldric frowned, glancing at him.

The Prime Archon’s voice was regretful. “It’s my fault after all.”

He kept walking, slower now.

“I don’t know where I went wrong with Asher. Maybe I pushed him too hard. Maybe I shouldn’t have tried to make him take after me. After all, who in their right mind would want to be the most hated man in the city?”

A dry chuckle, self-deprecating. Then his voice softened.

 “But he had everything, Haldric. He was honest. Good. Caring. Exactly what the city needs. And instead of rising to that, he betrayed his closest friends and ran off to be a Hunter.”

  He exhaled. “I’m sorry, Haldric. It should have been you.”

  Haldric gasped. He should have been a Hunter. That wasn’t something he had ever expected to hear from The Prime Archon. His fingers curled at his sides, but before he could even begin to process what that meant, The Prime Archon stopped walking, turned to him, and met his eyes.

“I guess what I’m trying to tell you is… you’re fired.”

Silence followed. The words didn’t make sense at first. Fired? …What?

When it clicked, Haldric snapped. “I’M BOLTING WHAT?”

The Prime only smiled. And in that moment, Haldric realized, it was a joke.

It WAS a joke… Right? Panic crept up His spine.

The Prime raised his hand, fingers flicking in a subtle but deliberate gesture.

A low rumble shook the cavern.

Haldric’s breath hitched as the groan of shifting stone filled the air. The final gate was opening.

Wind rushed in, crisp and unfiltered, carrying a scent so new it sent a shiver down his spine. The air felt different. Lighter, freer, charged with something alive.

The Prime’s voice cut through the hum of the wind. Calm. Absolute.

“You can’t go back to your old life, Haldric. Your days in administration are over.”

Haldric barely processed the words.

His eyes were locked forward as the gate continued to part, unveiling the last barrier between him and everything he’d ever wanted.

Footsteps echoed behind him. Someone was approaching.

The man was tall, broad-shouldered, his presence heavy with an unspoken authority. His gaze flicked over Haldric, sharp and assessing. Familiar.

The Hunter from the exam.

Haldric felt a single short-lived spike of hatred. He hadn’t seen this Hunter since the day he failed.

The Prime motioned toward the man. “I believe you’ve met Hunter Steel.”

Steel.

A name to match the man.

He gave Haldric a slight emotionless nod.

The Prime Archon’s voice was commanding again. “You have two choices.”

Haldric’s fingers curled into fists.

“You can begin your adventure as Hunter Steel’s apprentice. Or…” The Prime Archon turned fully toward him, eyes sharp. “You can become mine.”

Haldric barely heard him.

His gaze had locked onto something else entirely.

The surface.

The world stretched before him—wild, untamed, waiting.

A vast sky unfolded, a masterpiece of color unlike anything he had ever seen. Purple and red hues melted together as a giant SolarStone peeked over the horizon, its light refracting through stormy clouds. Swirls of deep blue churned in the distance, lightning crackling within.

And the land—it was nothing like the stories.

Less green than he had expected. Less… alive.

The trees stood hunched, their trunks an unnatural gray, nothing like the brown depicted in books. Thick vines coiled around them like veins, stretching toward the light. The dirt itself seemed to shift ever so slightly, as if it breathed alongside the wind.

It was beautiful. It was dangerous. It was an adventure calling.

But it would have to call for someone else.

He turned away.

“I’m no Hunter,” Haldric muttered. “What’s next, sir?”

The walk back down to the Administration Office was filled with awkward, nerve-racking silence. Why does The Prime look so troubled? Did… he want me to go? 

Haldric was lost in his thoughts. Did I make the wrong choice? It was right there—freedom. So why did it feel so… wrong? 

Like if he had taken it, he would regret it for the rest of his life.

He didn’t even realize when they arrived. The Administrative Office was silent—not the usual silence of routine work or quiet tension, but something else entirely. He snapped out of his thoughts to find every worker in the office staring at him.

Dozens of officials and office workers stood frozen, mid-task, mid-conversation, mid-motion. Waiting. Watching. They must be shocked to see him entering with The Prime.

Haldric kept his posture straight, stepping forward with The Prime Archon beside him. He didn’t speak. Neither did The Prime. The sound of their boots on stone was the only thing filling the space until The Prime finally stopped.

“Go to your desk and grab your things,” he said.

Ah, right. I was fired today, wasn’t I? They couldn’t have an acclimated sorting paper. Haldric’s exciting adventure hadn’t come to an end just yet—but what now? Guard duty? That made the most sense.

He gathered his things and returned to The Prime, waiting for direction.

The Archon’s voice rang out, firm and absolute. “Effective immediately, Haldric is no longer a member of Administration. He is my chosen successor.” The Prime Archon straightened as he raised his voice “The Heir Archon has been selected.”

No one moved. For a moment, the silence held. Then came a sharp slam. It took Haldric a few breaths to realize the sound had come from him. He’d dropped his supplies in shock.

Did The Prime just announce me as… as his Heir?

His mouth hung open. He shut it quickly, steeling himself. The stack of books and booklets remained perfectly upright, undisturbed. He placed one foot on top of it and leaned forward, resting an arm on his thigh in a majestic pose, as if this had all been on purpose.

Hopefully no one noticed his leg shaking uncontrollably.

Then a voice—hushed, uncertain.

“Sir… is it true? What they’re saying about him?”

Haldric’s pulse thrummed as he turned toward the speaker—a senior administrator near the front of the gathered staff. What about me? What did I do? It couldn’t be about what happened yesterday, could it? Only Hunters and soldiers were there, and it’s not like a Hunter would ever want news like that to spread. 

But there was something in the clerk’s eyes. Something in all their eyes.

The Prime Archon took his time. He glanced at Haldric once before turning back to the room, then gave a small, knowing smile.

“Yes. The Heir Archon defeated an armed War Claw—Bresdin, Mythical Beast Hunter—in a duel. Blindfolded and unarmed.”

A breath—

A pause—

Then the room erupted. Officials and clerks alike shot to their feet, cheering and stomping. Applause rang out, hands striking stone and cloth, maybe because someone had finally stood up to the Hunters.

Some saluted immediately, their motions crisp and full of approval, perhaps because the Heir had finally been announced.

A whistle pierced the air from the back of the room.

Haldric barely had time to react before the crowd surged toward him. Hands clapped his back, shook his shoulders, gripped his arms. Someone ruffled his hair—he wasn’t sure who. A younger official grinned wide. “About time one of ours made history.”

History.

That’s what he was now, wasn’t he?

A story. A legend.

And he loved it.

He squared his shoulders, lifted his chin. He was exactly where he was meant to be.

The Prime Archon let them celebrate. He stood off to the side, watching, letting the energy settle before speaking again. His voice cut through the noise—firm, yet pleased.

“Then you should have no doubt why I’ve chosen him.”

The cheers didn’t stop. If anything, they grew louder.

And for the first time in his life, Haldric felt something he never thought he would in this building: belonging.

After shaking hands with everyone, The Prime signaled for the Heir to follow him.



***



When the door to the office shut behind them, it muffled the celebration surprisingly well. For a moment, Haldric let himself enjoy the quiet. The stillness was a welcome contrast to the chaos outside.

The Prime moved behind his desk and paused as his hand hovered over a drawer as if weighing a decision. His shoulders lowered, not with weariness, but with a kind of resolve Haldric had never seen on him in public. He opened a drawer and drew out two objects, setting them on the polished desk with a deliberate care that made Haldric straighten instinctively.

A small leather-bound book, its corners worn soft with handling. And beside it, a key unlike any Haldric had ever seen. It bore stone teeth and a gray wooden handle. Scales were etched into the handle, forming a repeating pattern accompanied by a bolt and a set of fangs.

Haldric’s mouth went dry. “Sir?”

The Prime rested a hand on the book first. “This belonged to the Archon before me. Notes, philosophies, warnings, mistakes. A record not of rulership, but of burden.” His fingers tapped the worn cover. “You will add your own pages when the time comes.”

He slid the book a little closer to Haldric. “Do not take these words as law, Haldric. Let this be a guide. Learn from what came before you. An Archon who ignores the past does not spare his Heir from failure; he condemns him to inherit it.”

Haldric hesitated before reaching out. The leather was warm from The Prime’s hand. He slid his fingers beneath it to lift, but his hand shook, and the book hardly moved. It felt heavier than it should have, as if the weight came from what was written inside it rather than the book itself. He let it settle back onto the desk.

Then The Prime lifted the key.

“And this,” he continued, “is the Master Key.” He turned it once between his fingers, the faint lines within it catching the light of the chamber. “It opens every lock in Stormhaven. Gates, archives, vaults, homes. If anything in this city has ever been denied to you, this key can grant it.”

The weight of it settled into Haldric’s palm when The Prime placed it there. It was colder than it looked. Colder than it should have been. His fingers closed around it on instinct, as if afraid it might fall straight through his hand and crack the floor.

“You will not use it lightly,” The Prime said. “You will carry it always. As proof of your station as well as a reminder of the responsibilities it bears.”

Haldric’s throat tightened. He nodded, though he was no longer sure what he was agreeing to.

The Prime reached into the drawer once more and produced a thin cord. With a simple motion, he threaded it through the hollowed center of the key and stepped closer.

“May I?”

Haldric dipped his head. The cord brushed the back of his neck as The Prime looped it around and tied it off. The key dropped against his chest with a solid, unforgiving thud.

“With this key,” The Prime said, his voice barely above a murmur, “the city can no longer question your judgement. However, if Stormhaven falls, it will fall on the shoulders of its Archon.” The Prime then lifted the book from the desk and held it between them. “With this book, swear that you will always question your own judgement and only act with Stormhaven’s best interest in mind.”

“I… I swear,” Haldric answered, the words leaving him before he realized he’d said them.

The Prime placed the book into his hands. It was still heavy, unbelievably so. He could already feel the spine digging into his palm. Into his grip. Into his life. And yet it was lighter than it had been earlier. Haldric was a little more confident he had the strength to carry this burden.

He slipped the book into his pocket. The spine pressed against his thigh, while the chill of the key brushed his chest as it swayed. Every movement reminding him of their presence.

“Thank you, sir,” he managed.

The Prime searched his face for a moment. As if sensing Haldric’s new resolve, he nodded, then walked off, tracing his hand against the far wall until—

Click.

A deep, rumbling shift of stone filled the space.

Haldric’s breath hitched as a massive section of the wall slid open, revealing something hidden behind it.

A mural.

Not just any mural—this one felt ancient, older than the city itself, and more beautiful than even the surface. The figures were breathtaking, all inlaid with stunning precision. Thousands of tiny gemstones had been set directly into the stone, the colors shimmering beneath the chamber’s light, giving the mural an almost living quality.

It didn’t just tell a story. It breathed one. 

A figure at the center—a woman, radiant and full of light. Her form was distorted, her stomach round like an expectant mother.

Surrounding her were shards of something broken—something powerful.

The Prime Archon stood back, letting Haldric take it in.

Then his voice broke the silence.

“Do you believe in God, Haldric?”

Haldric didn’t look away from the mural. “No. I believe in what I see. And I don’t see Zephyr making our lives any better. No matter how much we pray, we struggle to survive.”

The Prime hummed. “Well spoken. I didn’t either—not until the night I became The Prime and was shown an ancient city abandoned by history… among other things. It was filled with murals like this one.”

He stepped forward, fingers gliding across the mural’s surface as if tracing a memory etched in stone.

“I heard it took years,” he said, voice low with reverence. “But the Zephyrian Acolytes managed to decipher the ancient language and translate the inscriptions. Would you like to hear what this one says, Heir? It is your right.”

Haldric didn’t answer. His gaze was already being pulled—past The Prime, toward a section of the mural he hadn’t noticed before.

He stepped closer.

A single figure stood apart from the others, tall and still, his gaze seeming to meet Haldric’s. There was no mistaking it—those eyes weren’t part of the stone anymore. They were looking straight at him.

“Hal,” the mural seemed to whisper.

That had to be his imagination… right? 

The figure wore a tunic woven from what looked like living roots and hanging moss. His long brown hair draped over his shoulders, and in one hand, he held a staff glowing faintly with amber light. Perched on his shoulder was a small, four-legged beast, draped in—

“Heir?”

The Prime’s voice pulled him back. Only then did Haldric realize how close he’d gotten to the wall.

He stepped back, turning to The Prime, pulse still racing. “Sorry. It just… pulled me in. Who is this guy supposed to be, anyway? Zephyrs?”

“Who, exactly, are you referring to?” The Prime asked.

“The guy right—” Haldric looked back at the mural of the Deity , only to find… it was no longer there. 

Praew would say he’s crazy.

“Nothing. Never mind,” he said, recovering quickly. “Just a trick of the light. Anyway, what does the inscription say?”

The Prime picked up a thick leather-bound book that had been hidden beneath the mural. After flipping through a few pages, he began to read aloud.

And Haldric listened.

“In the first days, when the heavens were young and the stones yet gleamed with the breath of divinity, there was but one who watched over mankind.”

Even translated, the words felt ancient and foreign to Haldric—the cadence of The Prime’s voice steeped in archaic speech, each syllable drifting past him like a language meant for someone else.

“She had no name, for none were needed. She was only known as Mother.

“Boundless was her love, unshaken her mercy. If her children hungered, she filled their tables. If they thirsted, she broke the clouds and wept rain upon the fields. If they shivered in the dark, she bid the sun to rise and hold them in its warmth.

“Yet power begets envy. And envy begets ruin.

“For the other creatures of the world had no Mother, no voice to answer their cries. They saw the gifts mankind had been given, and in their hearts, a question took root.

“Why them? Why not us?

“Their whispers turned to jealousy. Jealousy to anger. And anger to war.

“And so it was that ten great species raised claw and fang, stone and fire, against she who had given mankind life.

“They struck her low. Tore her open.

“Her womb—the cradle of creation—was sundered, its power spilled like blood upon the ground.

“And from its ruin, the great Deformite was shattered. Scattered. Its pieces carried by thieves and traitors alike. 

“For it is known: to wield two Heavenly Deformites is to stand among the True Gods.

“Thus, the great hunt began.

“And among the Hunters, there was one unlike the rest.

“A creature of shadows and silk, silent as breath, with eyes that gleamed in the dark.

“Sly. Greedy. Cunning.

“With quick hands and quicker wit, she slunk through the chaos, her steps softer than whispers. She needed no claws, no weapons—only patience, only guile.

“And in the dead of night, when war had left the world in silence, she stole away two Heavily Deformites, cradling them like baubles.

“And with them, she wove a world of her own.

“But folly is the sister of pride, and in her arrogance, she sought not only to shape a realm—but to fill it.

“To prove herself greater than Mother.

“She sought to create.

“But she could not.

“She had no gift of breath, no power of birth.

“And so, she stole.

“A city. A people.

“She ripped them from their home and cast them into her hollow world, watching as they floundered in the storms of her making.

“She did not guide them. She did not shield them.

“She left them to toil in a land never meant for them, to survive where survival was not promised.

“She was careless. Reckless.

“She abandoned them.

“And it is by her whim, her folly, her failure, that mankind now suffers.

“She was no Mother.

“Only a thief.”

The air was thick with dust and time, the scent of old stone settling deep in his lungs. Haldric stepped forward, once again drawn to the mural. The symbols carved into the rock seemed to hum with something ancient—something weighty.

His eyes traced the etched figures, the script of a long-dead tongue. Sharp lines. Curving strokes. A language he could not read, yet somehow felt.

His fingers hovered just over the stone, not quite touching, but close. Close enough to sense the weight of history pressing against his skin.

Behind him, The Prime Archon’s voice was steady. “That was but one fragment of a truth long buried… the true history of our people.”

Haldric nodded slightly. He had heard the story.

The Mother, the ten who tore her open. The thief who stole two Heavenly Deformites and wove a world of her own.

His gaze moved to the etched figures, lingering on the thief—the one who had cast them into this world.

The figure’s eyes had been carved with exacting precision, slanted just slightly, giving the illusion of something clever and unbothered.

“Do you have any questions from what I have read so far?”

The room held its silence. The weight of The Prime’s words settled into the stone, into the air, into Haldric’s skin.

Then—

“Hah. Well, that’s quite the dramatic retelling.”

Haldric flinched. The words … didn’t come from The Prime. The voice was too melodic and feminine.

“What’s next?” she continued. “Are you going to accuse her of devouring the sun every night as well?”

When had someone snuck in? Where were they?

His breath caught as he scanned the room.

There!

Leaning against the far wall, arms crossed, weight settled lazily into her hip—like she had always been there.

His mind reeled. Who—?

His gut twisted. How—?

She didn’t belong. That much was obvious.

Not in the way a stranger shouldn’t be here, but in the way she looked too clean.

The underground left its mark on everyone. The damp air clung to skin. Dust settled in hair, on clothes. Even The Prime Archon, meticulous as he was, bore the faint signs of someone who lived in stone corridors.

But her?

Nothing.

No dust on her sleeves. No dampness weighing down her hair. Not a smudge of grit on her skin.

Her hair was pale—not faded, not silver, but the kind of white that never touched grime. It should have looked dull in the dim torchlight, but somehow, it didn’t. Somehow, it caught the flickering glow just enough to seem like mist curling at the edges.

Her skin was smooth, too smooth, untouched by the roughness of this world. Like polished stone shaped by wind and time, yet never broken.

Her eyes—sharp, too sharp. There was an intelligence behind them, something calculating. Like she wasn’t just looking at him, but through him.

Even the way she stood was wrong.

Relaxed, unconcerned. Like the walls around her weren’t closing in with silence. Like she had all the time in the world.

Then, as if sensing his stare, she smirked.

She flicked her gaze toward The Prime, the corner of her lips twitching in an almost-smile.

“Tell me, did you stretch the truth on purpose, or is this just the best your little translators could come up with?”

Haldric’s eyes snapped to The Prime Archon, expecting a reaction.

But The Prime didn’t move.

Didn’t flinch. Didn’t shift, he just stared at Haldric expectantly.

He didn’t so much as acknowledge her.

Like she wasn’t there.

Haldric swallowed, throat suddenly dry.

He turned back to the woman. “Who—”

She cut him off with a flick of her fingers.

“No, no, let me guess.” She tilted her head, her expression caught somewhere between amusement and boredom. “You’re going to ask who I am, how I got in here, what I want. Blah, blah. But really, aren’t those such boring questions, Hal?”

Haldric clenched his jaw.

Everything about her was wrong but not in a way he could name.

She let out a short laugh, shaking her head.

“I’ll admit,” she mused, flicking a glance back at the mural, “I didn’t think anyone was still telling this story.” She smirked. “But since we’re all sharing stories today…” She gestured lazily toward the mural. “Tell me, child. Do you believe the story?”

Haldric tried to swallow, but his mouth was too dry. Did he believe it? He wanted to, but did he, really? When he finally found the courage, his voice came quieter than expected. “I… I don’t know.”

The woman nodded. “Looks like there’s hope for you little rodents yet.” 

Then, without another word, she strode toward The Prime’s door and—

…she—

Did she just walk through it? Gone.

No sound of footsteps. No lingering presence. The door hadn’t even moved. Haldric stared. The air felt different, wrong in a way he couldn’t place.

The Prime Archon sighed audibly. “I think this will be a good stopping point for the day.”

Haldric blinked, barely processing the words. A stopping point? That’s it?

Did The Prime really see nothing? Could he truly not hear her, even as she openly scorned him? What… what is happening to me? First that Nature Deity vanished from the wall, and now this?

The Prime turned away, but the sigh he left behind clung to Haldric’s thoughts like dust in the air.

Did he seem… disappointed?

The thought sat uneasily in Haldric’s chest. 

What was the last thing The Prime said before she appeared? He asked if I had any questions about what he’d read… right? And if he couldn’t see or hear the girl, then my reply was…

“Who—” …and later… “I don’t know.”

Stone me, he thinks I wasn’t paying attention at all!

Before Haldric could reassure The Prime, before he could even try to seem more engaged, the man spoke first.

“You’ll need time to digest what you’ve heard.”  Then with a sharper tone, The Prime continued “This goes without saying, but this isn’t information you can share. No friends. No family.”

His gaze settled on Haldric. “This must stay between us.”

Haldric nodded automatically. “I understand.”

The Prime turned away. Taking it as a dismissal, Haldric stepped out of the office, and the door clicked shut behind him.

And only then, alone in the quiet, did the weight of it all finally settle.

Haldric barely remembered the walk back to his home. His mind churned with everything: The Prime’s words, the mural, the woman who shouldn’t exist.

His Grayparents were waiting. Worry lined their faces, but before they could ask, he cut them off with a tired smile.

“I’m alright.”

They didn’t seem convinced, but they let it be. Wintrid muttered something going to bed, while Cenrick gave his shoulder a squeeze—wordless, but firm. 

Haldric exhaled, stepping into his room.

He was ready to collapse, sleep early for once. The last few days had been… too much.

Then, the knock.

Haldric groaned. He didn’t even need to guess.

When he opened the door, Praew grinned up at him.

“I’m honored!” she gasped dramatically, clutching her chest like she’d been blessed by a deity . “I get to spend time with such a famous person!”

Haldric pinched the bridge of his nose. “Alright, alright, that’s enough.”

But she wasn’t done. “You’re all anyone’s talking about! An unarmed administrator from The Prime’s office took on a Mythical Beast Hunter while blindfolded. In a sword fight. And won.” She laughed, shaking her head. “I do want to know how exactly you cut off his hand without a weapon, though.”

She raised a brow. “What did you do? Skip our meeting last night to run around spreading false rumors about yourself?”

Haldric sighed. The truth was… worse.

With the Grayparents in bed, Haldric and Praew sat at the living room table as he recounted the last two days. Praew listened, and the more he spoke, the wider her eyes got.

By the time he finished, she was staring. Jaw slack.

Then—

“ARE YOU INSANE?!”

Haldric winced. “Praew—”

“You did WHAT?!”

She looked ready to shake him, like somehow that would make the last two days sound less ridiculous.

He’d kept certain details to himself—the blinding sun, the mural, that woman. No one acclimated was allowed to talk about the first light. No one spoke about the strength they gained.

And the mural? That woman? There was no point.

Praew exhaled, rubbing her temple. “Wow.”

Then, more slowly, “So… you’re really going to be the next Prime Archon?”

She paused, then grinned. “This is good! This is really good!” She smacked his arm. “Now we can finally see what The Prime’s really up to!”

Haldric began tapping the table. “He’s not the enemy.”

She scoffed. “You don’t know that. He’s too suspicious—”

The Prime had saved him. Had believed in him. Had given him everything he lost.

“HE IS NOT AN ENEMY, PRAEW!”

The words burst out of him, louder, harsher than intended.

Startled, Praew jumped out of her seat into a defensive stance Haldric didn’t recognize.

Wow. Someone’s jumpy, he thought—until he looked down. The side of his fist was still pressed to the stone table—or what was left of it. The surface had caved under his touch, shattered into jagged pieces.

Praew took a half-step back. “Whoa. Okay. Relax.” She eyed the broken stone, then him. “How’d you even do that?”

Haldric swallowed. His pulse pounded. Ah, right. I’m a bolting idiot. Now that I’ve acclimated, I’ve gotta get better at controlling my strength. “It was already cracked.”

Too quick. Too defensive.

Praew raised a brow. She didn’t believe that, but she let it go. For now. After a beat, Praew spoke again. “That brings me to something else.”

Haldric frowned. “What now?”

She leaned in. “I made contact with someone. A group of people, actually. Worried citizens who think something’s wrong with the city.”

Haldric rubbed his temple.

“And their leader?” she continued. “His name is Note.”

Haldric blinked, then frowned. “Note?” He crossed his arms. “That’s the most suspicious name I’ve ever heard.”

“I know,” she admitted. “But they’re the only people actually talking about this.”

Haldric’s fingers curled against his leg. Something about this didn’t sit right. “You’re seriously trusting a guy who calls himself ‘Note’?” His voice was flat.

Praew shrugged. “It’s just a name.”

“It’s a fake name,” Haldric countered. “Which means he’s hiding something.”

She rolled her eyes. “Or it just means he doesn’t want enforcers tracking him down for asking questions.”

Haldric wasn’t buying it. “And what exactly is he asking questions about?”

Praew’s smirk faltered slightly. “The missing food. The city’s leadership. The people who’ve been disappearing.”

Haldric’s jaw tightened. “Sounds a lot like someone looking for trouble.”

She scoffed. “Or someone who actually gives a stone about this city.”

He couldn’t argue that, but it didn’t make him feel any better. “Tell me you didn’t agree to anything.”

Praew hesitated. “I… agreed to meet with them tomorrow night.”

Haldric groaned, dragging a hand down his face. “Of course you did.”

She frowned, arms crossing. “And what am I supposed to do, Haldric? Just forget about my missing friends? Pretend like I…” she hesitated, voice shaking, “like I didn’t fail them?”

Why didn’t she understand? He felt bad for her, sure, but people who asked too many questions didn’t last long in Stormhaven. Either they disappeared… or they were baiting someone else to.

Stone me. Haldric sighed, pushing past his frustration. “Okay I’m sorry, but I need to meet this guy first. Before you get too involved, before you start making promises and put yourself at risk. Understood?”

She sighed but nodded. “Alright, fine. You can meet him.”

Haldric wasn’t convinced. No matter what Praew thought, he’d be watching this ‘Note’ guy carefully.

Because even the best Hunters forget—sometimes, the real predator isn’t the one leading the prey.

It’s the one prowling just behind.

Waiting to strike.
