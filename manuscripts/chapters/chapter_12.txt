Chapter 12 - Trigger Identification



"Some may call my death an unnecessary sacrifice—"



The slums of the Third Floor outer ring were always damp, always dim, and always humming with an undercurrent of quiet desperation. The SolarStones overhead flickered, their uneven glow fading in and out of darkness. Every inch of space was crammed with makeshift stalls, piles of salvaged goods, and the hunched figures of unauthorized traders who knew better than to call attention to themselves.

Where was she? Haldric scanned the depressing street. This was the only part of the city where a black cloak that hid your face wouldn’t draw suspicion. People would just assume it was a trick of the dim light.

It’s not like Praew to be late.

They’d agreed to meet right here at the seventeenth flash—just one before curfew. As the Heir Archon, Haldric was curfew-exempt, but this Note person was way too suspicious. Meeting him past curfew would only draw the attention of the curfew patrol, even with clearance. But meeting too early could attract even more eyes. Someone was bound to recognize the second-most powerful person in the city.

Most traders and beggars were packing up, but a few still lingered.

  Haldric’s nose curled at the sour scent of sweat and unwashed bodies that wafted toward him.

  And waiting for them in this filth was Note.

  Haldric spotted him immediately. It wasn’t hard.

  Never in his life had he wanted to punch someone more. Not Renwick after what he said to Praew. Not even Asher after his betrayal.

  What annoyed him—no, what infuriated him—was how absurd this guy looked.

  Does he even know what discreet means? Even The Prime would stand out less than this freak.

  Note looked like he belonged in the statue garden on the First Floor. He stood with effortless stillness, arms crossed over a chest so broad it made Haldric’s own shoulders feel inadequate. His dark skin was streaked with soot, but his posture remained perfect, his presence unwavering. A shadow of a perfectly trimmed beard framed his sharp jawline, and those piercing light blue eyes burned with a confidence that made Haldric’s stomach twist.

     And then there was the muscle.

 Veins coiled over his arms like tree roots, his biceps bulging beneath a torn, sweat-streaked shirt. His legs were massive—grotesquely overgrown. It was comical, really.

 No one actually liked that much muscle.

 Right?

 Haldric exhaled sharply through his nose.

 And then he saw it.

 Just the tiniest flicker of movement beside him.

 He flinched as Praew stepped up, eyes scanning the street.

               Stone me, how did she—? I didn’t hear her at all!

 When Note approached, Praew’s mouth dropped open.

  A yawn. That’s all. Just a yawn. Not admiration. Not attraction.

  Just exhaustion.

  Right?

  Right?!

  Haldric squared his shoulders, forcing his expression into something neutral. This fraud was going down.

  “So, you’re Note.” His tone was as flat as the damp stone beneath their feet.

       The man smirked. Like he had already won something. “And you’re The Prime’s new pet.”

Haldric stiffened. His fingers twitched at his sides. You win this round… fraud.

Praew, oblivious or choosing to ignore the tension, stepped forward. “You’re the one asking questions about the city.”

Note’s smirk remained, but his gaze turned serious. “I ask nothing but for people to question more.”

Haldric scoffed. “Oh, well. Thank the Stones, then. A poet is here to save us all.”

Note ignored him. His gaze stayed on Praew. “You’ve lived on the Second Floor, sheltered from the third your whole life, haven’t you?”

“Yes,” she lied. She always kept that part of her life quiet. Haldric only knew because their families were close. She wasn’t about to tell some grotesque stranger, no matter how much his muscles rippled.

Note nodded, like he had already expected the answer. His eyes flicked toward Haldric. “And you?”

Haldric stifled a grin, pleased she was thinking cautiously. “Same.”

Note sized him up and down, then glanced away. “I doubt that.” He exhaled, shaking his head. “No matter, neither of you know how this city really works.”

Haldric bristled. “I know enough.”

     “I doubt that as well.” Note’s voice cut through the noise of the slums, low and steady. Not mocking but certain.

     And somehow, that was worse.

    Something in the weight of those words made Haldric’s skin crawl.

    For the first time, he wasn’t sure if he wanted to argue.

    Note’s piercing blue eyes swept over the market, then flicked toward a narrow alley. “Not here,” he said. “Too many ears.”

   Praew hesitated only a moment before following. Haldric sighed, dragging a hand down his face before trailing after her. This was already a waste of time.

The alley twisted into a hidden passage between two crumbling buildings, the stone walls damp with condensation. The deeper they went, the quieter it became. Finally, Note ducked through a doorway barely visible in the dim light.

Inside, the space was small but reinforced—an old storage room, maybe. Stacked crates lined the walls, and a table sat at the center, surrounded by mismatched chairs. A SolarStone embedded in the ceiling cast an uneven glow over the dust-coated floor.

Note gestured toward the seats. “Sit.”

Haldric didn’t move. Praew, ever the more trusting one, sat down first, watching Note carefully.

Note pulled a chair for himself, the wood groaning under his weight. He folded his arms, leaning forward slightly.

“Tell me,” he said, “what do you two know about government administration?”

Haldric narrowed his eyes. “I used to work in it.”

Note nodded. “And what have you learned?”

Haldric exhaled through his nose. “That everything is categorized, controlled, and precise. That every citizen has a role to play, and that’s what keeps the city running.”

Note’s lips twitched. “A good answer. The kind of answer they drill into your head. But let’s go a little deeper.” He leaned back, exhaling sharply. “Let’s talk about the sterilization program.”

Haldric frowned. “It’s voluntary.”

Note snorted. “Sure. Every man just happens to volunteer for sterilization after having two kids.”

His gaze flicked toward Praew. “Tell me, have you ever met a single man who refused it?”

She hesitated. “I… I don’t think so.”

Haldric’s brow furrowed. His mind raced through every conversation, every offhand remark from co-workers, from neighbors. But, no, he couldn’t recall a single one.

Note watched their reactions carefully. “Strange, isn’t it? A choice that no one has ever made?”

Haldric clenched his jaw. “Maybe they all just agreed.”

Note smirked. “Sure. And while we’re at it, let’s believe every single worker in the city just happens to love their assigned job.”

Praew straightened. “What are you saying?”

“I’m saying it’s not a choice,” Note said confidently.

Haldric’s arms crossed. “They don’t force it. No one is held down against their will.”

 “They don’t have to,” Note said. “The system does it for them.” His voice lowered. “Now, tell me something else. Have either of you ever heard of a family with more than two children?”

Silence.

Praew’s fingers curled against the edge of the table.

Haldric opened his mouth. Stopped. Because the answer was obvious. No, they had never heard of a third child. Never seen one. Never questioned why.

Note’s lips pressed into a thin line. “If it were truly a choice, wouldn’t someone have chosen differently?”

The words sat heavy in the air.

Praew swallowed. “What happens if… if someone has a third child?”

Note’s gaze darkened. “The third child receives no food ration.”

Praew inhaled sharply.

Haldric stiffened. “No way,” he muttered.

Note’s voice remained steady. “Both parents lose their job assignments. The family is reassigned roles.”

Praew’s breath hitched.

“And the worst part?” Note’s expression was unreadable. “The entire family is relocated to the Fourth Floor.”

Haldric let out a sharp laugh. “The Fourth Floor? That’s a myth. Everyone knows there are only three Floors in our city.”

Note didn’t argue. He didn’t push back. Instead, he stood up, walking slowly to the far side of the room. With deliberate slowness, he crouched down and rolled up a ragged old carpet. 

Beneath it—A trapdoor.

The wood was old but sturdy, a metal handle embedded in its surface. A chill ran down Haldric’s spine.

Praew was already moving. “What’s down there?” she whispered.

Note gestured. “See for yourself.”

She took a step forward, but Haldric’s hand shot out, gripping her wrist. “Praew, wait.”

Her voice was quiet but resolute. “I have to know, Haldric.” She pulled free.

Her fingers wrapped around the handle—and she tugged on it.



***



       The hatch creaked open beneath Praew’s hands, a thick breath of stale air rising from the depths below. The woodenl rung of a ladder was damp as she swung over the edge, her grip tightening. She had no idea how deep this went.

      Step by step, the world above shrank into a distant glow, then vanished entirely.

  Praew heard Haldric curse under his breath as he began his climb down. Somewhere farther up the ladder, Note chuckled and muttered, “Coward.”

  She rolled her eyes. Note may have had a not-so-terrible look, but she couldn’t feel any attraction to boys like that. The kind who couldn’t go five flickers without comparing the size of their Sifaralith Stones.

  The air grew staler the further she descended. The silence began to stretch. There was no telling how much time passed before her boots met solid ground.

       And when they did—she froze.

  Haldric and Note dropped down beside her a moment later.

  What lay before them wasn’t a city. It was something much worse. The cavern stretched outward, a gaping wound in the earth. Buildings slumped together like bones stripped of flesh, their walls brittle and crumbling. This place was cold—not just in temperature, but in something deeper. Something stripped and abandoned. Yet, people moved through it. Shadow-thin figures, barely more than silhouettes against the gloom. No conversations. No haggling. No life.

     And the only light? Ratrods.

     The rodents varied in size as they scurried along the broken streets, each with a single horn embedded with a SolarStone, pulsing with dim, dying light. Their trigger was a ZephyrStone, meaning each breath they took activated the glow. But the moment they sensed danger, they held their breath and darted away, their horns fading into darkness. The sight alone made her skin crawl.

A shiver ran down her spine. She opened her mouth—to ask, to demand an explanation—but something stopped her. Something crouched in the dirt, barely visible in the dim glow. A child. Praew blinked.

They couldn’t be older than six. Maybe younger. Their tangled hair clung in thick, matted knots, their rags stiff with filth. They were so thin, their limbs barely more than bones wrapped in skin.

Then she saw what they were doing and her stomach turned. A freshly killed Ratrod was still twitching as the child sank their teeth into it.

It was massive, easily the size of a newborn, its thick body nearly the size of the child’s own torso. 

Praew stopped breathing.

The child showed no signs of hesitation or disgust. Just raw hunger. Blood smeared across their chin as they ripped into the flesh, teeth tearing, throat swallowing. The noise—the wet crunch, the low growl—sent something sharp down her spine. And then the child’s head snapped up.

Praew staggered back.

Those eyes. Wild, feral, red eyes. The kid tensed, hunching lower over their kill. A thin arm curled around the carcass, as if Praew was about to steal it from them.

A low sound rattled in their throat. A growl.

She couldn’t move. She couldn’t speak. Her voice barely came out. “Where are your parents?”

The child didn’t answer. Didn’t blink.

Note scoffed. “Probably dead.”

The words were too flat. Praew turned sharply, staring at him. “You say that like it doesn’t matter.”



Note shrugged, unbothered. “It doesn’t.”

Something inside her twisted. How could he be so—The child was still watching her.

Still baring their teeth. Still guarding the only food they had. Praew felt sick. She’d thought she understood Stormhaven. Thought she had seen suffering. 

But this? She hadn’t known anything at all. And the worst part? Haldric hadn’t said a word yet. 

She turned toward him.

His fists were curled tight. His jaw locked. His eyes stayed on the child, on the feral look in their eyes.

He was absorbing it, wasn’t he?

The air in the Fourth Floor felt different —stifling, almost suffocating. It wasn’t just the humidity or the scent of damp stone. It was something heavier. Something she could feel pressing down on her ribs, sinking into her skin.

Praew followed closely as Note led them deeper into the hidden passageways, her ears straining for any movement. Haldric was right behind her, his steps just as careful. 

Once they turned a corner, Note finally spoke. “Did you know other cities let their citizens choose their own career paths?”

  Praew blinked. “Which… other cities?”

  “All of them. Is it that surprising?” Note shrugged, as if it were the most normal thing in the world. “If they’re good at their jobs, they get promoted. If they struggle, they’re reassigned or trained for something else. And if they hate their job, they quit and try something new.”

  Praew stared. That was… ridiculous.

  Haldric’s voice was quiet but firm. “That sounds like a mess.”

  Note triggered a weak SolarStone so they could see, then tilted his head. “Does it?” He leaned back slightly. “Because from what I’ve seen, it works quite well.”

  Praew frowned. It was hard to believe. But before she could argue, Note continued.

  “But here, in Stormhaven?” He gestured around them. “To be fired is the same as breaking the law.”

  Praew felt her stomach tighten.

  Haldric glanced toward him. “Fine. You obviously want me to ask, so what happens when someone loses their job?”

  Note let out a slow breath. “If they’re lucky, they’re demoted. If not? They’re sent here. To the Fourth Floor.”

  Haldric didn’t bother hiding his suspicion. “And how exactly do you know all of this?”

  Note’s bravado vanished. “As you’ve probably guessed, I was a Hunter. That’s how I’ve visited another city. I confronted The Prime about the fake food shortage. Since I had no family for him to hold hostage, he had no way to control me. Before I knew it, I was discharged and sent down here.”

     Haldric scoffed. “You expect me to believe that?”

  “No,” Note said. “Not yet, I don’t. But you will, Haldric. Maybe once you see for yourself that there are only two career paths on the Fourth Floor.”

  He lowered his voice. “You either become a beast… or livestock, preyed on by something even worse.”

  He gestured toward the dark. “That child you saw? That was the first path. The second is slaving away as a Sifaralith trigger identifier.”

  Haldric frowned. “Livestock? Sifaralith trigger identifier?”

  Note nodded. “Someone has to figure out what makes a stone activate. And the only way to do that… is to test it.”

  “Think of it this way,” Note said. “Junya’s parents were miners on the Third Floor, right? Lived in the outer ring, probably knew nothing about the Fourth. That’s how it works for most.”

  He glanced back at them. “But some people get reassigned or relocated to work in the mines. Those people are never seen again, because they’re sent here.”

  The words landed like a punch in her gut.

  She turned sharply toward Haldric. “Wait—our classmate Korven! He was relocated for mining, wasn’t he?”

  When the flicker of realization hit, Haldric looked like he was about to be sick. They had all assumed Korven had been… relocated. Moved somewhere for labor. But they had never thought to ask where.

  Her hands curled into fists. How many others had vanished? How many names had they stopped saying because they believed they’d just been ‘reassigned’?

  Note gestured for them to keep moving. Praew swallowed hard and pressed forward, following a long corridor that narrowed as it stretched on. As they neared a bend, a faint light began to rise in the distance.

  Note stopped abruptly, crouching behind the remnants of a stone pillar broken off at a height that nearly matched Praew’s own. She and Haldric dropped beside him. The rest of the pillar lay toppled along the ground, its length reaching toward the mouth of the enormous chamber ahead.

  A massive underground pit opened before them, the cavern stretching farther than any part of Stormhaven she had ever seen. It was the only place on this Floor where they’d found actual SolarStones, casting their glow across the stone and lighting the space in full.

     And the people—the hundreds of workers, if you could even call them that -- were treated like absolute waste. Lashings if they made a mistake. Lashings if they worked too recklessly. Lashings just for existing too long. Praew’s breath caught. Wasn’t slavery banned over a hundred years ago?

     Men, women, and children moved in slow, mechanical patterns. Some were hunched over stone tables, testing Sifaraliths with trembling hands. Others were dragging carts filled with jagged rocks, their bodies thin, their faces hollow with exhaustion.

     Praew noticed an enforcer grin as they whipped an old man, and her blood turned cold. They were enjoying it—every moment of hurting these poor people.

 They were always watching. Waiting for the next one to make a mistake or hesitate. Like a beast stalking prey.

 Then—a shockwave of sound. A crack, a blast. Praew flinched.

     One of the workers, a young man around the same age as Praew, staggered back from a table, clutching his hand. His fingers were gone. Blood spattered the stone.

  The people around him barely reacted. An enforcer stepped forward, calm and expressionless, and grabbed the man by the collar. He didn’t even resist as he was dragged away.

  Praew’s breath caught. That face. I know that face!

  “Zephyrs take me,” Haldric cursed.

  “I know him,” Praew whispered.

  “Stone me! Praew, don’t,” Haldric hissed.

  It was Jorpen. Her classmate. The one with the strongest moral compass. The one who disappeared after speaking out in Shadow Recon. They said he had dropped out. That he’d requested reassignment rather than commit treason.

  But he wasn’t the one bleeding.

  He was the one dragging the injured man away.

  Hot tears slid down her cheek. There would be no rescue today.

  There was something on the back of Jorpen’s neck. A burn or a band. Maybe he was little more than a slave himself.

  Mai had said something when failing Praew from her class. That Praew had to pass both her other classes, otherwise she could be—

  Zephyrs take me, she thought. Didn’t Mai say I could be “reassigned to the mines or something much worse”?

  At the time, it sounded like a threat. But she was just trying to warn me, wasn’t she? No wonder she insisted on training me.

  Praew was starting to realize just how little she really knew. But one thing was certain, Mai Azadi knew about this place. And when Praew got back, she was going to find out exactly how much.

     “This is Sifaralith trigger identification.” Note’s voice was steady, but Praew could hear the simmering anger beneath it. “The lowest career in Stormhaven. The fate of all who don’t fit The Prime’s ideal for a perfect city.”

     Haldric tensed beside her. “I… I can’t believe this.” His fists clenched at his sides. His voice wavered, like he was trying to convince himself as much as them. “The Prime Archon treats me like his own son. He’s like the father I never had… He cares. He cares about this city, about everyone.” His breath hitched. His fingers curled tighter. “I . . . he wouldn’t allow this. He wouldn’t.”

     Praew turned toward Haldric, watching.

     This would be even harder for him. She had already been introduced to the darker side of Stormhaven over the past few weeks. This was atrocious—yes—but she had begun mentally preparing long for something like this before arranging the meeting with Note.

  Haldric, on the other hand, was only just beginning to see it. The image of his new father figure was starting to fall apart.

     His gaze flickered over the brutal scene before him. The lashes. The lifeless stares. The silence of the forgotten. A hollow pit formed in his chest. She could see it. “The Prime,” Haldric continued, "probably doesn’t even know this is happening.” His voice cracked. Just a little.

Praew swallowed, the words weighing in her throat.

His eyes betrayed his own words. Moisture lined his lashes, catching in the flickering glow of the SolarStones. He hadn’t wiped it away. Maybe he hadn’t noticed. Or maybe he refused to. Instead, he pulled a small book out of his pocket.

He gripped the book tightly, as though someone would confiscate it.

She didn’t say anything. Consoling him would have to wait. They had bigger problems now. They would likely be attacked at their next destination.

Note exhaled through his nose. “I can see you’re not ready to accept the truth.” He turned away, his broad back cutting through the damp air like a wall. “We’re leaving.”

Praew turned to follow Note when a shadow moved in the corner of her eye. She looked over to see Haldric sneaking up to a pile of Sifaralith stones, grabbing a ledger from a table nearby. 

 He examined it, looked at one pile, then back at the ledger—then looked again at a different pile. And just like that, he was off. He grabbed a stone from two separate piles and returned before Note even noticed he was gone.

“Are you crazy!?” Praew hissed at him.

He glanced in her direction, then looked away.

They followed Note until they entered another lifeless chamber. The ceiling vanished into shadow, swallowed by a height too vast for light to reach.

 The shimmering debris littering the ground began to dance as their only source of illumination scattered and scurried. Praew never thought she’d miss the company of Ratrods, yet Mai Azadi’s training screamed in her head.

     She was too exposed and unfamiliar with the terrain in a way Note clearly wasn’t. He moved like someone accustomed to the dark, more so than either Haldric or herself. An ambush wouldn’t be difficult here.

     So this is it? It was likely. Haldric could be so dense sometimes. Since they failed to see eye to eye, anyone with a brain would know to eliminate the Heir before he runs off to The Prime.

     Couldn’t you just pretend to condemn The Prime?

     Tense, alert, Praew flinched when Note finally spoke.

     “I believe I know what happened to Junya’s parents.”

     Praew felt a flicker of hope. He’s giving me a clue? Perhaps I won’t have to kill him after all!

 An unsettling realization struck. Where did that come from? Since when had the idea of killing come so effortlessly? Zephyrs take me, Mai is rubbing off on me.

     Praew abandoned her stance, letting down her guard. “What did you find?” 

     “According to what Junya told me,” Note said, “their last assignment would’ve placed them right—here, one Floor up.” He pointed up while keeping his eyes locked with Praew.

     Praew and Haldric looked up to find a giant gaping hole in the ceiling. With how crude and misshapen it was, the hole didn’t look intentional.

     Praew gasped. If Junya’s parents fell… if Junya and Wannii fell, they wouldn’t be walking away uninjured. Praew started to search the debris desperately.

     “There’s no bodies. But there’s plenty of blood. And gore. You don’t want to see what the Ratrods were eating.”

     Note’s voice was steady, but his eyes kept flicking toward the dark edges of the chamber.

     “I used to be a tracker, Praew. A location will always tell the story behind a death.”

     He stepped carefully over a slab of fractured stone and motioned to the collapsed pile behind them.

     “Two bodies fell. Both died on impact. Not unusual for a pitfall—except for where they landed. Perfectly on top of all this rubble.”

     He waited a beat before continuing.

      “When a floor gives out, most people get buried. Pinned, crushed, maybe even thrown to the lower ground. Best case, you bounce off the rubble and land beside it. But to hit and stay right on top?” He looked at her. “That only happens if you fall after the collapse.”

     “Because of this, I have reason to believe Junya’s parents were murdered for discovering the existence of the Fourth Floor.”

     “I’ve heard no word of Wannii or Junya down here. Vanishing without an arrest or deceased report is concerning. Either they got caught investigating, or someone caught wind of what they were up to. Does anyone come to mind?”

     Godric and Mai both seemed to know something… but Mai wouldn’t do this.

     “No,” Praew said.

     “I understand. Then this is where I stop my investigation,” Note continued. “My condolences. But they were likely taken out. Professionally. I believe it’s best if you stop looking into this, or you may disappear next.”

     Haldric snorted. “We finally agree on something.”

     Note, never one to let a quip go, shot back, “What’s the matter, Haldric? You’ve been so quiet, I forgot you were even here.”

     Haldric didn’t bother replying.

     The climb back up felt longer than the descent. Each rung of the ladder, each damp, suffocating passage, weighed heavier than the last.



     Praew’s mind churned. She wasn’t stupid. She’d known their city had its problems, but this? People worked to survive, sure. But enslaved? Brutalized? Dying for the city’s convenience?

     The images wouldn’t leave her. The lash marks on those who had worked too slow. The empty, resigned eyes of children who hadn’t been children in a long time. She forced herself to focus on the rungs beneath her hands. On breathing. On not looking at Haldric, because she knew what he was thinking. She could feel it. The heat of his frustration, his doubt, his denial.
