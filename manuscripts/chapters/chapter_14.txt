Chapter 14 - Answers



“Today, I saw the future.”



     Haldric followed, each step slow, uncertain. The office door groaned shut behind them, sealing away the confrontation like a weight pressing down on his chest.

     The Prime walked with purpose, a balance between hurried and idle. Every step measured, deliberate. The kind of stride belonging to a Hunter or a ruler.

     They walked to the now-open ration station near the administrative building.

     “If everything you said is true, have you not wondered why your rations hardly improved after becoming the Heir?”

     Haldric received his breakfast first. A bland grain bar. He moved to sit at one of the tables, expecting The Prime to follow suit. Haldric was surprised to see The Prime continuing to walk, ration bar in hand. Haldric’s eyes widened slightly. He hadn’t expected such simplicity from a leader of the city. 

     “No time for rest, Haldric,” The Prime said. “We need to keep moving.”

     Where was his fancy breakfast?

     The Prime took a bite of his grain bar. He walked with purpose, neither hurried nor idle. Every step was measured and deliberate, until the corridor opened into a winding, empty road.

     The Prime’s demeanor shifted. His shoulders slouched. His strides grew heavier, less smooth. The exhaustion on his face was impossible to miss.

     This was not the walk of a man who ruled Stormhaven.

     It was the walk of someone who carried it.

     The Prime glanced at Haldric.

     “Have you ever wondered why your ration slips didn’t change when you became the Heir Archon?”

     Haldric blinked, taken aback by the unexpected question. He glanced at the simple meal in his hand, identical to the one he’d always known.

     “I… I guess I never thought about it.”

     The Prime smiled, continuing down the winding road that led them deeper into the upper district.

     “A leader never asks of his people what he is not willing to do himself.”

     Haldric fell into step beside The Prime as the words settled over him.

     Isn’t this the way to the noble quarter? His thoughts began to spiral. He had expected anger or cold fury. Instead, he was met with this… rationality, a challenge to see things through The Prime’s eyes.

     What’s your angle? To manipulate me?

     Or was it proof that the man was truly worth believing in?

     They moved through the winding halls of roads, past guards who straightened at the sight of The Prime Archon. No words were exchanged. No questions asked. They all knew better.

     After the sixth flash, they reached their destination, and what Haldric saw made him stop.

     The Noble Quarter had always been described in whispers. Rumors spoke in abundance of soft beds and wide streets, and of citizens untouched by hunger. At least, according to some workers who had once been assigned a task inside. That’s the only time people were allowed to enter.

     But what is this? There was no feast laid out before him. No indulgence. Just order. Meticulous, suffocating order.

     The stone paths were swept to perfection. Every home looked identical, clean and polished, as if waiting for an inspection that never came. SolarStones lined the roads in symmetrical arcs, glowing brighter here than anywhere else in the city. There were no stalls. No loiterers. No signs of imprisonment or poverty. But none of that unsettled Haldric as much as the people.

     They weren’t what he expected. These weren’t bloated bureaucrats lounging in luxury. The adults were thin, tired, hungry, and… happy? They looked toward the children with a fondness in their eyes that seemed to heal the exhaustion.

     The children they looked after were strong and healthy. Full of life, they laughed and played with an energy that stood in stark contrast to the adults. They brandished makeshift swords, pretending to be Hunters, their voices ringing out with youthful determination.

     Haldric’s brows pulled together as the contradiction set in. His voice came out low.

     “Where’s the supposed luxury?”

     The Prime didn’t even slow.

     “This is your so-called upper class.

     “Have you ever wondered why I bring the families of Hunters here?” he asked, glancing at Haldric.

     Haldric hesitated.

     “I—I’m not sure,” he said, remembering Note’s accusations.

     “Your face tells a different story,” The Prime chuckled softly. “I know what the rumors say. That I hold them hostage, use them to control Hunters.”

     Haldric looked away, a flush of embarrassment creeping up his neck.

     The Prime chuckled faintly.

     “I don’t blame you. It’s not your fault.” A distant look grew on his face as he sneered.

     “Lies spread like dye. It seeps into the truth, twisting the perception of your true colors. Though it may fade with time, it’s certain to leave a permanent mark on your reputation.”

     It was difficult to follow The Prime’s words sometimes. But this time, Haldric felt he sort of understood the meaning.

     They turned into a final corridor.

     Children raced between the benches. Although chaotic, their games followed a kind of structure, watched over by a few older ones who sat nearby, giggling between whispers.

     They didn’t look like hostages.

     “The best hostages don’t even know they are one.” Note’s words rang in his head.

     “They’re not hostages,” The Prime assured. His watchful eyes never left Haldric. “The men and women above ground risk their lives to keep the city alive. Their families remain here, cared for. Their children, whether orphaned or not, are raised in safety and given the best education we can provide, which ensures higher-quality job assignments.” The Prime sighed. “Though I’m ashamed to admit it, none of this will fill the void in their hearts, but it is regretfully the best I could do.”

     Haldric scanned the rows of matching homes again, his mind catching on the details. The emptiness. The quiet grief beneath the stillness.

     “Hunters die, Haldric. More often than I wish to admit. A surface storm takes them. A beast strike. Or worse, other Hunters. Some never return because it’s safer that way. Their families could become a target if a jealous or defeated Hunter discovered a family connection.”

     The Prime paused, watching a little girl braid another’s hair.

     “Even when a Hunter lives, their absence becomes permanent. Some families wait five years… ten. Eventually, they grieve for them like the deceased.”

     A boy tripped and fell on the stones. Before he could cry, two patient adults came forward and helped him up gently, with practiced care.

     The Prime continued walking.

     “You already know about the two-child rule. But what happens to the orphans of Hunters?”

     That stopped him cold.

     What’s going on? It’s like he knows what Note told me.

     The Prime gestured toward the quarter without pausing.

     “They’re raised here. By the upper class. Everyone here experienced loss. Families of Hunters take in the children of the fallen, or those whose parents never returned. It isn’t luxury, it’s gratitude.”

     Haldric’s eyes narrowed as he considered the words.

     “You said ‘never returned.’ What happens to the family of a Hunter who abandoned the city?”

     The Prime sighed.  “A stone can only weather the storm for so long before it cracks. And though its task is to shield others, even stones can be overwhelmed, rolling away in search of shelter of their own. Who are we to blame the pebbles left behind, when we failed to be the shelter the stone needed?”

     It made a strange, compassionate kind of sense. Haldric felt his stomach twist. Why couldn’t he quiet the doubt in his heart? After all, everything made sense.

     What am I missing?

     Haldric was unconvinced.

     “So this whole upper class is just a glorified orphanage?” He just wanted reassurance, but the words that came out were sharper than he intended.

     The Prime let out a quiet breath. He didn’t appear offended, but he wasn’t amused either.

     “That’s an oversimplification, but not entirely wrong.”

     Haldric stayed quiet, waiting for The Prime to continue.

     “Though I never understood why they declined the relocation invitation, you had your Grayparents to look after you. Many are less fortunate. The family of Hunters… the orphans of miners…” The Prime’s voice softened. “This is the only part of the city where they’re allowed to be children. That’s what this district protects.”

     Haldric let the ration bar rest in his hand. He didn’t open it.

     “Then where is the bolting food going?” He lashed out, unsure what to believe anymore. “I saw the ledgers! So much food is allocated here! The only other location that gets that much is the general education program!”

     “Do you still not understand, Haldric? Everyone must have a role. Those who once raised children of their own, those who lost sons or daughters to the surface, they’re reassigned to care for the little ones who lost everything.”

     He stopped, turning to Haldric fully.

     “But what about a child’s role? To dream. To give others a sense of purpose. These are often the most difficult tasks, yet children accomplish them effortlessly.”

     The Prime gestured toward the surrounding homes.

     “This place isn’t about indulgence or privilege. It’s about healing and meaning. You asked where the food goes, but didn’t you answer that yourself? Why would the Noble Quarters, which you called a ‘glorified orphanage,’ receive a food allocation similar to the education program?”

     Haldric felt it finally click into place.

     “I’m a bolting idiot. It’s for the children.”

     “To both statements, yes,” The Prime said with a grin. “Though it’s true everyone gets a little more food here, they’ve earned at least that much. Still, the bulk of it goes to the children. We as leaders have a responsibility to keep them happy and alive. We must give them a chance to grow into something more than grief and loneliness.

     “The Noble Quarter was my first project as The Prime. I had believed I was so clever. The name refers to the noble sacrifice their loved ones made for this city. Not some bureaucratic nobility with an unjustifiable entitlement toward indulgence.”

     The Prime grit his teeth, face flushing red. Anger boiled behind his eyes, veins rising along his neck. But just as quickly as it came, the fury vanished without a trace.

     Haldric took a step back. He didn’t mean to but—stone me! What was that?

     “People will twist anything,” The Prime said quietly. “You give someone shelter, they call it a cage. You give someone food, they call it leverage. You try to build peace, and they’ll say you’re trying to break them. That’s why I don’t waste time defending myself anymore.”

     He began walking again.

     “Intentions don’t matter when no one’s listening. So I stopped talking and kept building. ”

     Haldric said nothing at first. For a moment, he almost believed it. Every word sounded right. Every justification felt earned. But something still clawed at the back of his mind. His stomach growled, a quiet betrayal that brought it all rushing back: the breeding ledgers, the intentionally reduced meat orders, his starving Grayparents.

     You say you care about these people. And I want to believe you. I want to believe your intentions are good. But if you truly care about all of us… why the lie?

     He looked at The Prime and spoke with a calm he didn’t feel.

     “There is no shortage,” Haldric said. “We both know that. So why do we all go hungry? Why force the city to starve if it’s not about control?”

     The Prime leaned back slightly, watching Haldric with a look that hovered between patience and quiet expectation.

     “You think I’m cruel,” he said. “Because I control how much food enters the city. Because I ensure there is no excess. But tell me, Haldric. Do you know what happens to a body when it’s overfed?”

     Haldric gave a dry look. “Let me guess. They’d be happier?”

     The Prime exhaled through his nose.

     “Chronic disease. That’s the first problem. Excess weight strains the heart, slows the body, increases the risk of illness. High blood pressure, failing organs—an early death.” His fingers tapped once against the table. “And in a world like ours, that is not a luxury we can afford.”

     Haldric’s stomach twisted.

     “There’s also the strain on medical supplies,” The Prime continued. “Every individual who deteriorates due to their own gluttony takes resources from those who suffer through no fault of their own. Every medicine, every ration, every breath wasted on a body that was compromised by choice.” His eyes flicked toward Haldric. “And in a city with limited means, every unnecessary loss weakens us.”

     Haldric swallowed hard.

     The Prime leaned forward.

     “And what happens to a society when its people grow heavy? They slow. Productivity collapses. They take more than they give. They demand comfort over discipline. And over time, they become incapable of standing when the world demands they fight.”

     Haldric clenched his fists.

     “You think I’m cruel. Perhaps I am.” The Prime’s gaze locked onto his. “But tell me, Haldric. Have you ever seen a single obese Hunter?”

     Haldric hesitated, but said nothing.

     The Prime gave a small nod, as if that silence was all the confirmation he needed.

     “Do you know what happens to a body when it reaches the surface?” he asked. “You’ve felt it. The way your lungs expand. The way your muscles adapt. The way your heart strains against itself until it can withstand the weight of the world above.”

     Haldric swallowed. He had felt it. The burning. The pressure. The way his bones seemed to shift inside him.

     The Prime exhaled slowly.

     “Now imagine if you carried even a fraction more weight than you should.”

     Haldric’s stomach turned.

     No. It couldn’t be—

     “The expansion would kill you,” The Prime said. His voice was grim. “Your lungs would collapse before they could stretch. Your heart would fail before it could strengthen. Your body would crush itself from the inside out.”

     Haldric’s hands curled tighter.

     “That’s not—”

     “It is true,” The Prime cut in. “Do you think we don’t have records? Do you think we don’t know exactly what happens when someone is unprepared for acclimation? We learned the hard way, Haldric. We lost lives. We watched people suffocate because their bodies weren’t fit to withstand the change.”

     The words landed like stone.

     The Prime pressed on, his voice even.

     “If I allowed overindulgence, if I permitted the city to gorge itself into lethargy, it wouldn’t just be a matter of laziness or inefficiency.” His eyes darkened. “It would be a death sentence.”

     Haldric’s breath caught in his throat.

     The Prime’s tone didn’t rise. It didn’t need to.

     “This city survives because I do not allow weakness,” he said. “You may hate me for it. You may think I'm a tyrant.” His jaw shifted slightly. “But I would rather be despised than watch my people choke to death in their own skin.”

     The silence that followed was total.

     Haldric didn’t move. Couldn’t speak.

     Because for the first time, he had no idea if The Prime was wrong.

     It all made sense. Too much sense. The puzzle was snapping together too quickly, every piece falling into place with brutal logic.

     But something still gnawed at him.

     The words slipped out before he could stop them.

     “This makes sense for Hunters. For those who will go to the surface. But most people in Stormhaven will never leave the city.” His throat felt tight. “Why must they suffer too?” He looked down. “Why must my Grayparents?”

     Something flickered in The Prime’s expression. A shift. Guilt, maybe. Or sorrow. It vanished too quickly to be certain.

     “Because they might have to.”

     He let the silence breathe for a moment, then stood.

     “Come.”

     Haldric followed in silence as The Prime led him down the hall toward the Second Floor. Aware of his own ignorance, he figured he’d get more answers by listening with an open mind rather than resisting every piece of information that challenged his assumptions. After all, the more he listened, the harder it became to defend his own conclusions.

     The air thickened with the faint echo of workers below, the clang of stone tools and the steady murmur of motion. The city continued around them, unaware of where they were going. 

     Then The Prime turned left, toward the Gate.

     Haldric’s pulse picked up. He knew this path.

     The guards at the checkpoint stepped aside without hesitation. A heavy groan shuddered through the chamber as the first set of doors unlocked, revealing the path to the surface.

     A long climb.

     Yet as they began, the steady pace through the old tunnel felt like an exercise Haldric’s body had long since surpassed. His muscles didn’t burn. His breath stayed even. But his heart still raced, as if it disagreed.

    The stone walls smoothed around them, no longer jagged but almost natural in shape. The ceiling stretched higher. The air thinned.

     As they approached the final gate, Haldric noticed subtle changes in the stonework. A faint seam ran horizontally across the massive barrier. The Prime’s lips curved into a subtle smile.

“You should appreciate this innovation,” he said, his voice carrying a hint of pride. “We’ve just completed my newest gate design. If anything manages to breach the lower half, the upper section will release and come crashing down, eliminating any intruders instantly. Another layer of protection to keep this city safe.”

Haldric blinked, trying to mask his surprise. It hadn’t been long since he last surfaced. When had The Prime found time to make such a drastic improvement?

     The Prime raised a fist and twisted it, giving a sharp signal.

     Four guards responded immediately, each gripping a separate crank embedded in the stone wall. With synchronized effort, they began to turn the mechanisms.

        The gate groaned open in two halves: the top half rising upward and the bottom half lowering into the ground, like the opening jaws of a giant stone mouth. A rush of dry wind surged through the widening gap, sizzling like the aftermath of a BoltStone. The scent of scorched rock choked Haldric’s nose, cutting off a deep inhale in its tracks.

        Morning light pierced the dim cave, though it lacked the strength and heat of Haldric’s last journey to the surface. He squinted as the stone lips parted further, the tunnel’s mouth drawing a breath. A gust of air rushed through, whistling as it curled around the stone teeth of stalactites and stalagmites before brushing against Haldric’s skin. 

     He froze as the world exhaled, gentle, like a whisper. The timid but curious breeze crawled up his arms and across his face. The city’s ZephyrStones, which circulated air, could never imitate this strange sensation. It felt real. It felt alive. It felt wrong.

      So wrong that Haldric missed what The Prime was looking up at.

      Above them, stretching wide and terrifyingly close, loomed another planet.

      The second world hung over Ravour like a ceiling, green continents swirling beneath thick clouds. Rivers shimmered like silver threads, glinting under a distant sun.

      The Prime inhaled slowly and said, “Ferta Mathair. One of the six planets that pass us each week.” His voice was quiet. “Isn’t it beautiful?”

      He didn’t look at Haldric. His gaze remained fixed upward. “A planet that doesn’t try to kill you.”

      Haldric forced his gaze away from the sky.

      “Why are we here?” he asked. “What did you want to show me?”

      The Prime breathed out. “We were never meant to be here.”

      “What do you mean?” Haldric squinted, rubbing his tired eyes as they adjusted to the giant SolarStone peeking above the horizon.

      The Prime didn’t answer right away. His eyes lingered on the planet above.

      “Ravour, our planet, is dying,” he said at last.

      Haldric stilled.

      The Prime’s voice remained emotionless. “Acclimation is getting harder.” His breath was even, controlled. “It once required only half a day of decreased food to guarantee safe acclimation. Then weeks. Then months. Now, a permanent decrease in food. The surface demands more from unacclimated people each year. And the more it demands, the fewer people survive. Their lungs collapse. Their hearts rupture. It’s unpleasant to witness.”

      Haldric’s throat tightened.

      “I… I didn’t know,” Haldric stammered.

      “That’s why the food shortage exists,” The Prime continued. “It isn’t a punishment. It’s a requirement. If we don’t keep people slim, they won’t be ready when the time comes.”

      Haldric’s chest felt tight.

      The Prime exhaled. “But even now, it’s not enough. Eventually, people will starve long before they’re thin enough to survive acclimation. At the rate we’re going, the human population will go extinct in a matter of years.”

      He paused, then added, “You’re already acclimated, Haldric. This doesn’t apply to you. Any acclimated could eat in excess, but would that be fair to the others?” His expression didn’t change. “This is why I eat the same rations that I ask them to eat.”

 “Something is wrong with that explanation,” Praew said in his mind.

Yeah, there must be more to it, he thought.

Haldric didn’t bother to hide his suspicion. “Then ration just the children until their labor roles are assigned. Only Hunters and soldiers who reach the surface need to worry about acclimation, right?”

The Prime regarded him in silence for a moment. Then his gaze shifted toward the horizon. “Ten noble beasts possess the strength to drive all humankind from the surface. Just ten.” He paused. “Twelve years ago, one of those foul creatures had finally been slain.”

Haldric perked up. “They… they can really be killed?” He couldn’t hold back his excitement.

“Yes,” The Prime said. “They can be killed. Now only ten remain.”

Haldric frowned. “You mean nine.”

The Prime looked at Haldric, face devoid of emotion. “No, I do not. The noble beasts… have since learned to reproduce.”

Haldric tasted blood. When had he clenched his jaw so tight?

The Prime looked back toward the imposing city gate, his face stoic. “A single addition to their ranks could spell the eradication of our species long before acclimation has a chance to.”

Something cold gripped the base of Haldric’s spine.

This explained so much, yet… Haldric looked down, jaw still clenched. 

 “The Prime dodged your question,” Praew pointed out in his head.

 True, he thought, why my Grayparents? Most citizens will never reach the surface. Why are they forced to starve as well?

      He looked up sharply.

      “Earlier, you said ‘ready when the time comes.’” His voice was rough. “Ready for what? How can we stop this?”

      The Prime didn’t answer.

      Haldric’s frustration flared, but before he could push further, something shifted in the corner of his vision.

      A figure made his throat clench.

      A white-haired girl, small and weightless in the wind.

     Haldric knew her but wished he didn’t. His pulse slammed in his ears as his mouth moved before he could stop it.

     “You again!”

     The Prime turned. “What?”

     Haldric went still. A sick realization twisted behind his ribs.

     The Prime still couldn’t see her. He showed no acknowledgment of the pale shape darting across the rocks. Hadn’t seen her hands clap together. Hadn’t heard her laugh, a surprisingly dainty tone, when the silver beetle with glowing wings flew through her hand.

     Haldric squeezed his eyes shut. She wasn’t real. Just like the voices in my head. He snapped his gaze back to her.

     Still there, pretending she could catch it again.

     Haldric cleared his throat. Ignore her.

     His fingers curled into his palms as he forced himself to face The Prime. “Nothing.”

     The Prime narrowed his eyes but didn’t press.

     Haldric inhaled, forcing the apparition from his mind. His voice came out rough. “What can we do?”

     The Prime’s answer was simple. “We leave.”

     For a moment, the word didn’t feel real. Leave? Haldric’s mind turned it over, searching for the shape of it. The idea felt so… unheroic. How could he even entertain the thought?

     His voice came quieter than he expected. “Leave… the planet?”

     The Prime nodded once. “Like I told you before, Ravour is not our home. There are ways out.”

     Something in Haldric’s stomach twisted. Way out, escape, running.

     “Coward.” Asher’s voice this time. 

     He wanted to argue, but information was more important.

     Haldric swallowed hard. “How?”

     The Prime exhaled. “There are three portals,” he said. “Hidden somewhere on Ravour. They were built long before our time, and each leads to a different fate.”

     Haldric stiffened. “Portals?”

     The Prime’s gaze didn’t waver. “The ruins describe them as doors, placed long ago. One leads to Morlorn, the planet of death. One leads to the Origin. And one… leads to Ferta Mathair, the planet of nature where we came from.”

     Haldric felt his pulse in his throat as he looked up. “Ferta Mathair… so that’s where we were taken from by the thief?”

     The Prime nodded. “Stormhaven is only one of three human cities still standing,” he said, his voice low, burdened. “Three. And yet we waste what little strength we have fighting each other over scraps.”

     Haldric stayed silent, watching him. 

     “I’ve reached out,” The Prime continued. “Tried to form alliances. But they either don’t believe the portals exist… or they think it’s a ploy to invade.” He grit his teeth. “Bolting fools.”

     He turned then, facing Haldric fully. “There are three kinds of Hunters. Beast Hunters, stone Hunters, and resource Hunters. But the world forgot the first kind.”

     Haldric frowned. “The first kind?”

     The Prime stepped forward, voice harder now. “Portal Hunters.”

     A beat of silence passed between them.

     “We need them again, Haldric.”

     Haldric’s brow furrowed. “But… how is it possible no one’s ever found one? Not a single portal?”

     The Prime’s eyes held a quiet, tired fire. “Who says no one has?”

     Haldric hesitated.

     “If you found a way out of this wretched world,” The Prime asked, “and barely survived the journey to reach it, would you come back? Would you risk everything to tell others? Or would you stay, live, and leave the rest behind?”

     “I would return,” Haldric said without hesitation. “Even if it cost me my life.”

     The Prime studied him for a long moment, then nodded, almost to himself. “That,” he said softly, “is why I chose you.”

     Silence stretched between them.

     What a strange man, Haldric looked toward The Prime. He had noticed long ago that in moments when anyone else might show a flicker of emotion, The Prime became as still and cold as stone. It was as if, in trying to hide his feelings, he revealed them all the more. His stone heart had been eroded by a storm of emotions beneath.

     The Prime broke the silence. “The world needs you, Haldric. The world doesn’t need another strong arm or cold strategist. It needs a kind heart willing to risk everything. I… will not suffice.”

     Haldric kept his focus on The Prime, but something was wrong. The wind didn’t stir. The world around them was still. Too still. Then a flicker at the edge of his vision.

     He tried not to acknowledge it, but his eyes betrayed him, glancing in her direction before snapping back to The Prime.

     The girl stepped forward without a sound. She was close now. Too close.

     Every muscle in Haldric’s body forgot how to move as she leaned in, tilting her head, studying him.

     His skin prickled. He willed himself not to react. 

     “Control your breathing,” Asher warned in his head.

     Her head moved slightly, face inches from his. The pale strands of her hair didn’t sway, as if untouched by the wind. Her gaze flicked over his features, tracing them like she was trying to remember.

     The breath of a soft whisper tickled his ear. “You look so much like him. It’s uncanny.”

     Haldric turned to face her, but a deep, instinctive tension curled in his spine, urging him to run. Without meaning to, his foot slid back. The retreat was small, barely a shift in weight, but it felt cowardly. He was acclimated. Why should some imaginary girl who couldn’t even hurt a beetle scare him?

     The air felt colder as a shudder ran through him. He glanced back at her, but she was gone.

     The silence stretched.

     The Prime looked confused as he stared at Haldric.

     Did he see her?

     “Uhh,” Haldric choked out, “how did you learn about these portals?”

     “The ruins I told you about earlier.” The Prime’s reply felt terse.

     No, he definitely didn’t see her. Great, I really am crazy…

     “The ruins speak of the portals’ existence,” The Prime continued, “but not their locations. We know only that they are here… somewhere. The world has not been very accommodating during our search.”

     Haldric remained focused. “And what if we don’t find the right one?”

     The Prime met his gaze. “We’ll go extinct.”

     This was it, wasn’t it? The truth behind the biggest mystery. The real reason they all had to starve. Hunter or civilian, The Prime Archon intended for everyone to leave this planet together. Everyone would have to acclimate.

     What was a little hunger compared to the preservation of our species? What’s a little hunger compared to never living underground again?

     Haldric swallowed. “I… I understand.” And he did. Truly, he did.

     He steadied himself. “What can I do? How can I help?”

     The Prime met his gaze. “You can start by putting a little faith in me.”

     Again, fear spiked through him. His shoulder jerked just slightly, but enough for the sweat gathering at his temple to slip, trailing cold down his cheek. He clenched his jaw, forcing himself still.

     The girl was perched on a boulder, legs swinging. Watching.

     His fingers curled at his sides. He could ignore her. He had to.

     Her head tilted, eyes locked on him.

     The Prime… did he say something? Haldric forced himself to look away, just in time to see The Prime gesture toward the gate.

     Haldric nodded. Without another word, he turned and stepped back through the gate, descending into the city.

     The Prime watched The Heir Archon go. The stone doors rumbled as they sealed shut behind him.

     He sighed. Then, without turning, he spoke. “Must you do that to him?”

     A soft laugh rang out behind him.

     “How disturbed must one be to find amusement in the torment of others?” Arthur asked.

     “You’re the one who pretends I don’t exist,” she teased.

     The voice was closer now, but he didn’t turn. He never did.

     She would be perched on a nearby boulder, legs swinging, weightless as ever. Watching. Always watching.

     The Prime’s voice remained steady. “Are you certain he’s the one? What made you choose him as my successor?”

     A smirk flickered at the edge of his vision.

     Her.

     The white-haired phantom that refused to be forgotten.

     She swayed on her heels, hands tucked behind her back, watching him with that insufferable amusement. Like she found it hysterical to linger where she didn’t belong.

     “Oh, no reason,” she murmured. “I just like his name. It reminds me of someone…”

     Of course it did.

     Slowly, deliberately, he turned toward her.

     His glare was sharp. A silent demand to stop playing games.

     She only smiled wider.

     Of course she did.

     Tilting her head, she hummed. “Oh, come on,” she teased. “You don’t trust me?”

     He folded his arms. “Trust you? Don’t presume we are allies, girl. I will never forgive you. I only care about saving my people.”

     She hummed. Not offended. If anything, entertained.

     “Oh, how noble…” she mused. “Yet you lie to the boy.”

     His jaw tightened. “When did I speak falsehoods?”

     Her smirk widened. “A lie of omission is worse than a lie of volition.”

     Compose yourself, Arthur.

     “Then how about you stop omitting what you really know, girl?”

     She snorted. Then, like mist in the wind, she was gone.

     The Prime let the silence settle. He uncrossed his arms.

     The passage was empty again.

     Good.

     Without another glance, he turned and walked back into the depths of the city.
