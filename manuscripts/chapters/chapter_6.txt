Chapter 6 - To Kill or Die



In the blink of an eye, Praew was heading to class again. Yesterday was a blur—from going home, to crashing into sleep, to getting her ear talked off again this morning.

“I’m sooo excited! Today is basically my first class ever! I’m so nervous I feel like I could hurl!”

That chatty girl from yesterday was walking beside her again.

Zephyrs take me.

Praew had even left earlier than usual just to enjoy a silent morning. What was this girl doing—waiting for her?

“What do you think? Any advice?” the girl asked, snapping Praew out of her thoughts.

“Oh—um, have you tried meditation before class? It might help,” Praew offered.

“Yeah, I tried that yesterday. Helped a little but… now I’m even more nervous today. By the way, are you okay? You seem like you’re limping a little.”

Praew hadn’t even realized it. Her fall from yesterday already felt like a distant memory.

“I’m fine,” she snapped, a little too quickly.

The girl seemed to get the hint. “Oh, uh… right. Well, I better not hold you up. I’ll see you later, Praew!” She took off running, her footsteps sluggishly loud down the corridor.

Who was that girl? She still hasn’t even told me her name.

As Praew grabbed her food and headed to class, something else occurred to her.

How did she know my name? Did I ever introduce myself?

Whatever. It was too early for this.



***



When Praew stepped inside the classroom, another masked figure sat meditating—shorter than the one from yesterday, but dressed in the same all-black uniform. This one wore a different kind of lifeless mask.

What stood out most wasn’t the mask. It was the fact that the figure was meditating on top of the desk.

The indecency of it made every student hesitate in silent, obvious discomfort. No one said a word—still scarred from yesterday’s silence rule, still unsure if it was in effect. Nine students. No sign of Jorpen.

Oh no. He’s not actually dead… is he?

The masked figure climbed down without a word, turned their back to the class, and wrote on the slate board:

Ghost Operation

A muffled female voice came from behind the mask. “My name is—ughh, I hate these stupid things. Like seriously, how are we supposed to talk with these on when you can barely breathe?”

Praew’s brow twitched. The voice irritated her instantly, though she couldn’t explain why.

The figure pulled off the mask and head covering in one motion, revealing that same warm smile, those dark almond-shaped eyes, jet-black hair, and golden-brown skin that somehow still caught the light in this dim room—along with the same over-talkative energy that had haunted Praew’s mornings.

“You!?” Praew blurted.

“Me!” the girl beamed, all giddy and glowing.

The insufferable, walking EchoStone who had annoyed her two mornings in a row now stood at the front of the class.

“My name is Mai Azadi,” she said, hands on hips. “And in this class, you beautiful rays of SolarStones are going to learn how to assassinate people.”

She clapped once—loud and sharp. “Now I know what you’re thinking. Me? Kill someone? But I’m just a sweet little Faelhen trying to get through life mimicking everyone else—without clawing anyone!”

She spun around, twirled a writing stone between her fingers, and underlined the words on the slate.

Ghost Operation.

“That’s adorable,” she said brightly. “Truly. But no. If you’re in this class, you’re going to learn how to stab people in exactly the right place to make sure they don’t scream. Or better yet—how to make it look like they died in their sleep. Or tripped on their own stupidity.”

She turned back to the class, eyes twinkling. “Fun, right?”

No one spoke.

“Oh, still doing the no-talking thing?” she said, mock-whispering. “That was Instructor Shadow. So mysterious. I bet they even taught you to breathe quietly.”

She leaned over the desk. “Not me. Breathe loud. Ask questions. Or don’t. I’ll just keep talking anyway.”

Right on cue, the room flickered—one full breath of darkness.

Zzip. Zzip. Zzip.

Air split in rapid succession.

“Ow—what the—”

“Something hit me!”

Praew flinched at the sting. Her whole body tensed—something had definitely struck her ear. Her hand shot up on instinct. A thin needle pierced clean through her right lobe, warm and smooth, embedded like a forged earring.

The light slowly returned.

Mai stood at the front, clapping. “Beautiful form! Not a single miss.”

Praew looked around—every student had one. Perfect placement. Gleaming tips. Not a drop of blood.

Mai beamed. “Oh, and those were soaked in a very deadly poison, by the way. But don’t worry—it’s very slow-acting. You’ll be just fine… if you finish class and do your part. Otherwise, well… no antidote for you.”

A hand shot up. Bpen looked nervous and flushed, his voice cracking as he stood halfway.

“Wait, what? You poisoned us? That’s insane! And assassinating people? That’s not heroic, it’s cowardly!”

Mai tilted her head, eyes narrowing just a touch. Her smile didn’t move.

He tried to go on. “You’re clearly the same age as us, how can you be our instructor? You’re just some—”

Thip.  

As Mai covered her eyes with her right hand, a metal needle flicked from her left, piercing his other ear mid-sentence.

She sighed, still smiling but her voice grew sharp. “That was a larger dose of poison. Any more and you won’t make it to the end of class. Consider this a mercy. If it hit your eye, you would be blind. Lips or tongue? You wouldn't have survived another breath.”

She looked around at the rest of them, voice chipper again.

“To learn how to assassinate is also to learn how not to be assassinated. Cowardly or not… you will learn. Or you will die.”

Mai twirled her writing stone, then spun toward the board. “Now, arteries!“

With a few quick strokes, she drew a sloppy stick figure and jabbed three dots onto it—neck, inner thigh, and wrist.

“There are lots of ways to kill someone,” Mai said brightly, “but arteries are your best friends. Veins take too long, guts are too messy, and bones? Don’t even get me started.”

She tapped the neck dot with the stone. “This one’s your carotid. Slice it clean, and they’ll pass out in half a flicker. Dead in one if they don’t get help. It’s fast, flashy, and nice and quiet if you don’t hesitate.”

She moved to the thigh. “Femoral artery. Big one. You’ll know you hit it when the blood arcs. It’s dramatic. They’ll bleed out in a flicker, maybe two. Not subtle, but fun at parties.”

She pointed to the wrist. “This works if you’re patient. Slowest of the three. Might take three flickers or more, but if you’re trying to make it look like an accident? This one’s your girl.”

She spun back to face them, eyes bright. “There’s plenty more, but these are the classics. Stab here—” she jabbed her own neck lightly, “and it’s over before the breath’s even finished.”

Mai spun her writing stone once more, then walked to the back of the classroom. She pressed her hand against a panel that didn’t look like a door—until it slid open with a low hiss.

Behind it was a narrow, dim hallway.

“Closest to the door,” she said sweetly. “Come with me.”

The student nearest the back stepped forward, stiff and hesitant, then followed her through. The door slid shut behind them.

Three flickers passed.

Only Mai returned.

She was still smiling. “Next.”

And so it went. One by one, students were called. One by one, they entered the hallway. And only Mai returned. Her cheer never faded. Her voice never changed.

“Next.”

Soon, only four students remained—Junya, Wannii, Bpen, and Praew. They’d huddled together without even realizing it, like prey flocking tight. None of them spoke above a whisper. No one laughed.

Wannii was called. She swallowed hard, nodded once, and left without looking back.

“Next.”

Bpen stood slower. His legs shook. He looked like he might cry. He didn’t say a word as he followed Mai into the dark.

Now it was just Praew and Junya.

They stood in silence until Junya slowly turned toward her, her voice barely audible.

“If I don’t come back,” she whispered, “please look for a man named Note on the Third Floor. The food shortages aren’t real.”

Then Mai returned.

“Next.”

Junya stood. Her eyes met Praew’s for a heartbeat. Then she stepped into the hallway, and the door sealed behind her.

Praew was alone.

Her heart thudded in her chest—louder than the silence.

Finally, the door opened once more.

Mai stood there, glowing as ever. “Your turn, Praew.”

The door sealed behind her with a quiet hiss.

Praew stepped into a dim, narrow room lit only by a single SolarStone embedded in the ceiling. The air felt damp and wrong, like it had been still for too long. At the center of the room sat a man bound to a heavy wooden chair. His legs were tied down, his wrists strapped together in front of him and anchored to his knees. He wasn’t struggling but his breathing was so frantic, shaking with wide, terrified eyes.

When he saw Praew, his voice cracked. “Please. I have a wife… a daughter. She’s only four. They need me. I haven’t done anything wrong!”

Mai stepped forward, her voice disturbingly calm. “Don’t worry,” she said. “I already paid them a visit. Don’t you want to join them?”

The man broke. His sobs tore through the room, wild and unrestrained. He begged louder, blubbering nonsense, his words lost beneath panic.

Praew froze. Her limbs felt heavier than stone. There was nothing threatening about the man—not in his voice, not in his eyes. He looked like a man who’d been broken long before today.

Mai stepped in behind her and let the door close. She walked over to a small stone table without a word, picked up a narrow, polished blade, then held it out to Praew. “One of three arteries,” she said brightly. “Neck, thigh, or wrist. Clean or messy, doesn’t matter to me. Just end him.”

Praew didn’t move. Her hands clenched at her sides as the man began to sob, whispering his daughter’s name.

“I’m not a killer,” she said, barely audible.

Mai tilted her head, her smile still fixed. “You will be if you want to live. This is an order from The Prime Archon himself.”

Praew’s stomach churned. The man was pleading louder now, saying things she didn’t want to hear. Saying he was innocent. That he didn’t deserve to die. Her heart pounded, each thud louder than the last. Still, she didn’t move.

Mai lowered the blade slightly, her voice dropping to something flat and cold. “If you don’t do it, you won’t need to worry about the antidote. I’ll kill you right here and now. Then he will join you. Treason will not be tolerated.”

Praew turned toward her. She wanted to speak, to argue, but the words stuck. Mai took a step forward. Her gaze no longer playful, no longer forgiving. Just a blade with legs and a smile.

“I said no,” Praew whispered, then louder, trembling, “I won’t do it.”

Mai kept coming.

If this is what it takes to live… then maybe I’m not meant to survive this place. 

Backed into a wall of panic, Praew shut her eyes. Her thoughts darted wildly—until something Haldric once told her surfaced, clear as a breath between heartbeats:

“Every good Hunter knows to be careful when you corner a beast. Most will cower, becoming easy prey, but some bare their teeth. Cornered beasts are the most unpredictable—‘cause they have nothing left to lose.”

No! She would not cower in her final moments.

Praew’s eyes snapped open and locked onto Mai’s. “Then get it over with,” she said with a sneer, raising her chin like a blade ready to clash with the one in Mai’s hand.

Praew braced herself—body tense, breath held, heart wild.

Mai Azadi hesitated.

Praew felt it—a flurry of emotion breaking through her perfect mask. Mai’s eyes narrowed. She swallowed. Her fingers fidgeted around the hilt of the blade. 

Maybe she wouldn’t do it. Maybe the blade will lower.

But then—for just a blink—Mai’s gaze darted to the man. And when her eyes returned to Praew, they were set.

“No!” Praew shouted, lunging forward. But it was too late. Praew felt the warmth, wet and thick.

Shlick.

Blood sprayed across her face.

Praew gasped. Hands shaking, she wiped at her face and snapped her eyes open. The man was slumped in the chair, his throat neatly cut open. Blood streamed down his chest and pooled around his feet. His eyes were still open, but there was nothing left behind them.

“What are you doing!?” Praew cried out.

Mai rushed forward and clamped a hand over Praew’s mouth. “I’m saving your life!” Mai hissed. “Now eat a stone and listen.”

Praew froze. Her breath came in short, ragged bursts.

Mai turned and pointed to a side door near the back wall. “The others are waiting. Walk through like you did the job. Pretend you slit his throat. Don’t say a word. I’ll figure out what to do with you later.”

Praew nodded slowly and walked out the door. The blood on her face felt hotter than before.

The hallway was longer than before. Each step landed like stone. Her fingers twitched, slick with dried blood that had already begun to flake. She rubbed them against her pants but couldn’t get rid of the warmth, or the stain.

She hadn’t killed anyone.

And yet she couldn’t stop shaking.

The waiting room was smaller than Praew expected—and far too quiet. No one spoke. No one moved.

The air was thick with the metallic stench of blood. It clung to everything—skin, uniforms, mouths. Every student was drenched in it. A few trembled. Most didn’t blink. Wide eyes fixed to nothing. As empty as the dead man tied to the chair.

Praew spotted Junya near the far wall, her jaw clenched so tight it looked painful. “We need to talk,” Praew said quietly, stepping toward her. “After class.”

Junya gave a small nod. That was all.

The door behind them scraped open.

Mai Azadi entered like nothing was wrong, her smile intact—but not reaching her eyes.

“Congratulations,” she said brightly, clapping once. “You all passed.”

Not a single student reacted.

Her smile wavered. When Mai spoke again, her voice lowered in an unusual moment of solemnity.

“Look, I know what you just went through was traumatic. You’ll find no comfort in hearing that it gets easier. Honestly, that might just make it worse.” She paused, glancing at a student near the corner who wouldn’t stop shaking. “But The Prime Archon makes this test mandatory in the first class so we can get it over with.”

A long silence stretched. Then her gaze swept across the room.

“There will be times on the surface,” she said slowly, “when you’ll have to kill another human. Trust me. The worst beasts I’ve ever fought… the ones that haunt me the most… never had claws or horns. They were human.”

No one responded. No one dared to protest.

But then the moment passed, and with a flick of her hand, her voice returned to its usual springy rhythm.

“Anyway! You’ll be surprised what you can do with a little pressure! I mean, I wasn’t even acclimated yet when I almost killed your other teacher. Mr. Stone-Face himself, Silverwing Godric!”

That broke the silence. A ripple moved through the group. A few heads turned. A murmur rose.

Junya raised a hand, the movement stiff. “Wait… Professor Stone is the Silverwing Godric, Mythical Beast Hunter? The one who wrote Crescent Moon Srevlis?”

“Yup! Is that book any good? I’ve never read it,” Mai said cheerfully, like she hadn’t just dropped a boulder into the room. “You guys didn’t know that? Huh. I guess that’s why he told me not to use his real name around you. Oh well!”

The room said nothing. A few students shifted. One coughed. No one looked at anyone else.

She shrugged, then clapped again, a little off the beat this time. “Alright! Door to your right leads to two wash stations—men’s and women’s. Can’t exactly have you walking the halls covered in blood, can we? You’ll find fresh uniforms in your sizes inside.”

She grinned. “And if anyone enters the wrong wash station,” she added sweetly, “I’ll kill them where they stand.”

It was probably a joke, but no one laughed.

Mai gave the group one last sweeping glance. “Oh yeah, and Praew?” Her gaze stopped on her. “I’d like you to stay behind and speak with me. Everyone else, class dismissed.”

“Oh, one last thing,” Mai added, raising a finger like she’d just remembered something mildly important, just before anyone had the chance to leave.

“Don’t get too distracted, by the way. Forgetting little details can get you killed.”

The students froze. Several hands darted to their ears.

“The poison,” someone whispered.

Mai beamed. “Relax. I stabbed you with a second needle while you were busy executing your targets. That one had the antidote.”

“So congrats! You passed, you didn’t die, and now you smell like a birthing clinic.” She shrugged. “Still counts as a win. Now go clean up.”

Mai waited until the last of the class had shuffled out toward the wash stations. Only then did she speak. “Praew! I have great news. I failed you!”

Praew blinked. The words barely registered before Mai was already rambling on.

“I marked you down for successfully completing the kill—but I wrote that you hesitated too much and recommended expulsion from this class. So now you won’t have to assassinate anyone else!”

“Anyone else?” Praew echoed. The words felt loose in her mouth.

“Yeah! Isn’t that awesome? That means you’ll have to pass both of the other classes, though. Otherwise, you’ll get reassigned to the mines or something much worse, andm ugh, you don’t want that, trust me.” She grinned wide. “Ha! I see that stunned look on your face. It’s okay. You don’t have to thank me.”

This bolting woman.

“Thank you?!—I—” Praew began to protest, but Mai cut her off with a cheerful, “You’re welcome!”

Then she leaned in slightly, voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper. “So, how’d I do? Not bad for my first day? I took your advice and meditated before class—totally helped, but not gonna lie, I was still pretty nervous. And you! I was just about to kill you, but where did you get that strength? The way you looked at me and told me to get it over with… it gave me shivers!”

Praew didn’t respond. She couldn’t.

Mai’s voice softened. “You may have failed my class, but you’ve earned my respect, Praew. So I’ve decided, you can be my friend. I’m glad I met you. I can’t wait to get to know you better. I already feel like—”

“Can I go now?” Praew asked.

Mai paused. “ Uhh. Sure.” She sounded dejected.

Good, Praew thought as she turned and left. The dried blood made her skin tighten, but Mai’s words made her skin crawl.

What a farce. Who did that woman think she was—playing friend, pretending this was normal? Thank her? Not in this bolting lifetime. At least she’d failed this class. With luck, she’d never have to see Mai Azadi again.

Praew washed up and changed, fast and careless. No point fussing with the rest. 

When she stepped out, her hair still dripping, Junya was waiting for her in the empty hall.

Praew didn’t even slow her steps. “Talk.”

Junya followed, a little sheepish. Her long black ponytail swayed with every hurried step, the rhythm at odds with the tension in her shoulders. Pale skin, rose-kissed cheeks, and just a splash of freckles along her cheekbones—she looked far too soft for the weight she carried. And she carried something. Praew felt it.

“I was just scared, okay?” Junya mumbled. “I didn’t mean anything by it. Just nonsense, y’know? Thought I was gonna die…”

“Twice,” Praew said flatly. “You’ve mentioned it twice now. That wasn’t nonsense. The look in your eyes was too serious for it to be a joke. Especially as your final words.”

She kept walking. “Who is Note? What do you know about the food shortage?”

“I can’t,” Junya whispered. “I can’t get you involved. It’s too dangerous.”

Praew’s patience snapped. “You involved me the moment you brought it up in a classroom that’s probably being monitored by hidden EchoStones. Or did you really think they wouldn’t supervise an over-talkative assassin teacher, that couldn’t even keep Godric’s name a secret, just in case she said something she shouldn’t?”

The way Mai acted during the test… the way she covered Praew’s mouth and whispered about it after. She acted like they were being monitored.

Behind her, Junya stopped. Praew glanced back—and froze.

Her friend stood shaking, eyes wide, lips trembling. Zephyrs take me, was she… crying? Just what did I get dragged into?

“Fine, but not here,” Junya said, dabbing her eyes with the edge of her shirt. “If you’re right about the room being compromised, there’s no telling if the halls are as well. I don’t live far—let’s go to my place.”

Praew gave a small nod and let her lead. The concern felt a little dramatic, honestly. The classroom? Sure—plenty of places to hide Sifaraliths. But the hallway? The walls were smooth, bright, and clean. No cracks, no clutter, no shadows. Nothing that could mask an embedded EchoStone. But still… if this was what it took for Junya to talk, then fine. Let her feel safe. Safe people talked.

They walked in silence. Not awkward, just… heavy.

In less than four flickers, they arrived.

Before they even stepped inside, one thing stopped Praew cold.

The size.

Praew gasped. “You live alone already? How?!”

“Yeah,” Junya muttered, shrinking a little. “Both of my parents died in the Sifarilith mines earlier this year. I don’t have any other relatives, but since I was about to graduate, they didn’t put me in the orphanage. They gave me a single unit—the same they give all unmarried independents.”

Praew felt like the wind had been knocked out of her. She gasped again, hand flying to her mouth. “I’m so sorry. I couldn’t imagine going through that alone.”

Junya looked away, jaw tight. “It’s fine.”

But Praew could tell it wasn’t. Not even close.

Junya wouldn’t even look at her. She just opened the door and welcomed Praew into her home.

Well, Junya’s place barely qualified as a home—just a single-room unit buried in the Third Floor’s outer ring. A short step divided where she slept from where she lounged, like that somehow made it two rooms. The kitchen sat untouched. Praew never understood why every house had one when no one was allowed to eat at home until they’d had their first child. Another rule no one questioned.

“Sorry,” Junya said, fidgeting with the hem of her pants. Her fingers tugged at the fabric like it might unravel the tension in her chest. “Besides Wannii, you’re the only other person I’ve ever brought home.”

She glanced around the room, avoiding Praew’s eyes. “I—I know it’s not much. Being so empty and all. If it wasn’t for the killing, I would be so excited to actually finish this training so I can finally get supplementary slips and decorate the place. If I survive until then, this place will look a lot—”

Praew didn’t let her finish. She stepped forward and wrapped her arms around her.

Junya froze.

The silence stretched for half a flicker. Then her shoulders slumped, and Praew felt her start to shake. When she looked up, the mask was gone. Tears rolled freely down Junya’s pale cheeks.

“They didn’t care at all,” she choked out. “Someone from The Prime Archon’s office just showed up one day with two soldiers. Said they’re sorry—said my parents were dead—told me to gather my things. I had one flash to move out. One flash!”

Her voice cracked.

    “I only had time to make two trips. A handful of clothes and this protection bracelet my parents gave me.” She raised her arm to reveal a crude bracelet of braided cord with a stone in the center, carved to look like a BoltStone. “That’s all I got to keep.” Her body trembled harder, her voice breaking into hiccups. “How can they be so heartless?” 

The hiccups became sobs. Deep, guttural ones. The kind that rattled out of the ribs and left the throat raw.

Praew said nothing. Just held her tighter.

She didn’t know how long they stood there—three flickers, maybe more—but eventually the cries softened. Junya was still sniffling when she pulled back slightly, wiping at her eyes with the sleeve of her shirt.

“How do you do that?” Junya asked, voice still hoarse. “First with Bpen, now me. You always seem to know just what to say… or do.”

Praew gave a dry laugh. “I wish that were true. I catch myself blurting out ridiculous things at the worst times. I’m not as bad as my friend Haldric, but—stupid me, remember? I called a Mythical Beast Hunter ‘stone-faced.’”

Junya let out a snort. Then a laugh. Then a full-bodied, red-eyed, messy laugh that sounded far too rare.

They laughed together, the tension bleeding out of the room one breath at a time.

“But seriously,” Junya said again, more quietly now, “how do you do it?”

Praew shrugged. “I don’t know. I just speak my mind and act on instinct. Then worry about the consequences later. I may come off super nice or like the meanest person you’ve ever met, but at least I’ll never be known as fake.”

Praew glanced at Junya. “As for the hug… if I were in your shoes, I’d need one. So I gave you one. I’d rather you think I’m weird for hugging you than overthink it and miss the chance to be there.”

“Wow,” Junya whispered. “I hope I can be like that someday.”

“You can,” Praew said, her tone steady now. “Just stop worrying what other people think.”

Praew let that settle. Then, gently, “So… about this food shortage.”
