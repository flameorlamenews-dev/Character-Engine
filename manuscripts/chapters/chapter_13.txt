Chapter 13 - A Question’s Consequence



“But I will meet the Creator knowing I remained faithful to my code.”



     They had barely reached the outskirts of the Third Floor when Note spoke again. “Do you ever wonder,” he asked, his voice casual but sharp, “why Hunters hate The Prime Archon so much, yet do nothing about it?”

     Haldric scoffed. “They’re Hunters. They don’t care about anyone but themselves.”

     Note gave him a pointed look. “You really believe that?”

     Haldric hesitated.

     The question seemed to linger, scratching at something in the back of his mind. Did something happen with Bresdin? Praew wondered.

     “They can live above the surface,” Note continued. “They have power, strength, autonomy. So why stay here?”

     Praew crossed her arms. “They have families.”

     “Exactly.” Note turned to face them fully. “Stormhaven’s upper class.”

     Haldric stopped walking.

     Praew’s stomach clenched.



     Note’s usual grin faded. He placed a reassuring hand on Haldric’s shoulder and spoke in a surprisingly gentle voice. “Haldric, I know we got off on the wrong foot. But please, don’t let that be the reason you deny the truth about The Prime.”

  “Oh… and what truth is that?” Haldric demanded, his voice simmering with barely contained rage. He slapped Note’s hand away, refusing to be placated.

     Note withdrew his hand, along with any trace of empathy. “You may have replaced Arthur’s son. But not everyone gets to be the Heir Archon.”

      He took a breath.

  “Haven’t you ever wondered how one man keeps control over forty thousand people, and seventy active Hunters, each capable of wiping out a small army?

  “He holds their families hostage. I wasn’t lying earlier. The best hostages are the ones who don’t even realize they became one.

  “Every Hunter, every Special Forces member, every government official… they’re allowed to move their families to the First Floor. Lower crime. Better food. Nicer homes. More guards. More patrols. Who wouldn’t trade a little privacy for peace of mind?”

  “But that peace comes at a cost: constant surveillance and quiet obedience. It makes it impossible for even a group of Hunters to get their families to the surface without being executed first.”

     The flickering SolarStones above barely chased away the damp chill clinging to Praew’s skin.

     Haldric’s breathing quickened, his fists clenching and unclenching at his sides. Praew recognized that look. He was reaching his limit.

  Suddenly, he turned on Note. “You seem to know a lot for just a ‘worried citizen.’” His voice dripped with sarcasm and spite.

  He stepped forward. “You’re one of them, aren’t you?”

  Praew had come to the same conclusion some time ago.

  “A rebel,” Note said with a nod.

      Haldric spun and seized her arm. “Praew! We can be executed just for being seen with this guy! We need to leave, NOW!”

  Praew felt her eyes begin to water. 

  Note’s smirk vanished. He stepped forward, eyes blazing. “The greatest threat to her right now is you, Haldric!”

  Haldric let out a snort as he began to march her away. “Really? That’s the best you could come up with?” He wouldn’t even let her face the direction he was leading her.

  Praew watched as Note’s hand slid behind his back, fingers likely curling around a weapon. He didn’t draw it. He just held there, steady, as he spoke in a calmer voice.

  “Regular citizens can’t withstand the superior strength of an acclimated human. So how about you let her go?”

  “Enough!” Haldric yelled, his grip tightening.

  “Ow!” she hissed. His fingers bit into her skin with a monstrous grip. Like the jaws of a beast, ready to snap bone.

     Praew moved on instinct. She straightened her left arm against his grip, creating just enough slack to lean forward, shifting her weight onto her right foot. Then she drove her heel into the crook of his knee. His leg buckled. He reeled backward, off-balance. She planted her foot and pivoted sharply left. To build momentum, she twisted back, swung her free arm across her body, striking his throat.

     Haldric released her, stumbling back, coughing. His necklace slipped from beneath his shirt and struck the stone with a sharp clink.

     A key?

 Praew huffed, rubbing her wrist. Superior strength? Acclimated human? What did that even mean? Her mind reeled, but before she could speak—

  “Well blast me silly,” Note said under his breath. “You alright, Praew?”

  She nodded, still rubbing her wrist.

  They both looked at Haldric just as he straightened, a rasp in his throat. “I’m fine too,” he grumbled. “Thanks for asking.”

  “I may be part of the resistance, but does that make me wrong? The Prime must have records. Truth about the so-called food shortage, the Fourth Floor, or something that will open your eyes. See for yourself, Haldric. I’m not asking you to betray The Prime. I’m asking you to become a better one. When it’s your reign, only you will have the power to stop this.”

  Note’s eyes locked onto hers.

  “Thanks to your friend here, I’ll be leaving Stormhaven immediately. I would rather not give him the chance to have me executed.”

  “Good instincts,” Haldric sneered. “Though every time you speak, I’m more tempted to do it myself.”

  Note ignored him, gaze fixed on Praew. “If Wannii or Junya were captured, you’re in danger too. Come with me, Praew. Join the Song of Freedom.”

  Freedom? That’s all she’d ever wanted. To escape this dungeon. To go on adventures. To protect those too weak to lift a spear. Wasn’t that worth fighting for?

  “You can’t seriously be considering this, Praew!” Haldric snapped.

  “No, I—I can’t, Note,” she said hesitantly. “I can’t give up on my friends and family. Not when I have the power to help.”

  “Our loss,” Note said with a nod. “Follow the music if you change your mind.”

     Note crossed his arms, eyeing them both.

  “In Ravour, power is never created. It’s taken. So take heed, the more you claim, the more dangerous you become to those you may take it from.”

     Praew and Haldric turned to each other. And when they turned back, Note was gone.

  Vanished like a whisper in the dark. 

     “I believe it’s time you and I had a talk.” Her anger bled into every word.



     Haldric let out a slow breath through his nose, his fists clenched at his sides. “I couldn’t agree more.”

     She blinked. That wasn’t the reaction she expected. His voice was tight, seething, his stance rigid. Praew narrowed her eyes. “Wait, why are you angry?”

     Haldric stepped closer, there was something burning in his gaze.

     “You knew, didn’t you?” he asked, voice low and trembling with restrained fury.

     She frowned. “What?”

     “You knew that bolting Ratrod was a rebel, didn’t you!?” Haldric accused.

     Praew didn’t respond.

     “Stone you, Praew! I’m the bolting Heir Archon! What do you think would happen if I was caught talking with a rebel!? Not just you and I—our whole families would be executed!”

     The words cracked through the air like a whip.

     Praew inhaled sharply.

     “I only started to suspect when he spoke about a Fourth Floor, but I had to know!” Her frustration flared, matching his. “And what are you yelling at me for?! You’re the one keeping secrets!”

     Haldric’s eyes flashed. “What are you talking about?”

     “What’s this about an acclimated human and superior strength?” she snapped.

     Haldric’s lips parted like he was about to say something, but then—nothing. His throat bobbed.

     Praew folded her arms, waiting. Daring him to lie to her.

 “It’s none of your concern,” he finally muttered.

    Praew let out a bitter laugh. “Oh, really?” She held up her arm, the faint light catching on the swelling bruises already forming around her wrist. Courtesy of his unnatural strength. “My arm would disagree.”

     Haldric stiffened. His gaze flicked to her wrist, just for a second. Then he looked away.

     “What is going on with you!?” She took a step closer, forcing him to look at her. “You’re getting so aggressive. You defend The Prime Archon more than anyone. And now you’re able to crush my wrist like it’s a ration cracker?”

     She jabbed a finger at his chest. “Talk. Now.”

     Haldric’s gaze was cold. He didn’t answer immediately. Instead, he exhaled, slow and measured, before speaking. “There’s more going on than you could ever understand, Praew.”

     His voice wasn’t angry anymore. “You always think you’re so smart,” he continued, “So bolting sure of yourself. But look at what you just did.”

     She blinked. “What—?”

     “You just made your closest friend commit treason.”

     “And justice we will deliver,” Haldric said alone, looking even more disgusted with her. She hadn’t said the words.

     She’d stopped reciting that bolting chant every time she heard the word treason weeks ago. Though the guilt still lingered, she refused to show it. “I didn’t—”

     “You met with a rebel,” Haldric interrupted. “Talked with him. Listened to him!” He sneered. “If anyone saw us, we would be dead, Praew. And for what? Some conspiracy theories and a good story?”

     She felt herself blink rapidly as her eyes began to water. Not now Praew, she thought, compose yourself!

     “There’s a reason you’re in Special Forces and not Administration.”  The words dripped with something ugly. Something cruel. “You’re not as smart as you pretend to be.”

     Heat flared behind her eyes. She felt it building in her chest, a pressure that was too much, too sharp. Praew clenched her fists.

     She could have fought back. Could have told him how blind he was, how stupid he sounded. But she didn’t.

     Because she knew—deep down, she agreed with him.

     Instead, she turned on her heel and stormed off without another word. She didn’t look back. Not once. She headed toward the upper Floors. Away from him. Away from his arrogance.

     Because if she didn’t leave right now—

     She might stoop to his level and say something she couldn’t take back.

Praew walked home in silence, her thoughts still tangled from the fight with Haldric. The raw emotion had ebbed, but dried tears clung to her cheeks, leaving her face stiff and tight. 

     Only now did she register how unnaturally quiet the street was. This was her first time using her new clearance to bypass curfew. Shadows shifted across the walls, and the echo of her breathing made the corridor feel narrower than usual. 

     A faint breeze brushed past, likely the drift of a distant ZephyrStone triggered somewhere to circulate oxygen. The twentieth flash lit the path just as her home came into view.

     She slowed.

     Mai Azadi was waiting out front.

     Praew froze for a moment, then remembered—she hadn’t shown up for training. The guilt hit her instantly.

     “I’m sorry,” she said. “I forgot to tell you I had plans today. I didn’t mean to skip.”

     Mai didn’t ask what the plans were. She just looked at her carefully.

     “Are you okay?” she asked.

     Praew gave a weak nod. “I’m fine.”

     Mai’s eyes narrowed faintly, unconvinced. But she didn’t push.

     “When I’m worked up, it helps to blow off some steam,” Mai said. Then paused. “Come on. Let’s go train. It’s not too late.”

     Praew hesitated, but only for a moment. It was the middle of the night, but she didn’t have classes tomorrow. She could sleep in.

     She nodded. “Alright.”

    They walked in silence, cutting through the halls toward the empty classroom. From there, Mai led her through the hidden passage into the training chamber.

     The space no longer felt foreign.

     Praew had grown.

     She could now cross all four terrain paths, even water, without triggering a single stone. Those drills were behind her. Her feet knew the rhythm. Her breaths matched the stone.

     Now, she and Mai trained for real. No tricks. No tests. Just hand-to-hand combat.

     Tonight, they didn’t speak. No commands. No corrections. Just movement. Precision. A silent, unchoreographed dance between them. Each strike and each block carried by a rhythm that neither of them heard but both instinctively followed. There was no music, yet something deeper played beneath their feet, guiding each motion like the undertow of a song.

     Praew moved with more force than usual. More weight behind each swing. More heat in every advance. She needed the chance to burn through her frustration. She could feel it. And Mai could too.

     Mai broke the silence.

     “Do you want to talk about it?” she asked, breath steady.

     Praew hesitated, just long enough to defend Mai’s strike. Between movements, Praew told her everything. About Note. About the Fourth Floor. The disappearances. The Sifaralith identification program.

     Mai didn’t respond. Her eyes glazed over, staring through Praew, through the room, like she was watching something only she could see. Her movements flowed by habit alone.

     Praew’s elbow slipped past her guard and struck clean. A solid hit. Mai didn’t flinch.

     Praew hesitated, stunned by the opening, but her instinct took over. She stepped forward and raised her hand for another strike.

     This time, Mai caught her wrist.

     Her grip was firm. Her eyes locked on Praew’s.

     “Don’t go back there,” she said.

     She stepped in and grabbed both of Praew’s arms, holding her in place.

     “Promise me. Promise me you’ll never go back there!”

     Her grip was urgent, her voice shaken, and her eyes were—

     Zephyrs take me. Her eyes were terrified.

     “I knew it!” Praew exclaimed. “You know about the Fourth Floor!”

     Mai didn’t answer. Her body stiffened as she let go, eyes refusing to meet Praew’s.

     “You need to tell me everything,” Praew demanded.

     Mai still didn’t respond. Instead, she turned and took a few slow steps away from her.

     “I—” Her voice caught. “I can’t, Praew.”

     Praew stood in place, refusing to give up. She wasn’t angry, not anymore, but she needed to know what Mai knew.

     “I thought we were friends,” Praew said, softer now. “Friends don’t keep secrets from each other. Please… just tell me.”

     Mai stopped. She didn’t turn right away. Her hands hung loose at her sides, but Praew could see the slight tremble in her fingers.

     When she finally turned, her voice came quiet. Not fragile, but tired.

     “I was born on the Fourth Floor, Praew.”

     The words lingered in the air like dust refusing to settle.

     “My whole family worked in the Sifaralith Identification Program. My father died before I was born. I don’t even know what he looked like.”

     Mai stepped toward the far wall, eyes fixed on nothing.

     “My mom and my two older brothers did the dangerous work—testing reactions, moving volatile stones. I wasn’t allowed near the tables. I just carried stones to the correct bins when they called out the placement.”

     Praew frowned. “Wait… two older brothers? That means your family had three kids?”

     Mai nodded faintly. “Yeah, I think that’s why my family was sent there.”

     She hesitated.

     “I loved them. Even when they were cruel. My brothers blamed me for everything. Said I shouldn’t have been born. That my dad died because of me.”

     Her face didn’t crack, but something in her voice tightened.

     “I never understood it. But my mom, she never blamed me. She told me I was special. That I was hers.”

     Her gaze dropped.

     “One day, everything changed. My mom was handling a BlastStone and a BoltStone. A strong ZephyrStone triggered nearby. It triggered both stones in her hands.”

     Mai raised her own hands as if remembering, then slowly let them fall.

     “She lost all her fingers so the guards came. We knew what that meant. If you’re no longer useful, you’re removed. Disposed of. I’d seen it happen before.”

     “Yeah, me too,” Praew nodded.

     Mai paused, swallowing.

     “My brothers lost it. They fought back. Screaming, throwing tools. I tried to help, but a guard hit me—hard. I flew into a pile of unprocessed stones. I landed right next to the ZephyrStone that tore our lives apart.”

     She closed her eyes.

     “I blacked out. But just before everything went dark… I saw them. My mom. My brothers. All three of them being dragged away.”

     Her arms hung limp now. Her voice thinned. “I never saw them again. But when I woke in the same location, there was something next to me. A red…” She stopped.

     Praew stepped closer, gentler now. “A red what?”

     Mai didn’t answer. Her lips parted, then closed. She turned her head, chest rising and falling. “I’m sorry,” she whispered. “I… I really can’t say.”

     The silence held. Mai looked down at her hands again before continuing.

     “But I never forgot the three guards’ faces. Not one.”

     Her voice hardened.

     “When I was alone, I found a broken chunk of stone. I remembered something—something my brothers used to do to pass the time. They’d sharpen rocks with other stones. Said it kept their hands busy when they weren’t. So I did the same. I sharpened it. For days.”

     Mai smiled, looking up and locking eyes with Praew. Those eyes held a hatred Praew had never seen before.

     How can someone hide so much pain behind a smile? Instead of pity, Praew’s admiration for her new best friend only grew.

     “I waited. I watched. I was ready,” Mai continued. “Then the day finally came.”

     Praew could feel the weight behind every word.

     “The Prime Archon came down. Four guards flanked him. And standing in formation to greet him were the three men who destroyed my family.”

     Praew’s stomach turned.

     “They stood at attention. Clean uniforms. Eyes straight. Like nothing had happened.” Mai’s hands clenched slightly. “That’s when I struck.”

     She stared past Praew, not really seeing her.

     “I snuck up behind them. Quiet. So quiet. No one noticed. Not even The Prime. Not until it was too late.”

     “I drove the blade into the backs of their knees. All three. Dropped them screaming. And before they hit the ground, I slit their throats.”

     Praew’s breath caught.

     “The last guard tried to stop me. The Prime… he looked amused. He raised his hand, and the others stepped back. They didn’t intervene. They just watched.”

     Her jaw clenched.

   “The last one was bigger. Stronger. But I was faster. I stabbed through his foot, and when he stumbled—I went for the face.”

     She didn’t describe it. She didn’t need to.

     Mai looked at the floor again. This time, her voice came low and distant. “The Prime was impressed. He brought me up to the First Floor and enrolled me in Ghost Operations.”

     Mai looked away.

     “So I trained. I learned. And I survived.”

     Praew stood still. The pieces began to arrange themselves, clicking together in the quiet.

     “So, The Prime does know about the Fourth Floor,” she said. “I knew it. I have to tell Haldric and—”

     Mai’s eyes widened. “No,” she said quickly. “Wait. There’s… something else I need to tell you.”

     Praew paused, mid-step. “What is it?”

     Mai hesitated. She shifted her weight, looking down at her hands as if the words were buried in her palms.

     “This all happened a long time ago,” she said. “Longer than you’d think.”

     Praew tilted her head. “It must have been traumatic. I’m so sorry, Mai, but I—”

     “No. That’s not it.” Her voice rose sharply, cutting through. “Praew, please listen to me.”

     Praew nodded once. Quiet. Waiting.

     Mai didn’t look up right away.

     “I don’t even know if the current Prime knows about the Fourth Floor,” she said. “It was the previous Prime who discovered me.”

     Praew blinked. “That’s… not possible, Mai. Maybe he used a disguise.”

     Mai’s voice softened. “No, it really was the last Prime. Because of something that happened… I don’t age.”

     “What?” Praew asked, mind reeling.

     For a second, she thought she’d misheard. Even for someone like Mai, Praew wasn’t prepared for levity in this conversation.

     But if it was a joke, Mai didn’t show it. Her face remained serious as she shifted uncomfortably beneath Praew’s piercing stare.

     “I don’t get older,” Mai said again, insistent. “I’ve looked like this since the day he found me.”

     Mai took a deep breath and shut her eyes, balling both hands into fists before forcing out one last sentence.

     “The truth is, I’m probably even older than—”

     “That bolting bag of bones is even older than me,” growled a voice behind them.

     Mai froze, then looked up with a tight smile. “Well slap my back with a BlastStone, I’m getting rusty.”

     Praew turned slowly. Silverwing Godric stood in the doorway, arms crossed.

     “What is with you bolting hatchlings and your obsession with classified information? I thought you’d learn a lesson after what happened to the others, Praew.”

     He started walking toward them.

     Mai stepped in front of her, calm and composed.

     “Godric Rivers,” she said, “you’re quite light on your feet. I didn’t even hear a footstep.”

     Godric gave a short nod. “Thanks. Years of practice. Though… I guess not as many as you.”

     Then his tone cooled.

     “You shouldn’t have told her about the Fourth Floor. No matter what she is to you… treason is treason.”

     He stopped a few paces away.

     “It’s kind of sick, you know—imitating a hatchling to experience your missed youth.”

     His eyes flicked to Praew.

     “What is she? Your next victim?”

     Mai stared daggers at Godric as she unsheathed a voice sharp enough to cut the air.

     “That’s rich coming from you.”

     She tilted her head, locking eyes with him.

     “Have you already forgotten what we Ghosts used to call you before your Hunter title?”

     She let the silence drag.

     “Madman Godric… the Deadly Scientist.”

     Godric stepped right up to her. Close. Looming.

     His fists clenched.

     “I thought I made it clear what would happen to you Ghosts if you ever called me that again.”

     Mai smiled her usual carefree grin, but her eyes told a different story. They were wide, fierce, and beastly—like the Ratrod-eating child from the Fourth Floor.

     “I think it’s best if you leave, Praew,” she chimed through the teeth of her stiff smile.

     “No,” Praew said calmly, though her heart was racing as fast as her thoughts. “You’re my best friend, and I don’t care about your age. I’m sorry for breaking my promises. So come on! Stay alive and scold me like before!” She turned to Godric with a sneer.

     “You were one of my heroes, Godric.”

     “I’m no bolting hero,” he growled back, eyes locked on Mai. “But I’ll do what I must. Go home, Praew. I’ll deal with you later.”

     Praew slowly backed toward the door. “If anything happens to me, Haldric will find you, Godric. Not even you can ignore the Heir!”

     Godric turned toward her, fury breaking through. “Do you really think that I—”

     Praew didn’t hear the rest, because Godric made a deadly mistake: he took his eyes off the best bolting assassin in the city.

     Mai dropped to a knee and slammed a concealed dagger through his foot.

     The monster didn’t even flinch. He just looked down at her.

     “Praew, run!” Mai shouted.

     Godric moved effortlessly. A casual kick launched Mai across the training hall like a child’s toy sphere.

     Praew ran. The hall blurred as the first flash signaled the start of a new day.

     You better be okay, Mai. You’re the best assassin. You must know how to escape that beast. You understood my hidden message, right? I’ll see you there.

     A plan? Yeah… she had a plan. Or part of one. She’d meet Mai on the Fourth Floor and ambush Godric together. But first, she’d need weapons. She’d need time.

     Haldric will expose Godric and clear our names… right? He has to.

     Godric’s echoing voice chased her down the hall, catching her the only way he still could—by seizing her attention with subtext that completely escaped her.

     “Drop the act, Zephyr’s Blade. Your prey already escaped. What’s the point in hiding what you really are now?”

     Zephyrs take me, what did he even mean by that?

     Pebbles began to scatter across the stone near the battle, clicking louder with each heartbeat. A low howl rose, and before she could look back, a sudden gust blasted Praew off her feet. The wind hurled her down the hall until she slammed face-first into the wall at the next bend.

     What in the bolting seven stars? That’s not what I meant when I said ‘Zephyrs take me.’ What kind of ZephyrStone can even do that!?

     Shaking off the daze, she pushed herself up and ran toward Haldric’s home. His family wouldn’t appreciate her barging in so late, but apologies would have to wait.

Sprinting without a breath to spare, Haldric’s home finally came into view. Just as Praew began to slow her pace, a shadow cut in front of her.

“Wait, how did you—“

The second flicker ended, returning the street to a stillness that matched the silence. Not the kind of silence that invited peace, but the still, stone-deep hush that came with curfew. No voices or footfalls. Just the faint, distant dripping of stalactites hard at work to form stalagmites throughout the street. Its perseverance seemed to echo throughout the city, only to be sabotaged by the morning commute of hard-working citizens.

     Haldric paced in front of the administration building.

     What came over me? he thought. Why am I so impulsive? I could be in bed by now. What did I expect to accomplish by coming here instead? That bolting woman is making me lose my mind!

     He shouldn’t have cared this much. Shouldn’t have let that bolting conversation get under his skin. But the words wouldn’t leave. 

“You’re not as smart as you pretend to be.” His words to Praew words rang in his head.

     Praew was reckless. Always had been. But she wasn’t stupid.

     And Note. That smug bastard. The way he spoke, the way he looked at Haldric like he was naïve. Like he was blind. Like he didn’t understand how his own city worked.

     Haldric gritted his teeth and shoved the thought aside. He did understand. The Prime Archon had given him this position because he trusted him. Because he deserved it.

     So why couldn’t he stop thinking about the Fourth Floor?

     The bruises. The whip scars. The way those workers didn’t even scream when they were punished, like they had accepted it. Like it wasn’t new.

     That kid. Gnawing on a Ratrod like some starving animal.

     His stomach twisted. He wasn’t like Praew. He didn’t jump to conclusions. He needed proof that The Prime knew.

     If Note was lying, then fine, he’d throw it back in his face. But if he wasn’t…

     Haldric’s hands curled into fists. There was only one way to find out.

     And that meant breaking into The Prime Archon’s office.

     It wasn’t treason. 

     You’re proving them wrong, he told himself, shoving aside the unease curling in his chest. You’re proving you were right to trust him. And that he was, to trust you.

     Then why did it feel like he was about to cross a line he couldn’t uncross?

     His footsteps scraped softly against the carved stone floor as he walked toward the monolithic entrance. The building itself bled straight out of the cavern wall behind it, a symbol of smooth, chiseled order. The stone face rose two stories high, cut with sharp lines and recessed archways, everything symmetrical, exact. No guards stood at the gate. None were needed.

     The Prime’s key hung heavy on Haldric’s neck. He shifted the small book in his pocket so it would stop digging into his thigh.

     He could feel the objects’ discontent. Every step closer to the door made their presence feel unbearable. It was more than a book or key. It was a symbol of trust. The Prime’s trust. The city’s trust. And here he was, unable to give them the same.

     He stopped and ran a hand through his hair. No, he wasn’t breaking in. That’s what Note wanted him to think. That he was sneaking around, chasing conspiracies, doubting the one person who’d believed in him from the start. But that wasn’t it. That wasn’t it.

     He looked up at the sealed door. It was a massive slab, locked with a complex rotating seal embedded into the stone. It was a safe door. Every home held a miniature one that contained a copy of the family’s identification documents. No one could unlock this giant mechanism without the proper key. But his master key could open every lock in the city.

     This wasn’t betrayal. It was loyalty.

     Right?

     The second flash triggered, and Haldric’s breath caught as realization struck. Time was slipping through his fingers faster than he had anticipated. He knew that by the fifth flash, early risers would start filtering into the building, and The Prime had once confided that he never set foot inside before the fourth. That gave Haldric a narrow window. Two flashes at most to uncover the truth and vanish before anyone else arrived.

     Haldric took a breath. Pulled the key off his neck. He hesitated only once, then pressed it into the stone.

     The lock turned.

     The hall swallowed him in shadow.

     Haldric stepped inside, the heavy door sealing behind him with a dull grind. No light greeted him, just rows of benches stretching into dark silence. The benches were bare. The chambers, empty. Only the long stone aisle stretched ahead. This was where he’d normally worked, where others gathered for meetings, hearings, and reports. But now it felt hollow, like the air itself was waiting to catch him in the act.

     His footsteps echoed louder than they should have.

     As he neared the far end of the hall, a faint sliver of light cut across the stone floor. He froze. The Prime’s office door was shut, but underneath, a warm glow spilled from within. SolarStones. They were always triggered in The Prime’s chamber, whether occupied or not. But was he there? Haldric couldn’t tell.

     He moved closer, his heartbeat in his throat. The glow didn’t flicker. No shadows moved across the light. Still, he hesitated. Hand hovering over the latch. If The Prime was inside…

     He turned the handle.

     The office was empty.

     He exhaled sharply, the tension pouring from his shoulders as he stepped in and shut the door behind him.

     Light bathed the room in a soft, golden warmth. The Prime’s office was as he remembered, orderly, immense, carved into layered alcoves and shelves, papers neatly stacked, quills lined like weapons across the desk. It felt sacred. Untouchable.

     But he was here now. And there was no turning back.

     He took a steadying breath.

     Let’s begin, he thought. But still, he stood there.

     His hand twitched at his side.

     The light from the SolarStones glinted softly off the polished stone of the desk. The same desk he’d sat beside so many times. The same walls that had once made him feel respected and needed.

     What am I doing? He glanced at the door again. I shouldn’t be here. He treats me like his own. Gave me this key, this position…

     This is wrong but… if I’m going to be Heir, I have to lead. That means facing the truth, even if it hurts.

     He stepped toward the desk and reached for the first drawer.

Drawer by drawer. Sheet by sheet. Neatly stacked papers filled with daily notes, reports, and appointments. Training logs. Ledger summaries. Transport slips. He found his own name a few times, mostly evaluations, nothing unusual. Everything felt above board. Too above board.

     He moved to the shelves, scanning the spines of bound records. Storage rotations. Personnel files. Surface reports. Still, nothing stood out.

     The place was clean. Spotless. Like someone had swept the truth away before it could settle.

     He frowned. Stepped back.

     The light flickered once overhead.

     He glanced toward the hall. Still no footsteps yet.

     How many was that now? Five? Ten? Every flicker dragged him closer to exposure. But he couldn’t leave yet.

     There had to be more. Something hidden like the murals he’d seen.

     His eyes swept the bookshelves, stone-carved edges, orderly stacks, nothing out of place. It all looked clean. Neutral. But at the base of the lowest shelf, something interrupted the symmetry. A shallow groove, almost hidden in shadow.

     He crouched. Pressed his fingers against the unnatural line.

     The third flash triggered.

     Down to the last flash already?

     He broke out into a cold sweat.

     He traced along the edge of the slit, feeling for any give in the stone. A block of stone gave slightly. It shifted with a soft grating click. The shelf creaked, then pushed inward.

     A hidden panel.

     He exhaled and pulled it open.

     Inside, carefully stacked documents filled a shallow recess. Each bundle bound in string, labels written in firm, practiced hand. These weren’t discarded reports. They were current. Maintained.

     He lifted the top file and unfolded the first page.

     BoltStone Recorrection Program.

     The heading alone made his chest pull tight.

     Below it, a sketched outline of a human spine, annotated with clean lines and technical marks. A BoltStone embedded at the base of the neck. Margins filled with calm, efficient handwriting:

     “Sustained exposure reinforces impressionable behavior. Recommended in high-risk subjects.”

     “Scarring minimal. Long-term effects still in testing.”

     “Early-stage results promising. Further trials required.” —Godric Rivers

     He stared at the words. The paper barely crinkled under his grip, but his knuckles had gone pale.

     What was this?

     He set it aside. Pulled the next stack into the light.

     Breeding Ledgers.

     His brow drew low. That wasn’t a term he recognized from any official archive.

     He opened it.

     Inside, dense columns of tracking data. Hunter assignments. Population statistics. Breeding yards. Slaughter rotations. And then a final column—one that made his throat tighten.

     Meat distribution orders.

     Quantities requested. Approved. Then lines drawn through them. Decreased. Cut down.

     They weren’t just tracking the beasts. They were raising them.

     And The Prime Archon was holding back the food.

     He—

     Before he could think, a soft creak. A presence behind him. Haldric’s head snapped straight toward the door, but no one stood in the hallway.

     The Prime Archon walked out of a secret door directly behind Haldric. Intent on a stack of papers in his hands, it took The Prime a moment to register Haldric’s presence.

     Startled, The Prime stopped in place, turning around toward Haldric slowly. A brief flicker of surprise tightened his brow before the mask fell back into place. His hands clasped behind his back, rolling the papers he held into a tight cylinder. His gaze swept over the open drawers, the displaced books, the hidden compartment left ajar.

     His expression sank.

     Not into anger. But into disappointment. A heavy, quiet kind of disappointment. The kind that didn’t need raised voices or fury. The kind that sat deep in the chest, unsaid, unspoken, yet unmistakable.

     Haldric felt it like a blade to the gut.

     The Prime exhaled, slow.

     “Did you find what you were looking for?”

     Jaw agape, Haldric’s gaze flicked to the hidden door, to The Prime, then the papers in his own hands. No excuse would fix this.

     He had been caught.

     “I… I’m sorry,” Haldric stammered.

     The Prime’s grip tightened around the rolled papers. He sighed.

     “No. It’s my fault.” The Prime’s voice was steady, but there was something heavier beneath it. “My fault for believing in you.”

     Haldric clenched his jaw.

     The words struck harder than they should have. He wasn’t sure why.

     The Prime stepped forward, gripped the edge of the hidden passageway, and slammed it shut. Stone grinded against stone, sealing away whatever lay beyond.

     Haldric barely resisted the urge to step back.

     The Prime exhaled sharply.

     “Perhaps… if you’re satisfied,” his voice was calm, but patience thinned with each word, “you should put back the highly classified documents you just pilfered?”

     Haldric’s fingers twitched. Pilfered? The word lodged itself in his skull. Like he was some petty thief caught red-handed. Still, he forced his hands to move, placing the documents back where he found them, each motion slow and careful.

     The Prime watched. Didn’t move. Didn’t speak.

     A flicker later, he sighed. “I’m not mad, Haldric. I’m just disappointed.”

     Haldric clenched his jaw. That felt worse.

     “The whole city blames me for everything,” The Prime continued, his voice quiet but edged. “When food runs low, when water rations shrink, when a Hunter dies above ground, it’s always my fault. The people need someone to blame. And I accept that burden.”

     His gaze flicked toward Haldric, sharper now.

     “But I thought you, of all people, would be different. That you would look past the stories and rumors to see the real me.”

     Haldric’s throat tightened. A sharp, unsteady breath. “I—”

     The Prime didn’t stop. “To learn that even you think I’m a bad person?” A quiet scoff. “That’s frustrating.”

     Haldric’s stomach twisted. Because he had believed in The Prime. Still wanted to. But—

     “You are holding back food.”

     The words came before he could stop them. His hands curled into fists. “You’re giving more to the upper class and using people’s hunger to maintain control over everyone.”

     The Prime stilled. “Am I?”

     The words were measured and indifferent. But there was something there, something he couldn’t quite place.

      Haldric hesitated.

     The Prime didn’t wait for an answer. He turned toward the office door. “Come.”

     Haldric blinked.

     “What?”

     “You wanted answers.”

     The Prime didn’t glance back. “Walk with me.”

     Haldric hesitated. A test? A trap? But what choice did he have? He stepped forward, following.

     The doors shut behind them.

     And for the first time ever, The Prime instilled fear into Haldric.

     Fear for his life.
