Chapter 2 - Acclimation



The hall was silent.

No more tests. No more proving themselves. The choices had been made.

Praew sat stiffly on the stone bench, surrounded by the weight of what had happened. Haldric was deathly quiet beside her, jaw clenched so tight she could hear his teeth grind. Asher, a few steps away, hadn’t spoken a word since the trial ended.

The Hunter stepped forward, holding a roll of parchment in one hand. The gathered hopefuls—those who had passed and those who had failed—held their breaths as they watched him.

“This is the final stage of the selection process,” the Hunter announced, his voice carrying across the stone chamber. “For those of you who failed, your paths do not end here. The city still has use for you. You will be assigned a labor role according to your strengths or the current needs of Stormhaven.”

A murmur of unease rippled through the crowd. This was, singlehandedly, the most important moment of their lives. It determined their labor role until retirement, their lodging, their clothing—even how much food they’d get each day. 

Food had always been scarce, but in recent years? If a Ratrod slipped into an upper-class stew, they’d probably dance to the nearest temple and thank Zephyrs for the blessing. The shortage had become nearly unbearable. And while students received the largest rations in the city, The Prime administration forbade them from sharing any of it with their families.

The Hunter’s eyes swept across them. Then, without further delay, he began.

“Haldric.”

Haldric straightened, shoulders rolling back as if bracing for impact.

“You are assigned to government administration in The Prime Archon’s office.”

A flicker of something—hope? Relief?—crossed his face. At least he wouldn’t be shoveling waste like some of the others who failed. But Praew could still see the tension in his shoulders. He wasn’t happy.

“Praew.”

Her stomach twisted.

The Hunter studied the parchment, then her. “You have been assigned to enforcement under the city’s constable Special Forces”

A hush fell over the room. Special Forces? That was no small position.

Praew swallowed hard. That was… unexpected. She had braced herself for something far worse, something lowly. But the way the Hunter looked at her, the way his gaze lingered just a moment too long—it unsettled her.

She didn’t thank him. Just nodded and sat back down, heart pounding.

He continued. Edrik and Korven were sent to labor assignments: one to the mines, the other to forge work. They both cried, begging for a reconsideration.

Then, only two remained.

The Hunter took a slow breath before speaking. “Two of you passed the trial.”

Renwick sat back, smug, hands folded behind his head. Asher remained stiff, staring at the floor.

The Hunter’s gaze settled on them. “You are no longer civilians. From this day forth, you are Hunters.”

Praew felt Haldric’s body lock beside her, felt a blazing hatred radiating from him like heat from a fire.

The Hunter continued.

“Renwick. You will be a Stone Hunter.”

A proud smirk stretched across his face. He leaned back as if he already knew. Of course he did. Renwick had been aiming for this since day one—hunting and extracting Noble Stones, working closely with the city’s elite.

Then, the final name.

The one that changed everything.

The Hunter straightened, rolling the parchment tight in his hands.

“Asher.”

Asher didn’t look up.

“You will be a Beast Hunter.”

The hall gasped.

For a second, the entire room was frozen.

A Beast Hunter.

Not just any Hunter. The most prestigious, the most feared, role any Hunter could obtain aside from a Mythical Beast Hunter like Srevlis.

Praew’s breath caught in her throat.

She knew what would happen next. .

Haldric’s fists clenched so tightly his knuckles turned white. His chest rose and fell in sharp, shaking breaths. And then—a low, guttural growl rumbled from his throat.

“That should have been me,” he whispered.

Praew turned to him, startled by the sheer hatred in his voice. “We’re going to be okay, Haldric. We were not assigned poor jobs. This may be the end of our hunting story, but realistically, we should be proud that we made it this far.”

Haldric was shaking. He wasn’t looking at Asher. He was staring through him.﻿﻿

Asher, to his credit, didn’t move. He didn’t acknowledge the gasps, the murmurs, the stunned expressions. His shoulders squared, his chin lifted slightly, but his hands were trembling at his sides.

The Hunter continued, “Beast Hunters are our first and last line of defense. You will leave the city, track down threats before they reach our walls, and ensure our survival.”

Then, his tone softened. He wasn’t talking down anymore; he began speaking as though to a friend.

“Brother Asher, Brother Renwick—my name is Hunter Steel. You have the choice to change your names so as to not implicate your families when you’re above surface. Hunters can be petty. If they learn your name and the city you hail from, they may bribe someone from your own city to harm your family. You can discuss this further with your team lead. But first…”

He gestured toward the crowd. “Say your goodbyes. You may not come back alive.”

Silence.

Praew turned to Asher.

He hesitated.

For the first time since the test, Asher looked at them. First at Haldric, still sitting rigid, shaking with fury. Then at her. 

Why is he looking at me like that? As if I hurt him. I’m not the one who broke their promise and betrayed their best friend.

She stared back, then nodded toward Haldric. She was staying out of their drama, but that was low. How could she trust a guy like that?

It was the same thing she told Hal and Asher years ago: If either of you ever get a girlfriend and cheat, we can’t be friends anymore. If someone can betray the person they love the most, what would stop them from betraying their friends?

Asher’s lips parted.

Zephyrs take you, this better be a bolting good apology, Praew thought.

But no, he looked away instead.

Asher’s hands curled into fists. Then, without a word, that bolting Ratrod literally turned his back on them and walked away. No apology or goodbye. The coward didn’t even glance back.

Renwick, on the other hand, grinned.

“Hey, Praew!” he called out. “Looks like I was half right, ehh? Don’t look so heartbroken. I’ll always come back for you.” Renwick flashed her a wink. “You can keep my bed warm until I do.”

Praew barely resisted the urge to throw something at his face. Haldric, however, didn’t hold back. His hands slammed against the bench, his voice a raw snarl. “You—”

Renwick only laughed as he turned and strutted toward the Hunter, joining Asher at the threshold.

The doors slammed shut behind them.

Praew sat there, staring at the empty space where Asher had stood. Haldric’s breath came ragged beside her.

No one spoke. The silence was deafening.



***



The corridor was dark, damp with the scent of stone and metal. Asher stepped forward, his boots echoing sharply against the stone.

     Renwick whistled beside him, hands laced behind his head like he didn’t have a care in the world.

“You sure it’s okay to leave like that? Not even a word to Praew?”

     “Why do you care?” Asher snapped. “Who are you to lecture me? You belittle and harass her constantly. She’s just trying her best. Don’t pretend you care now.”

     Renwick looked down in embarrassment—no, mocked embarrassment. A wide grin crept across his face.

“Lecture you?” He let out a chuckle, nudging Asher’s arm. “I’m just impressed. Never knew you had it in you.”

Asher clenched his jaw, ignoring him. Then, just ahead, someone shifted in the dim light.

“Asher,” a voice called out. It stopped them. Just for a breath.

The Prime Archon stood in their path, hands clasped behind his back, expression as stoic as ever. He was tall and narrow-framed, with neatly combed sandy blond hair streaked faintly with silver. His face was carved with patience and precision—high cheekbones, a blade-straight nose, and unreadable eyes that gave nothing away. He looked like a man built for stillness, each movement measured, each word sharpened before use.

     “Son, before you go, I just want to say—” The Prime trailed off as Asher walked right past him, then stopped.

     “You should know better than to call me that in public, Prime Archon. I had wondered if you would still try to sabotage me, or if what you said last night was true—that you had finally given up. But lo and behold… who else in this Zephyrs-taken city could twist people, rules, and words that masterfully but you?

   “Pitting me against my best friend?” Asher chuckled softly, fists clenched. “You’re not as clever as you think you are, if you thought that would stop me. After all, a Hunter mustn’t hesitate.” Asher resumed walking.

     The Prime Archon sputtered, as if to continue, but the words never came. Behind him, Asher heard Renwick greet The Prime.

     “Prime Archon. It’s an honor, sir.”

The Prime didn’t respond, likely still caught off guard. Not to be discouraged, Renwick continued:

“Must be difficult, maintaining the city’s approval. But who knows what the air may bring. Perhaps I’ll return with a prime contribution.”

Asher stopped in place. Wait, what?

  “Young man, what—was that you just said?” The Prime said sharply, his voice low and dangerous.

“Hmm? I won’t let you down,” Renwick said smoothly.

Then, without another glance, he turned and jogged to catch up to Asher.

“Damn,” Renwick drawled, falling in step beside him. “And you said I—strive to be the most hated? Guess it’s true what they say.”

Asher stopped in place, staring daggers at him. “And what’s that?”

Renwick just smiled, like he’d been hoping Asher would ask.

“That all Rivers run cold.”

Asher gritted his teeth.

That bolting Ratrod. Though… he’s right, isn’t he? Asher’s fists clenched at his sides. I’m sorry, Haldric. Praew.

But he stayed quiet. Letting Renwick rile him up would only prove him right.

Renwick only laughed. “Oh, this is gonna be fun.”

Asher followed in silence, breath steady, fists tight. The sound of their boots echoed against the stone as Hunter Steel led them through the heart of the city. 



***



     The path was a familiar one—one that every citizen had passed a hundred times but never truly seen. Two guards stood at the end of the corridor, their spears crossed over the entrance to a reinforced gate.

     The only passage to the surface. No one was ever allowed through. The moment the guards saw them approaching, their stance shifted. Their expressions hardened—not with suspicion, but with awe.

     Hunter Steel’s voice rang with quiet authority. “Hunter Steel, Apprentice Hunter Asher, and Apprentice Hunter Renwick requesting surface gate access.”

The air felt thick.

The guards hesitated for only a breath before snapping to attention. In near-perfect unison, they withdrew their spears and stepped aside.

The lock unlatched.

The heavy mechanisms groaned as the doors slowly swung open.

Renwick, for once, was silent.

Asher barely heard his own footsteps as he stepped forward, his heartbeat roaring in his ears. Every eye in the city would be on his back if they could see him now. The weight of it pressed against his shoulders.

And then—

The door fully opened.

Asher stepped through and the door shut behind them.

     The tunnel stretched ahead, splitting into two paths. To the left, a downward spiral vanished into endless darkness. But their path led upward—a steady, sloping incline carved into the stone. It was wide, yet somehow felt suffocating, the walls pressing inward the higher they climbed. No railings, no markers—just a path of smooth, worn stone leading into the unknown.

Asher and Renwick followed in silence, their boots scuffing against the floor. The only sound was the faint, steady rhythm of their steps and the occasional soft drip of water from somewhere unseen. The higher they went, the thicker the air became, charged with something unseen—something humming just beyond reach.

Asher rolled his shoulders, trying to shake the unease creeping into his bones.

The incline was gradual, but each step forward felt heavier than the last.

He exhaled, only to realize his breath came out shakier than before.

Renwick was quiet. Too quiet.

Asher flicked a glance at him.

The usual smirk was gone. The cocky swagger, absent. His shoulders were rigid, his eyes darting to the walls, the floor, then up ahead where the tunnel curved just out of sight.

For once, the bastard looked just as nervous as Asher felt.

The further they went, the more the air changed. It wasn’t something he could see—it was something he could feel.

The air moved. Not like the stale, recycled drafts of the underground, but something alive. It curled around him, slipping past his collar, gliding over his skin like unseen fingers tracing every inch of exposed flesh. It wasn’t harsh—it was gentle, almost soothing, yet it sent a shiver down his spine. It touched his face, his arms, his chest, an invisible tide washing over him, and for the first time in his life. It was eerie and intimate, all at once. Asher felt violated. He clenched his fists, resisting the urge to shudder. He had imagined his feet first touching the surface, but he had never imagined it would touch him first.

A strange, tingling sensation crept up Asher’s spine, buzzing at the back of his skull. He flexed his fingers, then frowned.

His skin prickled.

The hair on his arms stood on end.

He stopped mid-step, watching as his own forearm bristled—every fine strand of hair rising like needles, as if pulled by some invisible force.

A few paces ahead, Renwick cursed under his breath.

Asher turned to him.

Renwick’s arms were the same. Every strand of hair spiked upward, standing in jagged, unnatural rows along his forearm. His blonde hair, normally messy, now seemed to lift slightly at the edges, the static charge pulling at the very air around him.

“What in the ten stonesl…?” Renwick muttered, running a hand through his hair only for it to frizz back into place.

Asher swallowed hard, rubbing his arms, but the sensation didn’t fade.

The world felt wrong.

A pressure weighed against his chest—not enough to choke him, but enough to make breathing feel unnatural. He wasn’t sure if it was the incline or the sheer weight of what lay ahead, but his heart was hammering harder than it should.

He looked back at Renwick, who met his gaze.

Neither of them spoke.

They didn’t need to.

They were standing on the edge of something immense. Something terrifying.

And they knew it.

Asher clenched his fists, pressing his nails into his palms to ground himself.

Was Steel smirking ahead? That bastard was enjoying this!

They kept walking.

Each step felt heavier, like trudging through water. The incline curved again, drawing them closer, closer to whatever lay at the end of this path. The dim SolarStones embedded in the walls flickered, their glow paling against the overwhelming pull of the world above.

They passed another massive set of doors, their heavy iron frames groaning as they swung open. The moment the seals broke, a powerful gust of air blasted through the passage, slamming into them like a living force.

The moment the fresh air hit their lungs, both Asher and Renwick doubled over, coughing violently. It was as if they had taken a breath after nearly drowning—too much, too sudden, too real. Their chests heaved, their bodies instinctively rejecting the unfamiliar sensation. The air was richer, heavier, carrying a depth they had never known underground.

Hunter Steel stood unfazed, watching them with quiet amusement. “The air above is cleaner, thicker,” he said. “You’ll get used to it. Your lungs will stretch, expand. More air means more stamina. More stamina means more strength. That’s why Hunters are stronger than civilians.”

Asher forced himself to steady his breathing, although his lungs still burned. He exchanged a look with Renwick, whose usual smirk was nowhere to be found. For the first time since they’d stepped onto this path, the weight of what it meant to be here truly settled in.

Renwick clutched his knees, shaking his head.

Seeing that jerk suffer made Asher feel the whole experience was worth it.

Asher glanced at him, then back to the path ahead. This was only the beginning.

A strange mix of fear and exhilaration twisted in Asher’s gut.

He’d spent his whole life underground. Imagining the surface. Dreaming of it.

“This is the last gate! Prepare yourselves!” Steel called out.

He was finally here!

One last door

One final step.

His heart slammed against his ribs.

The doors crept open.

Then—he saw it.

He saw—saw—

The beautiful… bright light?

No. Not beautiful. NOT AT ALL.

BLINDING. ALL-CONSUMING. UNRELENTING.

A searing, golden-white radiance consumed everything, swallowing the world in an unbearable glare. Asher flinched, squeezing his eyes shut as pain lanced through his skull. Tears welled instantly, stinging as they streaked down his face. His hands shot up to shield his eyes, but it was useless—the light pierced through his lids, burned into his vision. Why didn’t anyone warn us?

Somewhere beside him, Renwick let out a strangled curse. “No one ever said it would be like this!” he shouted, voice panicked.”

Asher’s breath came fast, disoriented. “I—I can’t see! What’s happening!?”

Hunter Steel exhaled, unfazed. “It’s just the sun,” he said simply. “You’ll adjust.”

Adjust? Asher fought against the sharp throbbing behind his eyes. He had imagined this moment a thousand times—stepping onto the surface, seeing the endless skies, the vast lands beyond the city’s walls. But not this. Not blindness.

They were led forward, but they couldn’t see where. Their steps were unsteady, guided only by the firm grip of the Hunter pulling them along. Time stretched. The burning never stopped. Time lost all meaning. Flickers, Flashes, what was the difference? The burning in his eyes had dulled to something worse, a deep, pulsing ache that throbbed in sync with his skull. The world was still nothing but white, endless and merciless. No edges. No depth. No escape. He squeezed his eyes shut, but even that didn’t help. The afterimage was seared into his mind, haunting the darkness behind his lids.

Renwick yelled a string of curses beside him. “How are we supposed to bolting hunt if we’re blind!?”

No answer. Steel let them suffer. No reassurances —just silence. He wanted them to sit with it. To learn.

Asher exhaled sharply, his breath ragged. He could feel it now, the change creeping in at the edges. The air wasn’t as heavy as before. Cooler, maybe. His skin, which had prickled under the heat, now felt…normal? Had the temperature dropped? He frowned, trying to piece it together. Shadows—there had to be shadows now. Or maybe his mind was playing tricks on him, grasping for something, anything, that wasn’t the overwhelming brightness.

How long had it been? A whole day? More? He didn’t know. His body ached from standing, from waiting. From the sheer exhaustion of it all. He felt hollow. Heavy. Miserable.

Night fell, and with it, his vision returned—sort of.

The burning light faded, replaced by a deep, endless dark. For the first time since stepping onto the surface, Asher could make out shapes, vague and formless. The shadows of trees swayed in the breeze. The rocky terrain stretched in uneven ridges, disappearing into the distance. But beyond that? Nothing.

No sprawling landscapes. No endless sky. Just a void of ink-black emptiness.

He let out a slow breath. He could see again… but there was nothing to see.

Hunter Steel led them back inside the gates—thick, reinforced walls that loomed behind them, standing as the last barrier between the city and whatever lay beyond. It was still the surface, but it wasn’t the wild. Not yet.

A crude shelter had been carved into the stone, a resting space for newly inducted Hunters. Asher was too exhausted to argue. He sank onto the makeshift bed, muscles aching, mind heavy with the weight of the day. Renwick muttered something under his breath before rolling onto his side, already half-asleep.

The absence of silence felt unnatural—distant rumbling, nearby booms, and the eerie cries of beasts too close for comfort. Asher closed his eyes and forced himself to sleep.

When morning came, it wasn’t the harsh, blinding white of before.

Asher stirred, groggy, noticing first how much easier it was to breathe. His chest didn’t feel as tight when his lungs expanded fully.

The light was bearable now, filtering dimly through the thick gates. He sat up slowly, blinking against the golden hues spilling through the cracks.

Asher frowned, scanning the room. No sign of Renwick. No sign of anyone.

Except—

A figure leaned against the far wall, arms crossed, golden eyes glinting in the dim morning light. His spiky gray hair stood in wild, jagged tufts, like a storm permanently frozen in place. His armor—jet black scales, Tarnox hide, maybe?—reflected faint glimmers of light. A Hunter’s grin stretched across his face—sharp, knowing, amused.

Asher’s pulse quickened. He didn’t recognize the man.

“Where’s Renwick? And who are you bolting supposed to be?” The words slipped out before he could stop them.

     The stranger’s grin widened. “The name’s Azel. If you’re asking about that kid that was with you, I’m sure he already left with his team lead. But is that any way to speak to your team lead?”

Asher stiffened like he’d been struck. His stomach lurched.

“N-No, sir! Sorry, sir!”

Azel pushed off the wall, stretching his arms behind his head with a slow, deliberate roll of his shoulders—relaxed, but watching him like a wolf sizing up prey.

“You sleep well, kid?” His voice was smooth, casual.

Asher swallowed. “U-Um… yes, sir?”

Azel tilted his head, like he was giving it some serious thought.

“That’s good. I was worried you had trouble falling asleep. You seemed pretty exhausted… sleeping past the tenth flash. I figured maybe I should let you sleep in a little more, then let you find your own way to my base.”

 Asher’s stomach dropped. “No, sir!”

Azel clapped his hands together, the sharp sound like a BlastStone in the empty room.

“Then perhaps—if it’s convenient for you—you might want to GET OUT OF BED AND GET READY TO LEAVE!”

     Asher practically threw himself out of bed. “Y-Yes, sir!” He scrambled to pull on his boots, nearly tripping over himself as he stood. His heart pounded. He wasn’t sure if he was more afraid of Azel or of being left behind.

     Azel just chuckled, watching him with that bolting grin.

     “Have your eyes adjusted? Can’t have you screaming and crying again—” Azel put on an exaggerated, mocking voice. “I—I can’t see! What’s happening?—like a baby bird.”

     Asher’s face burned. “I—I didn’t—you saw that?”

     Azel smirked. “Don’t worry, kid. A lot of Hunters did. It’s tradition: hazing the greenhorns. You’re not the first, and you won’t be the last.”

     Then the grin faded.

     “But out there?” Azel’s voice turned hard, flat. “You won’t get the luxury of panicking.”

     His eyes locked onto Asher’s,

     “Out there, hesitation gets you killed.”

     The weight of those words sank into Asher’s chest like a stone.

     Azel took a step back, jerking his head toward the door. “Get dressed. We move now.”

“OPEN THE GATE!” Azel barked, his voice carrying over the grinding metal.

Asher stood rigid beside him, heart pounding as the final gate loomed ahead. The iron behemoth towered before them—thick, reinforced, lined with NullStone to keep the outside world at bay. Every groan of the gears sent a fresh spike of tension through his chest.

Azel turned to him, his golden eyes hard as steel.

“This is your first time on the surface.” His voice was gravel—rough, final. “You’ve heard stories. You’ve seen glimpses. You might think you understand it.”

Asher nodded stiffly.

Azel took a step closer. “You don’t.”

Asher’s breath caught.

“The surface isn’t the city. It’s not safe. It’s not predictable.” Azel’s voice was calm—too calm. “Every step is a risk. Every turn could kill you. And the second you stop paying attention, you die.”

Asher swallowed hard.

Without warning, Azel jabbed a finger against his chest—hard—forcing him to take a step back.

“You will do exactly as I say, when I say it, how I say it.” His tone left no room for argument. “If I tell you to stop, you stop. If I tell you to move, you move. If I tell you to drop, you drop. No hesitation. No questions. Do you understand me, son?”

Asher clenched his jaw. “Yes, sir.”

Azel held his gaze for a long moment, then gave a single nod.

“Good.”
