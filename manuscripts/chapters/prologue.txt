Prologue



The wind howled through the jagged cliffs around Stormhaven’s gates, slicing through the cold, damp air like a blade. Godric stood at the edge of the mountain pass, his spear held firm, every fiber of his being focused on the shadows shifting in the stormy distance. His body ached, his muscles stiff from years of battle, but he knew better than to let it show.

Behind him, the gates loomed tall, carved deep into the stone, a fortress for the people below. He could feel the tension from the guards stationed at the entrance: greenhorns in borrowed armor, clutching their weapons like lifelines.

“Keep your hands steady,” Godric barked, his eyes fixed ahead. “Shake now, and I’ll have more to clean up than feathers and blood.”

One of them, braver than the rest, stammered, “We’re not Hunters, sir. We’re just—”

“Cowards, I know,” Godric growled under his breath, cutting him off.

The soldiers shifted uncomfortably. Perhaps he shouldn’t be so hard on them. They were little more than civilians.

“Do you think you can kill it?” one of them asked, his voice barely above a whisper.

Kill it? Boltrax, the mythical beast? No. Not alone. Stone me, not even with a whole hunting party. But someone has to stand here. And no one else has the spine.

That was reason enough.

“I’m not here to think,” he growled, letting the weight of his spear settle in his hands. “I’m here to hold the line.”

Another roar, closer this time. His heart pounded in his chest and he took a long deep breath, his ritual before any battle. Fear? No. Fear wouldn’t help him. It helped some stay alive, but was useless in a battle where retreat is not an option. He forced the thought away, letting the weight of the spear anchor him. The NullStone tip glinted faintly, its edges dulled from years of use.

A screech ripped through the air, sharp and grating, setting his teeth on edge. The beast was near. His grip tightened around the spear, his knuckles white against the worn wood. Come on then, you bastard.

The creature descended like a shadow, its wings spread wide, blocking out the dim light of the SolarStones lining the gate. Black feathers shimmered in the faint glow, streaked with veins of glowing blue. Its molten eyes burned with malice, and its talons gleamed like blades.

Godric planted his feet, his gaze steady. He wasn’t afraid of death—not anymore. If this was where he’d die, so be it. But he’d make bolting sure the beast remembered him.

It dove, fast and precise, its claws slicing through the air. Godric moved on instinct, twisting to the side and striking with his spear. The NullStone tip caught the edge of its face, slashing across one of its glowing eyes.

The beast roared, rearing back as blood—a strange, electric blue—dripped down its face.

“Not so pretty now, are you?” Godric muttered, circling it warily.

The beast twisted, its tail sweeping low and smashing through the stone. Godric leapt, the wind from its wings battering his frame as he brought the spear down, scraping across its shoulder. Sparks flew where the NullStone met feathers, but the creature barely flinched. Its claws raked forward, forcing Godric into a clumsy back-step that left him off balance

The creature hissed, its wings flaring as it launched itself at him again. He dodged, but not fast enough. Its tail lashed out but this time, catching him across the ribs with bone-crushing force.

The impact sent him flying.

Godric’s body hit the ground hard, skidding across the stone before slamming into the gates with a sickening thud. Pain erupted in his side—sharp and unrelenting—but he forced himself to move, slowly dragging himself toward the spear he’d dropped ten—no, fifteen—arm lengths away.

The beast stalked closer, its remaining eye blazing with fury.

Damn idiot! he thought. His arms shook with every inch. His shoulders felt ready to rip from their sockets. What a rookie mistake. Never drop your spear.

I’m… I’m not going make it.

Gritting his teeth, Godric stared into the eye of the one who bested him. This wasn’t his first time staring death in the face, although it would likely be his last.

Then, out of nowhere, a dark-skinned figure stepped into view.

The boy was no more than eight cycles, a scrap of skin and bone draped in a patched cloak. And yet, he walked like the storm bent to him, his steps too steady for a child, too sharp for anyone raised soft behind gates.

“What are you bolting doing?” Godric rasped, his voice hoarse from pain. “Get back inside!”

The boy ignored him. His dark eyes locked on the beast, unwavering.

“Kid!” Godric barked, trying to rise, but his body wouldn’t obey. He watched in disbelief as the boy stopped a few paces from the creature, his posture relaxed, his head tilted slightly.

“You know you can’t win,” the boy said, his voice cutting through the storm like a blade. “Do you really want to die?”

Godric froze. Was the boy… talking to the beast? His tone wasn’t taunting, nor was it pleading—it was something sharper, something final. For a moment, it seemed like even the creature hesitated, its wings twitching in response.

I must have hit my head harder than I thought.

The boy moved like no Hunter Godric had ever seen. In an instant, he snatched up the fallen spear, wielding it as though it had been made for him. His steps were impossibly light, his strikes unnervingly precise, each motion carrying a grace that belied his age. Godric’s jaw tightened, not in fear for the boy, but in awe. This wasn’t instinct—this was mastery.

The beast roared, unleashing a burst of lightning from the BoltStone embedded in its chest. Arcs of electricity seared through the air, but the boy was faster, twisting and leaping out of harm’s way.

The boy yelled, a sharp, guttural sound that cleaved through the storm. And then, impossibly, the lightning froze. It hung in the air, shimmering and crackling, before dissipating like mist. Godric’s breath hitched. No Hunter could command a storm. No mortal child could wield a voice that could still the chaos itself. Yet this boy—this child—had bent nature to his will.

What was he? There was no hesitation in his strikes, no wasted effort. Every step, every swing of the spear, was a calculated masterpiece, as if the boy had trained for decades. And yet, the training halls couldn’t teach this. Godric’s jaw tightened, watching the child fight as though born to wield death itself.

As he attempted to get up and join the fray, the guards rushed forward, dragging Godric inside the gates. He fought against them, his voice a growl. “Let me go! That kid—he can’t—”

The gates slammed shut, cutting off his protests.

The guards stood frozen, their whispers mingling with the storm. “Did you see that?” one breathed, his voice trembling. “He’s not human,” another whispered, clutching his chest.

Through the narrow crack, Godric saw the boy drive the spear into the beast’s leg, forcing it back. The creature shrieked, blood and sparks flying as it stumbled.

Then, with a final screech, it spread its wings and took to the skies, disappearing into the storm.

The boy stood alone, his chest heaving, the spear still clenched in his hands. How could he even lift that weapon? Let alone move like that?

When the gates opened again, I was already on my feet, wincing at the ache in my ribs, my glare fixed on the boy. “Who the ten stones are you?” I demanded.

The boy turned, his sharp brown eyes meeting mine with a calmness that unnerved me. “Srevlis,” he said simply.

An odd kid, no doubt. Five gems along his face gleamed faintly, tracing a crescent around his eye like they were part of him. His hands, though scarred and worn, they belonged to a seasoned Hunter, not a boy. He didn’t look like one of ours. He couldn’t be. “You’ve got a place here if you want it,” I offered, though I wasn’t sure where the words had come from.

Srevlis shook his head. “No.”

“What do you mean, no? Why not?” I snapped, my frustration spilling over.

“Do you think you’re the only victim?” His voice cut through the night like a blade.

And then he turned, his figure disappearing into the storm and shadows beyond the gates. I wanted to call out, to stop him, but something in his tone left me frozen.

That was the last I saw of him that night.

I was left with my thoughts, battered and bruised, leaning against the closed gates of Stormhaven.

Eight years old, and that boy did what I, Godric, a veteran Hunter, couldn’t. The beast didn’t stand a chance. Srevlis chased it through the storm like a shadow on its tail. He didn’t just hunt it, he haunted it. That night, he became something more. Crescent Moon Srevlis, the youngest Mythical Beast Hunter I’ve ever seen.

At the time, I didn’t understand the weight of his words. ‘Do you think you’re the only victim?’ God, how those words gnawed at me. They carved a place in my mind and stayed there, whispering every time the aches in my ribs reminded me of my failure. Not just as a Hunter but as a man.

It took a wild-born child, just eight years old, to remind me why I fight.

This account isn’t for those behind the gates. It’s for those who will stand at the edge. Those who will pick up their spears and face the storm, even when it costs everything.

We take up our arms for those we hold dear, and for those cruelly slaughtered by beasts.

We toil and train, that there might be one less soul claimed by this endless cycle of terror.

We shall never yield whilst we tire of being naught but the prey.

We fight, for it is time that WE become the Hunters!

And I’ll die before I forget it again
