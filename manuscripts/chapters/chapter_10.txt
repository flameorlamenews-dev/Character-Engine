Chapter 10 - War Claw Bresdin





“I stare down the crucible that defines my fate.”



Mettra, Fourth Week of Ferta

Fifteen days since beginning job assignment



Three uneventful work shifts came and went. As soon as Haldric got home, he bathed and cleaned his room. She was coming. They’d picked tonight weeks ago—before curfew, to share what they’d learned. 

But why did it have to be my house? He shook his head. He’d given up trying to understand Praew’s thought process long ago.

     Still, when the knock came, he felt a twinge of irritation. Not at her. At himself. Because this time, he was looking forward to it.

He opened the door.

Praew stood on the threshold, arms crossed, in the dim glow of the embedded SolarStone. She stepped inside without waiting for an invitation, already smirking. “Did you forget how to answer a door, or were you hoping I’d just leave?”



Haldric sighed. “No, I was just preparing myself for whatever nonsense you’re about to drag me into.”

“Good,” she said. “Because I’ve got plenty.”

Before she could say more, a voice rang out from the hallway. “Oh my!”

Haldric’s stomach sank.

Wintrid. She appeared with a delighted expression, her eyes already sparkling. Cenrick wasn’t far behind, moving slower but just as amused.

Praew blinked. “Uh… hi?”

Cenrick stroked his chin, looking between them. “Now this is a sight.”

“No, it isn’t,” Haldric said immediately.

Wintrid clasped her hands together. “She’s here at night, dear. What else could it be?”

“Nothing,” Haldric said flatly.

Cenrick hummed. “You know, lad, there was a time when we thought this day would never come.”

Praew smirked. “You doubted me?”

“Oh, we doubted him.” Cenrick nodded sagely. “You, my dear, always had more sense.”

Haldric rubbed his temple. “You’re both reading into things that aren’t there.”

Wintrid sighed dramatically. “Oh, let me have this, Haldric. I need some joy in my old age.”

Praew elbowed him lightly, tilting her head. “Dear… I don’t think I can lie to my future Grayparents like this.” She placed a hand over her heart, feigning guilt. “It just wouldn’t feel right.”

Haldric whipped his head toward her, eyes wide. “You!—”

Wintrid beamed. “Oh, I knew it!”

Cenrick chuckled. “Took you long enough, boy.”



Haldric exhaled sharply through his nose, his fists clenching. “You’re feeding them, Praew.”

She smiled innocently. “Just letting nature take its course.”

Wintrid patted Haldric’s shoulder fondly. “Well, I suppose we should leave you two lovebirds to talk.”

Cenrick grinned as he turned away. “Just don’t make me a Grand-Grayfather yet, boy.”

Haldric nearly choked. “What—? No—! That’s—”

But they were already gone.

Praew snorted. “They really have no faith in you, huh?”

Haldric exhaled, dragging a hand down his face as the last echoes of his Grayparents’ laughter faded into the next room. 

Then he turned to Praew, arms crossed. “Why did we have to meet at my place?”

Praew didn’t miss a beat. “Would you rather get interrogated by my father?”

Haldric hesitated. Just for a second. Then he shivered.

Praew grinned. “Thought so.”

Haldric scowled. “That still doesn’t explain why you had to lead my Grayparents into—whatever that was.”

Praew shrugged, entirely unbothered. “Now we have an excuse.”

Haldric narrowed his eyes. “An excuse for what?”

She smirked. “To meet, late at night.”

Haldric exhaled sharply, rubbing his temples. “You really think ahead, don’t you?”

Praew beamed. “Of course. I plan for everything.”

Haldric gave her a flat look. “Except how to avoid embarrassing me in my own home.”

She tapped her chin, mock-thoughtful. “Yeah, that part was just a bonus.”



Haldric sighed. “Let’s just get on with it.”

Haldric and Praew headed up to his bedroom.

Has my room always been this small? It doesn’t smell bad… right?

Not that he cared what Praew thought or anything. But still…this was the first time he’d ever had a girl in his room.

Don’t make this weird, he told himself.

Below, his Grayparents were whispering and giggling on the stairs. He tried to block it out, setting aside the embarrassment as he began turning over everything he’d gathered.

A few days ago, he told her, he’d witnessed something far above his ration allocation.

“Godric, the Mythical beast Hunter, met with The Prime Archon last Ravtra… if you can even call it that.”

Praew’s brows furrowed. “What do you mean?”

Haldric’s grip tightened against his forearm. “I was outside the office. I didn’t hear everything, but what I did hear?” He shook his head. “It wasn’t a meeting—it was a fight.”

Praew straightened. “A fight?”

Haldric nodded. “Godric was furious. I couldn’t make out The Prime Archon’s words, but Godric? He wasn’t holding back.” He hesitated, then recited the words burned into his memory:



“Are you bolting doing?!”

“You asked me to take over this position!”

“…DOING TO THEM?!”

“IF I FIND OUT YOU—”



He stopped there, watching as Praew’s expression darkened.

She leaned forward. “Them?”

Haldric nodded. “That’s the part I can’t stop thinking about.” His fingers drummed against the table. “Whatever this is, it’s big. Godric was ready to rip The Prime Archon apart.”

Praew narrowed her eyes. “And The Prime?”

“Shaken,” Haldric said. “Not openly, but… I noticed the little things. His sleeve was wrinkled, like someone had grabbed him. His collar was slightly off-center. And for the first time, I saw him lose control.”

 Praew absorbed that, her fingers tapping against her arm. “If those two are arguing, something serious is going on.” She frowned. “You know, that’s not the only weird thing about Godric.”

Haldric raised an eyebrow. “What do you mean?”

She leaned forward slightly. “He’s my drill instructor.”

His jaw dropped. “You’re getting trained by Silver Wing Godric?!”

She nodded. “At first, I thought it’d be exciting. He’s a legend, after all. But he’s always on edge. Either angry or irritated, nothing in between. I swear, there’s something off about him.”

Haldric straightened. “Off how? I heard he’s grumpy, but—”

“No, it’s more than that,” Praew cut in, lowering her voice. “Wannii, Junya’s best friend, and I were talking about the investigation and how Junya planned to slip away and enter the mines. Wannii told me she purposely let a stone slip during her Hunter test just to keep an eye on her. I’m pretty sure Godric was eavesdropping. He told us it’s dangerous to let stones slip. Said it’s not uncommon for someone to end up missing.

“And then, after Junya and Wannii snuck into the mines… they disappeared.”

Haldric sat back, an uneasy weight settling in his chest. “So you think he knew something? Or maybe he made them disappear on purpose?”

Praew nodded. “I can’t shake the feeling he was trying to scare us off. Like it was a final warning not to dig any deeper.”

Haldric stilled. He didn’t want to believe it. Godric was still one of his heroes. One of the two Mythical Beast Hunters of Stormhaven.

Praew’s eyes were sharp. “And do you know how Godric reacted when Bpen and I asked about Junya and Wannii?”

 She didn’t give him a chance to respond. “He didn’t,” she said flatly. “He didn’t acknowledge it at all. Just told us to get back to training. Like they never existed.”

Haldric absorbed that, his stomach twisting.

“You think he knows what happened?” he asked.

Praew clenched her jaw. “I don’t know. But The Prime and Godric are both acting suspicious right now. Something’s happening, Haldric. Something big.”

Haldric exhaled. “Maybe. But… I don’t know if The Prime Archon is in control of it.”

 “What do you mean?” she asked, tilting her head.

Haldric hesitated. “He wasn’t acting like someone who’s pulling the strings. More like someone barely hanging on.”

Praew considered that. “So, you think he’s a puppet?”

Haldric shrugged. “I think someone’s pushing him into a corner.”

She hummed, turning the thought over.

“Then we find out who’s pulling the strings.”

Haldric nodded. “And we do it without getting caught.”

Praew tapped her fingers against the table, thinking. Then, she met his gaze.

“You have a way in.”

Haldric frowned. “What?”

 “The Prime likes you.”

He stiffened. “He doesn’t like me.”

        Praew smirked. “You don’t see it, do you? Ever since you tried to convince Asher to make peace with his father, The Prime’s been treating you differently. He wouldn’t have let you stay in his office otherwise.”

  Haldric clenched his jaw. “That doesn’t mean I can just—”

  “It means you should use it,” Praew cut in. “Get close to him. Earn his trust.”

  He looked away, something uneasy curling in his stomach.

  “He already trusts me,” he muttered.

  “Then get him to tell you things,” Praew said. “Not just what he wants you to hear. Find out what he’s really up to.

     Haldric exhaled sharply, rubbing his temple. This was a bad idea. A really bad idea. And yet…

“If I do this,” he said slowly, “then you stay out of trouble. No stupid risks. No sneaking around on your own.”

Praew grinned. “No promises.”

Haldric gave her a flat look.

“Fine,” she relented. “I won’t do anything reckless. Happy?”

“Not even remotely.”

She just smirked. “Then we have a plan.” 

Haldric exhaled, resting his elbows on the table. They had a plan. He just hoped it wouldn’t get them killed.



***



Morning found Haldric back in the records hall, where everything carried on as usual: silent, orderly, stifling. He had just settled into the mindless rhythm of sorting when the door swung open.

A supervisor entered, his presence neither hurried nor casual, but with purpose. He made his way past the rows of clerks and stopped beside a mid-level officer—a man Haldric recognized but had never spoken to.

“You won’t be handling your task today,” the supervisor said, handing the officer a slip of parchment.

The clerk barely glanced at it. “What? Why?”

“It’s been reassigned.”

The clerk sighed, shaking his head. “To who?”

The supervisor’s eyes flicked toward Haldric.

“Haldric?” the clerk said, frowning slightly. “He’s too new for this.”

The supervisor’s voice remained steady. “It came from higher up.”

That ended the discussion. Without another word, the supervisor turned and walked away.

The clerk exhaled, stretching his neck.

“Fine. Guess it’s yours now,” the clerk muttered, pushing a stack of papers toward Haldric.



Haldric took them, still processing what just happened while breaking the wax seal. The parchment was thick, heavier than standard notices. Official. Haldric skimmed the text.

Then his fingers tightened around the edges.



RETIREMENT NOTICE

For: War Claw Bresdin, Mythical Beast Hunter



Retirement. Honorable discharge.



Not exile but grounded. The city didn’t want Bresdin to die out there. But he wasn’t allowed on the surface anymore. Effective immediately.

And the one assigned to make it official? Haldric himself. 

     Stone me.

He had to strip a legend of his active-duty sash. The weight of it settled in his stomach, cold and uneasy. He looked up—

The clerk, who had looked so irritated a moment ago, was grinning ear to ear. Now that the supervisor was gone, the clerk had no reason to feign disappointment. That Ratrod was faking the whole time!

Haldric said nothing. Just exhaled sharply and stood. He had a job to do.

And something told him… this wouldn’t go well.

He took the parchment, folding it carefully before slipping it inside his uniform. His fingers clenched slightly at the feel of it. Honorable discharge. A legend forced into retirement. This is just a formality, he told himself. I’m sure they’ve already briefed Bresdin. He is probably just waiting for the official notice.

Haldric sighed loudly and turned toward the exit.



The Hunters’ Office was technically just one Floor down from The Prime Archon’s Office, but getting there was another story entirely. The administrative sector and the Hunters’ sector didn’t connect directly.

By design.

To reach the Hunters’ Office, he had to exit into the outer ring, descend through the crowded lower tunnels, then cut back through the city’s winding pathways to reach the center again.

It was a long, inconvenient detour—a deliberate obstacle.

A reminder that The Prime Archon’s people and the Hunters were not meant to cross paths.

As he stepped into the streets, the air changed. The polished stone of the inner ring gave way to rougher, more uneven pathways. The slums, still waking from the night, pulsed with slow-moving bodies—workers heading to shifts, traders setting up in whatever spaces they could carve out.

Haldric moved quickly, efficiently, blending into the flow of traffic.

No one paid attention to a clerk.

By the time he reached the entrance to the Second Floor’s outer ring, the crowd had shifted. The atmosphere here was different from the quiet order of the administrative halls—it felt more alive, but also more dangerous.

No enforcers patrolled here. Not unless they wanted trouble.

Hunters policed themselves.

As he climbed the narrow, uneven steps, the stone walls seemed to close in, the passage roughly carved and less refined than the polished walkways above.

And then, at last, reaching the innermost ring of the Second Floor, Haldric stood before the entrance to the Hunter’s Office: a heavy iron door, slightly rusted around the edges, embedded deep into the cavern wall.

The weight of what lay beyond settled in his chest.

This was a bad idea.

The moment he stepped inside, they’d see the uniform. They’d know where he came from.

And they wouldn’t welcome him.

The iron door groaned as Haldric pushed it open, its weight cold against his fingers.

The air here was different—charged, heavier. Conversations dulled, movements slowed, and heads turned.



They had noticed him.

A clerk in a room full of Hunters.

Haldric kept his steps measured as he approached the central desk. The Hunters lounging nearby didn’t hide their stares. Some muttered under their breath, others openly watched.

The man at the front desk barely looked up. Older. Scarred. Unimpressed. His armor bore fresh claw marks—a Hunter still active in the field.

Haldric cleared his throat. “I need to speak with War Claw Bresdin.”

The man didn’t react, just kept flipping through a logbook. “He’s busy.”

Haldric’s fingers twitched. “It’s official business.”

A beat. Then, the man let out a slow exhale, finally lifting his gaze. Flat. Unimpressed.

“Official business, huh?” His lip curled. “From The Prime?”

The room stilled.

Haldric ignored the tension climbing up his spine. “Yes.”

The man started at Haldric, then called out over his shoulder. “Bresdin!”

Nothing.

He called again. “Oi, War Claw! You got a fan.”

Silence.

Haldric exhaled sharply. “I’ve been sent to conduct an Honorable Discharge.”

That got a reaction.

A few Hunters straightened. A handful exchanged glances.

This was a REALLY bad idea.

Near the back of the room, a figure slipped out the door. Quiet. Quick. Gone before anyone could react.

Bresdin.



Haldric’s stomach sank.

A low chuckle broke the silence. One of the Hunters—tall, wiry, jagged scars across his jaw -- grinned. “Did you just say ‘Honorable’?”

Another snorted. “You came to put War Claw Bresdin out to pasture? Stormhaven’s first Mythical Beast Hunter in the last two centuries?”

A third Hunter let out a sharp breath. “Is that a joke?”

Haldric’s jaw tightened. Here it comes.

The room shifted.

A few Hunters stood up. Slow, deliberate.

Haldric forced himself to stay still as three of them closed the distance, their expressions outright hostile.

The tall, scarred Hunter spoke first. “Tell me, clerk. Do you even know how a Beast Hunter earns the Mythical title?”

Haldric exhaled through his nose. “Of course. Who doesn’t? You have to injure a mythical beast solo, with at least three witnesses.”

A rough, older voice cut in. “Yet you want to retire the only man in this city’s history to not just injure, but kill one?”

The scarred one shook his head. “The bolting Prime thinks he can force a legend into retirement?” He leaned in slightly. Too close. “You think Bresdin’s gonna let that happen?”

Another Hunter scoffed. “You ever seen a real fight, boy? Ever watched your team get torn apart? Ever stood drenched in blood that ain’t yours?”

Haldric’s jaw tightened. “No.”

“Then don’t talk to us about ‘honor.’” The last word was spat.

A hand shoved Haldric’s shoulder. Not hard. But not gentle, either.

He stayed firm. Didn’t react. Didn’t flinch.



Another Hunter scoffed. “We answer to one thing, clerk.” His fingers tapped the handle of a long knife at his hip. “And it sure as stones ain’t some bolting Prime underling.”

He took a step towards Haldric, but Haldric didn’t move.

Another step. Then—a voice.

“Enough.”

The Hunters stopped, their attention snapping toward the front desk. The old Hunter behind it hadn’t moved. But his tone had changed. The scarred one held Haldric’s gaze a moment longer, then exhaled sharply. “Fine.” He rolled his shoulders. “Guess you get to leave in one piece.”

Haldric took that as his exit. He turned, keeping his steps measured, controlled. Never rushing. Never looking back. And the moment he stepped past the threshold and back into the tunnels, his breath finally left his lungs.

He didn’t go far. He ducked into the nearest side path, pressing himself against the stone wall, exhaling slowly. His heart was still racing. They hadn’t hurt him but they’d bolting wanted to.

He had been sent to take the sash from one of the only two Mythical Beast Hunters in Stormhaven. A true Hunter.

And that man?

Had slipped away the moment he heard. That wasn’t the reaction of someone accepting their fate. That was the reaction of someone who had already made their decision.

Haldric pushed off the wall, his feet already moving. There was only one place Bresdin could be going. And if Haldric was right—if Bresdin was planning what it looked like—then this wasn’t over. 

Not even close. Haldric could catch up before Bresdin reached the surface.

Haldric reached another set of gates—no, doors. Massive, looming structures of reinforced stone and iron. Their sheer size made him feel small. His breath came ragged, lungs burning from the unfamiliar air. Static prickled across his skin, his body still struggling to adapt. But he refused to show weakness.

“Give up, Bresdin!” Haldric called out.

Bresdin stood just ahead, waiting before the gates.

He stepped forward with a smirk. “Do you really think you’re in control, boy? Do you even know what’s behind this door? How close you are to the surface?”

Haldric’s fists clenched. He wasn’t in the mood for taunts.

“Why do you chase me? You want a fight?” Bresdin spread his arms wide, his voice thick with amusement. “Very well. If it’s a fight you want, it’s a fight I’ll give you.”

 He gave a subtle signal, and the door swung open.

The light struck Haldric like a hammer, searing through his skull. His vision shattered into a storm of burning white, blotting out everything. His breath hitched. He was blind.

His body tensed, but his mind reached for something steady: training.

Since childhood, he and Praew had drilled for moments like this. At first, it had been a game. A test of skill. Blindfolded sparring sessions that started with clumsy swipes and uncontrollable laughter. But as they got older, the laughter faded. The training became real. Because one day, they knew, they might have to fight blind—mud in their eyes, blood dripping down their face, the dirty tricks of a dishonorable Hunter.

He never thought he’d use it against one of his heroes.

The light from the surface was so bright, he could feel heat on his skin, the glare rendering his eyes useless. But that didn’t mean he was defenseless. He slowed his breathing, stretching his awareness outward. The tunnel was narrow, the space between them small. The air shifted—Bresdin was moving. His boots scraped against the stone, the slight shift of weight betraying his stance.

  Haldric heard the faintest rustle of armor. A punch cut through the air. He tilted his head just enough for it to graze past his cheek. Another step, another shift in weight—he twisted, ducking under a second blow. He could hear the faint rustle of armor, the drag of fabric against skin. Bresdin swung again, but Haldric sidestepped, each dodge fueling Bresdin’s aggravation. This Hunter, this so-called legend, had resorted to blinding a young, unarmed man, but it wasn’t working.

  His attacks grew sharper, faster, but they weren’t landing. Haldric could hear his growing frustration.

  “You think you can dance around forever, boy?” Bresdin’s voice held a growl now.

  Haldric didn’t answer. Words wouldn’t help him here.

  Then, the sound he had been dreading: a long, deliberate scrape of metal leaving its sheath.

  A weapon.

     His heart kicked against his ribs. This was different. He could dodge fists, predict movements. But a blade? That was an entirely different kind of fight. Too many possibilities. Too many ways for this to end badly.

  The whoosh of steel came fast.

  Haldric twisted on instinct, feeling the air part where the blade should have cut him in two. He barely had time to reset before it came again, each swing faster, more precise. Bresdin wasn’t slashing wildly—he was testing him, seeing how long he could hold out. The air burned in his lungs, his vision still nothing but white light, but he gritted his teeth and focused.

  Another shift. The blade lifted. A downward swing.

  Haldric dropped low.

  And then, the unmistakable sound of metal colliding with stone. A loud, sharp clang.

  Bresdin’s breath hitched. A hesitation.

  Then, a scream.

  “My hand! Arthur! You cut off my bolting hand!”

  Haldric braced for another attack—only, none came. The air shifted. Boots shuffled.

     The Prime Archon’s voice cut through the tension, smooth and laced with disdain. “Come now, War Claw, did you truly think your dealings with the rebels would go unnoticed?” He gave a quiet chuckle. “You’re late, Bresdin. We’ve been expecting you.”

  “Take him away,” The Prime Archon ordered, and Haldric could hear the shuffling of feet as guards moved in.

  “Prime Archon? Sir?” Haldric called out, still blinded.

  “Yes, son, I’m here,” The Prime answered, his voice unusually soft. “Are you hurt anywhere?”

  Haldric felt hands on his shoulders—steady, firm, checking him over. Not like a superior evaluating an officer. Like a father making sure his son was still in one piece. It was quick, efficient, but there was something else beneath the movement. Tension. Relief.

  “I’m okay,” Haldric managed.

  The Prime Archon’s grip lingered for a second too long. Then, just as quickly as it had come, the moment snapped shut.

  “Of course you are.” His tone had changed—back to the sharp, composed authority of Arthur Rivers. A controlled breath. “That was impressive, how you managed to dodge while blind. I didn’t know you could fight like that.” 

He hesitated, then added with quiet satisfaction, “The Hunter administration was foolish to fail someone like you.”

  Haldric was still trying to process everything. His thoughts blurred together, but one rose to the surface.

  “How long have you been here?” he asked The Prime.

  “For quite some time,” The Prime Archon said smoothly. “I knew Bresdin might attempt to flee.”

  Haldric, with a hint of accusation, asked, “Why didn’t you just have the guards block the first gate?”

  The Prime Archon explained, “For the guards’ safety. They can’t stop a Hunter. If a group of them came, it could start a war. It’s better to handle it in an area with no guards around, nowhere for him to escape.”

  Haldric hesitated before responding, “I suppose that makes sense, sir.”

  The Prime Archon nudged him. “I’m sure you still can’t see, but I believe your audience wishes to speak with you.”

  Haldric blinked in surprise. “Audience?”

  The Prime Archon smiled. “It seems quite a few Hunters followed you from the Headquarters. They watched your fight.”

  Haldric’s surprise grew. He hadn’t heard them at all. The realization revealed just how focused he’d been on chasing down Bresdin. He had thought he was chasing prey, but all along, a group of Hunters had been tracking him.



***

Arthur Rivers watched with a keen eye as the Hunters surrounded Haldric, bombarding him with questions. “How did you do that? Someone from The Prime’s office fighting a Mythical Beast Hunter while unarmed and blind? Have you been acclimated to the surface before?”

  Their admiration was palpable, and Arthur felt a surge of satisfaction. This young man could be the key to bridging the gap between his administration and the Hunters.

  Arthur observed how the Hunters shook Haldric’s hand, their awe evident. He recognized the potential of this moment. Haldric’s performance today had shown not just skill but also resilience and adaptability, qualities Arthur highly valued.

  Standing beside Haldric, Arthur felt a sense of excitement, tinged with disgust. This young man could be the bridge he needed. The bridge that Asher was supposed to be.

  This must be the reason they insisted I reassign the task to Haldric. Just how far ahead can they predict? …How unsettling.
