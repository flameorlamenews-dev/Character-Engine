Chapter 9 - The Breakup



The next morning came far too fast.

     Praew found herself in an empty classroom standing in front of Mai Azadi. Just as she hoped.  Unfortunately, everything she had planned to say had fled her.

Mai spoke first, not even bothering to look up from her book.

“You don’t look like one of my students.”

“Hey Mai,” Praew began, “I just —“

“If you are not one of my students,” Mai interrupted, “You are not permitted to enter the classroom at this time. Please leave to avoid being forcefully escorted off the premises.” Mai stood up, likely to escort her.

“You didn’t deserve what I said,” Praew blurted out, her voice low and shaken. Mai froze in place. “I was scared. Angry. I thought I knew what kind of person you were, but I didn’t. And you didn’t deserve that. I’m sorry.”

Mai turned, tilted her head. Sideways looking confused.

“I still don’t know how to feel about you,” Praew said. “I’m not like you, Mai. I don’t know if we ever could be friends. But… I’m willing to try if you still want to…but there needs to be boundaries.”

Mai studied her in silence. No teasing, no smile.

Then finally, a soft one surfaced. Not her usual grin. Something smaller. Realer.

“That’s fair,” Mai said. “First rule: no more calling me a psychopath.”

Praew almost smiled. “Deal. Second rule: no more showing up at my house.”

Mai’s head tilted to the opposite side. “Even if I bring gifts?”

“Rule 3,” Praew continued, “No stalking. No threats, and no talk of murder.”

Mai sighed, dramatically. “Fine. But you’re no fun. Then… rule four: you let me train you in silent movement. At this rate, you’re going to fail your final.”

Praew awkwardly stepped forward in an attempt at quieter movement.

“What? How would you know?”

Mai looked at her sympathetically.

“The same way I knew it was you in the hallway without ever looking up from my book. Praew, my sweet porridge, I’ve seen blind children move with more grace than you.”

     “Fine,” Praew said sharply. “But I’m not doing it because it’s your condition. I’m only doing it because I never turn down a chance to train.”

     Mai blinked. “Sure thing. Then we start today. When I finish class, let’s meet here to start your training.”



***



After agreeing, Praew left before any students got to class. Spending the day training, she didn’t return until the students were starting to file out. 

“Praew! What are you doing here?” Junya asked cheerfully as Praew entered the room. Right behind her, Wannii gave Praew a nod. 

      Praew nodded back before responding to Junya. “Oh I’m going to do a little sparring with your teacher.” 

“You’re going to do what?!” Bpen asked walking up to them

“Don’t worry,” Praew said. “I’ll try not to hurt her. 

Mai near the front of the class snorted loud enough for them to hear. 

“Right,” Bpen said, putting a hand on her shoulder. “You will be missed, old friend.” He shook his head as he left the room.

“So…” Junya started, “are you going to be busy all night tonight?” 

That seemed a little too excited

 Praew glanced towards Wannii, who had the same look on her face. That question felt off to both of them. Wannii shook her head quickly, then pointed to herself. 

Good. Praew nodded. Wannii would keep an eye on her tonight while Praew trained.

“Yeah… unfortunately,” Praew said, realizing she still hadn’t responded.

“Well, that’s too bad” Junya said.  “I guess we can hang out some other time.”  Before Praew could reply, Junya was gone. Wannii had to break into a jog to chase after her.

     The classroom emptied, leaving just Mai and Praew.

     “Doesn’t it ever get uncomfortable being so popular?” Mai asked curiously. “I mean, anytime you walk into a room, you can’t even blend in. What if you have to assassinate someone?”

Praew raised an eyebrow.

“So…” Mai bounced on her heels. “We made up now, right? Like officially made-up, not a fake make-up, because there’s this book I’m reading that says your first argument is, like, the most important moment in a relationship. It said this argument sets the tone for how she’ll treat you from then on, and determines whether she’ll respect you, neglect you, or completely reject you.”

Mai blinked. Totally serious.

“You’re not gonna break up with me, are you?”

Break up with…” Praew rubbed her temple. “Mai, what exactly was that book called?”

 “Oh! Uh…” Mai scrambled to the front. “I have it with me.”

She reached under her desk and pulled out a black book with a white sketch on the cover. It showed a pair of eyes winking and a mouth biting its own lip.

Mai held it up proudly. “Her Smile, Your Success: Satisfy Her and Keep the Relationship Strong.”

Praew stared. “We’re not courting, Mai.”

Mai blinked. “What’s courting?”

“What do you mean, what’s courting?” Praew was aghast. She was no expert by any means. In fact, she was probably the worst person to ask altogether. But to not even know what it was? Poor girl, Praew thought, taking the back of Mai’s hand and patting it pitifully.

     Mai tilted her head like a confused Faelhen. “Like… I know I was trained on it, but I just can’t remember. I think it involves people and… emotions? Or dancing maybe? I never did any missions that required it. My handler always said I’m too young.”

     She fidgeted with the book’s corner. “Anyhow, when we argued, you made it pretty clear I’m not very good at this friendship thing, and I might’ve made you a little uncomfortable with my eagerness and spying.”

“A little?” Praew asked incredulously.

“Okay, so a lot uncomfortable,” Mai admitted sheepishly. “Well, I figured I should learn how to be a better friend. The book said that when you test her patience and she gets angry, it’s best to give her space, don’t pester her, and give her time. I didn’t think it made much sense, because how is it a test if it was an accident in the first place?”

     She perked up. “But we made up, so I guess that means we passed the test!” She clapped once. “Yay us!”

     Praew couldn’t help but smile. How could she ever think Mai was a bad person? She was so painfully innocent—and trying so hard. Misguided, sure. But she was trying.

      Mai clapped again. “Well, we better start training, eh? Partner?”

     Praew rolled her eyes as Mai opened the secret passageway leading to the testing area. Mai led the way, and Praew followed a step behind through the dim corridor.

     “Hey, Mai,” she said, her voice a little softer, “can you do me a favor?”

     “Anything! What do you need?” Mai responded, eyes bright.

     “Next time you get a chance… can you burn that book?”

     Mai paused mid-step. “It’s not the right book, is it?”

     Praew shook her head.

     “Yeah, I guess that makes sense,” Mai said with a sigh. “Chapter Eight was really weird. It seemed very unsanitary, so I skipped most of it.”

     That finally did it, Praew burst out laughing. Mai joined in a beat later, giggling as the hidden door creaked shut behind them.

     Praew followed Mai into the training room, their giggles mingling with the sound of their footsteps. Mai led her through a narrow passage until they reached the familiar testing path, a strip of polished stone flanked by SolarStones. Each one was triggered by EchoStones or sound.

     Praew shook out her arms behind her. “Now don’t go easy on me. I need to get better—fast. I’m sure you’re really good at this, so train me the way you were.”

     Mai glanced back over her shoulder and nodded. Her eyes seemed… distant, somehow.

Mai glanced back again. Her gaze held, focused and unusually still. “You’ve been practicing silent movement in Shadow intelligence, right?”

     “Yeah,” Praew said, a bit hesitant.

     Mai nodded toward the path. “Show me.”

     Praew stepped forward, took a breath, and moved. Her foot scraped slightly, and one of the SolarStones flickered in response. She winced, kept going, then stumbled just enough to throw off her rhythm. Another faint glow pulsed along the edge. She stopped halfway and turned back, her return just as uneven.

     “I’m not very good at it yet,” Praew said, walking back.

     Mai was already smiling when Praew reached her again. Or trying to. One corner of her mouth tugged upward in a stiff, uneven stretch, like it was embedded in place. The way her eye twitched uncontrollably, Praew first thought a BoltStone had shocked her mid-blink. It was the first time Praew had ever seen her struggle to smile.

     Is she trying to reassure me? Zephyrs take me, that looks painful!

     “I can tell,” Mai eventually said. “Your overthinking makes you stiff. Take a deep breath and slowly exhale while relaxing your shoulders.”

     Praew complied.

     “Good,” Mai said, a genuine smile returning. “But there’s still too much tension in your hips.” She stepped in front of Praew. “Loosen here—yeah. Now don’t look down, but when you step, roll your foot from the tips of your toes to the ball. No heel. No slap. Quiet like blinking, not a scuffling boot.”

     Praew nodded, shifting her weight, trying to roll her foot like Mai said. But halfway through the motion, her heel clipped the floor with a sharp clack. She froze.

     Then she sighed, eyes dropping to her boots.

     That’s when Mai moved.

     Praew glanced up just in time to see Mai’s shoulder twist—just enough warning to catch the blur of a fist shooting toward her gut. Praew gasped, stumbling back as her arms flew out and caught the strike just in time.

     “What are you doing?” she snapped.

     Mai blinked. “What am I—?” Her fist still pressed against Praew’s palm. “What are you doing? Why did you block it?”

     “Why wouldn’t I?” Praew bellowed. “And answer the question. Why did you try to hit me?”

     “Because you asked me to!” Mai snapped. “Didn’t you tell me to train you how I was?”

     “Zephyrs take me. That’s how you were trained?”

     “Well, yeah,” Mai admitted, “my handler said not to train students like this, but… is it really that bad? Wait—did I mess up again? Oh no. Did we just have another fight?”

     Praew exhaled, lowering her guard. “Fine, no, we’re fine. But prepare yourself, Mai. If you’re swinging, I’m blocking. If I’m taking hits, I’m hitting back. I learn by standing my ground, not by standing down.”

     The next three days with Mai made Praew question her life choices. Repeatedly.



***



With all that bravado, Praew failed to land a single hit. It felt like she spent more time sparring than actually practicing silent movement. Eventually, they agreed to just one swing each, per mistake. Still, hopefully the bruises weren’t too noticeable.

     “Focus, Praew,” Godric barked in class afterwards. “Then maybe you’ll actually win your next match with whatever boulder ran you over.”

     Zephyrs take this bolting man. If this is how he talks to women, it’s no wonder he’s never been married.

     “Sorry, sir,” Praew muttered. One look at Godric was enough to keep her from saying what was really on her mind.

     “Don’t be sorry, be better,” Godric growled. “I asked where Junya and Wannii are. They do not have permission to skip class.”

     Praew was so tired and sore, she hadn’t even noticed they were gone.

     “I don’t know, sir. I’m sure they have a good reason,” she said.

     Like staying up all night investigating and failing to wake up on time, she thought, but wisely kept that to herself.

     Godric grunted and turned to the rest of the class. “Today we’re going over the uses and limitations of SurgeStones.”

Godric didn’t pace around the room like usual. He stomped. Back and forth like a riled Tarnox, boots hammering the stone.

     “SurgeStones,” he barked, “can keep you alive, if you know what you’re doing. But one mistake, and you’ll be staring death in the face. You want to stay alive? Then remember these rules.”

He slammed a fist on his desk. Every student jumped.

“Rule one. A SurgeStone doubles the power of a Sifaralith Stone and raises its rarity rank by one. Temporarily. But it also shuts it down afterward, for exactly the same length of time. You surge for five flickers, both stones are deadweight for five more. You surge for a flash?” He locked eyes with Praew. “You’d better hope you have more stones, or you’ll be left with nothing but your fists and regret.”

     Praew’s stomach turned. What’s going on? He seems more grouchy than usual today.

     “Be mindful of how long you surge,” Godric warned, stomping back toward his desk. “Whatever stone you’re surging, it’ll spiral and crumble twice as fast. Doesn’t matter if it’s Common or Noble. If you forget to check on it or time your surge, it could spiral straight to dust. That’s how many Hunters lose their lives.

     Bpen, off to Praew’s right, raised his hand. “But sir—”

     Godric stopped mid-stomp and shot him a look sharp enough to silence a Faelhen mid-mimic.

     “Now pay attention. This is where most rookies screw up. SurgeStones aren’t one-size-fits-all. A Noble SurgeStone can override another Noble or a Common Stone. But try using a Common SurgeStone on a Noble, and you’ll be standing there looking stupid with a stone that does nothing.”

     Yeah, something is definitely off, Praew thought. He wasn’t letting anyone interrupt today.

     “You’ve probably guessed by now. This is how we identify rarity. You want to know what kind of stone you’re holding?” Godric asked. “Test it. If it doesn’t react, it’s stronger than your surge. If it connects, congratulations. You just found your limit.” He spat the next words like bile.

     “Unfortunately, just like in life, one’s rank isn’t measured by success, but by their failure.”

     He turned to the slate board and scraped three shapes: a circle, a bolt, and a fang. “Core. Nature. Beast.”

     He stabbed the stone into each, making the slate board click. “Core and Beast Stones can work on each other, no problem. But like I told you before, don’t mess with Nature Stones. They’re too bolting unpredictable. I had one nullify every stone I carried once. It’s safe to assume there’s one out there that could surge them too.”

     He let the silence settle.

    “If you ever sense Nature acting suspicious, hide your stones at all costs. Maybe you’ll live to see another day.”

     A few students began to whisper to each other. But Godric’s growl cut through the room and silenced it immediately.

     “You can surge your own body, too. Strength. Speed. Stamina. Even reflexes. Makes you sharper for a flicker or superhuman for a flash. But when the surge ends?” He slapped his chest. “Your body crashes. Hard. That weakness lasts twice as long. You surge for ten flickers, you’ll be crawling for twenty. Surge too long, and your veins turn black, your skin starts to crack, and eventually your body crumbles like a beast’s corpse. I’ve seen it, and it ain’t pretty.”

     Godric stopped, closed his eyes, and shook his head like he was trying to shake off a memory.

     “One of the most dangerous things on the surface, second only to Nature itself, is other humans,” Godric said, not even trying to hide his disgust. “And make no mistake, you will have to fight them.”

     He began to pace again. “You can surge other people’s stones. If you make contact. Just like with NullStone contact requirements, your surge must trigger on your body or weapon while in contact with the effect of their stone. Weapon-to-weapon contact counts, as long as the embedded stones are both active.

     “When successful, your SurgeStone links to theirs. Their stone temporarily increases one rank in rarity, becoming more powerful, but your surge takes over as their trigger. Even if they cut theirs, you can keep their stone active for as long as yours is.”

     He sliced the air with his hand.

     “And when you cut yours off? Their stone times out. Useless. Perfect moment to strike. Just remember to keep that stone in your sight.

     “There are three ways to lose control.”

     He raised a finger. “First and most common, they hide their stone. Tossing a cloth over it or putting it away long enough will break the link.”

     “Second, they can use a NullStone with equal or greater rarity than your SurgeStone. That severs the connection instantly.”

     He gave a short nod, walking back toward his desk.

    “Both of those will trigger cooldown. Your stone and theirs are unable to trigger for the same length of the surge. That’s why surges work best on embedded weapons. Makes it easier to throw off their rhythm and harder for them to break contact.”

     Reaching under the desk, Godric pulled out five wooden baskets and gently placed them on top with a tenderness Praew was surprised he was capable of.

     “Listen up, Ratrods. Split into pairs. One attacker, one defender. Each of you will take two stones: one SurgeStone, and one other of your choice.”

     He scanned the room. “We’re heading to the training room to practice surging and cutting surge links. After that, I’ll test each of you on your body surge limits. You need to feel, not guess, how long you can push yourself before you crumble.”

     Godric opened the secret door.

     “If we get through everyone without any major injuries, I’ll tell you the third method. The only true counter to a surge.”

     His voice echoed without emotion.

     A sharp light cut through Haldric’s window, searing into his eyes even through closed lids.

     That’s how it happened every morning. One giant SolarStone in each district, triggered at the fifth flash, acted as both alarm and signal—the day had begun. At the fifteenth flash, the trigger would stop, and a thick stone box would be hoisted over the SolarStone to dim its flashes throughout the night.

     They said the surface had a SolarStone as big as the planet hanging in the sky. That made no sense, though. What could possibly trigger a Sifaralith that massive?

     Haldric rolled onto his back, staring up at the uneven rock above him, half-listening to the muffled sounds of Cenrick and Wintrid moving downstairs. The faint scent of boiled blood cubes and rice paste lingered in the air—their breakfast, if it could even be called that. His would be distributed at the ration station near the administration building.

He wasn’t hungry, though. His appetite had vanished the day he found out his Grayparents had been quietly skipping meals, just so he could have a little more.

He dressed without thinking, pulling on the stiff, standard-issue uniform they had given him on his first day. Muted gray, like everything else in this city. Nothing about it marked him as anything special—no insignia, or indicator of status.

Haldric said goodbye to his Grayparents and rushed out the door. The middle ring district of the Third Floor was already awake, but barely.

The slums never felt truly alive—just a dull, exhausted shuffle of bodies, the sound of footsteps against uneven stone. The air was thick with the faint sourness of unwashed clothes, the ever-present dampness of underground life. A few traders set up makeshift stalls, their eyes darting around, watching for enforcers. The last thing anyone needed was to be caught selling without permission.

     Haldric kept walking.

     The Second Floor was quieter in a different way—less desperation, more resignation. Here, the roads widened, not much, but enough to show the difference. The homes had doors. Real wooden ones, not the thick stone slab he lived behind or the cloth-draped entryways of the less fortunate. People moved with purpose, but without hope. It wasn’t hunger that slowed them so much as it was acceptance.

And then, as always, the First Floor was something else entirely.

The shift was immediate, buildings carved with precision, the streets perfectly aligned. Enforcers stood at key intersections, motionless as statues, helmets reflecting the glow of SolarStones overhead. The people here moved calmly, orderly, as if they knew eyes were always watching.

Haldric exhaled as the administrative building came into view—its tall, imposing entrance flanked by two guards who barely even looked at him as he stepped inside.

By the time he entered the building, his mind had already begun shutting down in preparation for the tedium ahead.



The records hall was a monument to routine. A vast, windowless chamber, its tall, domed ceiling pressing down like the weight of the cavern itself. The desks stretched in perfect, unmoving rows, each identical, each occupied by another clerk just like him. No marks of ownership, no sign of individuality, just worn stone and endless parchment. The air smelled of old ink and dust, heavy and unmoving. The only sounds were the scratch of Faelhen quills, the shuffle of parchment, and the occasional clearing of a throat. 

Somewhere in the distance, a slow, deliberate flicker of light was swallowed by six flashes—the unlucky number that marked the start of his shift.

Start at the sixth flash, work six more, six until curfew, seven to snore.

For the next six flashes, he’d do nothing but sort, stamp, and record data. Hunter reports: Mostly filled with exaggerated kill counts, half of them barely legible. Resource distribution forms: He wasn’t allowed to question why certain districts got more than others. Incident logs: Minor disputes, market altercations, someone caught trying to barter with ration slips.

Stamp. Sign. Sort. Repeat.

It was enough to make his brain crumble.

The worst part was the redundancy. Every form had to be checked, verified, logged, then sent to another department for a second verification. As if they didn’t trust their own system.

He flipped through a stack of Hunter evaluations, eyes glazing over the familiar categories:



Kill Efficiency – How fast they eliminated threats.

Resource Retrieval – How many usable parts they recovered from beasts.

Behavioral Stability – A vague but important column. Too reckless? Too independent? Too many violations? Hunters who failed this category usually disappeared.



Haldric’s fingers hovered over that last section for a moment before he forced himself to keep moving. 

Stamp. Sign. Sort.

Across from him, Gerrik, a man in his mid-thirties with a permanent slouch and an unfortunate mustache, yawned loudly. “You ever feel like we’re just moving numbers around to make it seem like something’s happening?”

     Haldric didn’t look up. “We are.”

     Gerrik hummed in agreement. “I spent an hour yesterday logging a report about another report being misplaced.”

     Haldric flipped another page.

     “Then I had to write a report about the process of recovering that report.”

     He finally looked up, narrowing his eyes. “You’re making that up.”

     Gerrik raised his hands. “I swear on my ration card.”

     Haldric sighed and went back to work.



     When the midday break finally came, Haldric stretched his aching hands and stepped out onto the upper walkway, overlooking the lower levels of the administrative sector.

     Below, clerks and officers hurried between offices, voices blending into a low murmur. It was all so structured. So controlled. No one questioned anything. No one resisted.

He found himself wondering—did it really have to be this way? Had the people of Stormhaven always been forced into their roles? What was so wrong with choosing your own path?

He shook his head. That sounded an awful lot like treason.

And justice we will deliver, he thought. 

His gaze flickered toward the hallway leading to The Prime Archon’s office. Sealed doors, guarded at all times. Behind them, decisions were being made that people like him would never hear.

He exhaled sharply, turning away. This wasn’t his concern.

He had work to do.



The afternoon was worse than the morning.

The monotony stretched endlessly. His hand was cramping from stamping documents. His back ached from sitting in the same position for multiple flashes. The words on the pages started blurring together, names and numbers losing all meaning.

Occasionally, he’d hear footsteps—someone important passing by. But he never looked up.

Until just before the end of the day, when he arrived.

Haldric didn’t see him at first. He only felt the difference.

The way the guards near The Prime’s office straightened slightly, how clerks at nearby desks suddenly paid just a bit more attention to their work, avoiding glances upward.

Silver Wing Godric.

The Mythical Beast Hunter wasn’t here for pleasantries.

He stood near the entrance of The Prime Archon’s office, arms stiff at his sides but his posture was sharp, wound tight.

Haldric had seen Godric from a distance before. A disciplined, structured man. A model soldier in every way. A true hero.

But now…

Godric’s normally neat hair was slightly unkempt, a few strands falling loose from where they should have been spiked back. His uniform, usually crisp, had a slight misalignment at the collar, the fabric wrinkled like he had been gripping it too tightly.

He stepped forward. Spoke low. “I have questions for The Prime.”

The guards exchanged a glance but didn’t argue. One of them pushed open the door, and Godric stepped inside.

The door clicked shut behind him.



And that was when Haldric moved.

He pushed back from his desk, stretched as if casually shaking off fatigue, then made his way toward the same guards.

One of them barely looked at him before speaking. “What do you need?”

Haldric kept his expression neutral. Relaxed. “I need to pass along a report. The Archon’s office requested it this morning.”

That wasn’t entirely a lie. The Prime had requested it. Just… not today.

The guards didn’t bother verifying. One of them gestured to the chair against the stone wall. “Wait here. He’s in a meeting.”

Perfect.

Haldric nodded, sat, and waited.

Or rather—listened.

At first, everything was muffled.

A steady murmur of voices, broken by occasional pauses. The stone walls did their job well.

But then—a shift.

A sharper voice. Raised. Not The Prime’s

Haldric’s back straightened slightly, feet firm against the floor. His heart kicked up just a fraction.

Then, through the thick wooden door— “. . . are you bolting doing!?” Godric’s voice, rough, edged with barely restrained anger.

Muffled response. Too low to make out.

Another pause. Longer this time.

Then—sharper, more direct— “You asked me to take over this position!”

A murmur of words followed, but still too quiet.

Haldric’s fingers tightened against his knee.

Then the words that made his stomach drop. “…DOING TO THEM?!”

A spike of cold unease. Them? He wished he could hear what The Prime was saying. Only Godric spoke loud enough to catch.

The pause that followed was tense—even through the wall, Haldric could feel it.

Then, one last snap— “IF I FIND OUT YOU—”

The door creaked open.

Haldric’s pulse jumped. He immediately hunched forward, feigning disinterest, flipping open his stack of papers as if the reports actually mattered.

From his lowered gaze, he saw Godric exit. He didn’t slam the door but the way he walked—sharp, fast, his hands curled into fists at his sides, said everything.

The moment the door closed behind him, he didn’t even glance at the guards. Didn’t look at anyone.

He just left.

Haldric forced himself to stay still, resisting the urge to watch Godric disappear down the hall. Instead, he let his gaze drop to the floor, mind racing.

What had The Prime said? What was happening? And most importantly—who was ‘them’?

A voice snapped him from his thoughts.

“Haldric.”

One of The Prime’s attendants addressed him. “The Prime Archon will see you now.”

Haldric swallowed, schooling his expression into neutrality. No hesitation. No visible nerves. He stood, adjusting the paper in his hands like it was the only thing that mattered, and stepped inside.

The silence in the office felt different.

Not the usual, weighted kind of silence that accompanied The Prime’s presence. This was cracked—uneven, like something had shaken the room just before Haldric stepped in.

And maybe it had.

     The Prime Archon’s office was Stormhaven in miniature: pristine, controlled, and cold. The desk, carved from a single slab of dark stone, was polished to perfection, its surface nearly bare, as if clutter itself was beneath him. Behind him, a massive carved relief of the city loomed, a precise map etched into the rock, every district accounted for, every tunnel controlled.

     And yet, for the first time, the man sitting at the desk did not match his domain.

     His fingers pressed too firmly against the papers before him. His collar was slightly askew—not enough to be immediately noticeable, but just uneven enough to feel off. A set of deep creases ran down the center of his tunic, not the usual folds from wear, but bunched in a way that didn’t seem natural. His sleeve bore a faint wrinkle near the elbow, as if it had been shifted, pushed, or gripped at some point.

Haldric wasn’t supposed to notice these things.

But he did.

And before anything else, The Prime exhaled sharply, his upper lip curling slightly in disgust.

“Blasted Hunters,” he muttered, almost spitting the word before catching himself.

Haldric’s pulse jumped.

The Prime never did that.

For all his control, all his public-facing image, the words came out like a slur. Haldric had never seen The Prime display open disdain like this before. 

But just as fast as the slip happened, it was gone. The Prime Archon straightened, his expression smoothing back into its usual, unreadable mask.

Haldric hadn’t realized he was staring until he felt his own throat tighten. He forced himself to keep his posture steady. No reaction. Nothing out of the ordinary.



But something of his reaction must have shown, because The Prime’s gaze flickered up, just for a second, sharp, assessing.

Don’t acknowledge it, Haldric thought. Move on.

He swallowed and opened his mouth, only to realize that the reason he had come here sounded much weaker now than it had in his head.

He had an excuse. Something about a report. But standing here, with the tension still thick in the air, it suddenly felt ridiculous.

The moment stretched.

Too long.

And before Haldric could stop himself, before he could pull together the excuse he had planned—the words that came out were not the ones he intended.

“Are you okay?”

The Prime stiffened.

Then, almost amused—“Ahh, you heard that, did you?”

Haldric’s stomach dropped. 

Idiot.

He had just drawn attention to something he should have pretended not to notice. The first rule of working in the system: don’t acknowledge things above your station.

He clenched his jaw, already kicking himself internally.

Stupid, stupid, stupid. Fix it. Now.

He straightened, forcing a quick breath through his nose before shaking his head. “I… I didn’t hear anything, sir.”

Too fast, stupid!

The Prime Archon let out a short chuckle, his composure snapping back into place. He adjusted his sleeve, smoothing out a crease as if that was the only thing worth fixing.

  “Son, it is quite all right. I won’t tell your superior.”

  The way he said it was almost amused, like he was indulging a joke only he understood. He leaned back slightly, tilting his head.

  “Hmm, how long has it been since the two of us spoke like this?”

  Haldric shrugged, too nervous to speak.

  The Prime gave a slow, thoughtful nod in return.

  “Funny, you might very well be the first person to worry about my well-being since the previous Prime Archon named me his Heir. Thank you, Haldric.”

  Haldric hesitated.

  The Prime Archon wasn’t as untouchable as he had always seemed. There was something in his voice, not just amusement, but… something.

  “Right, I apologize for my son and what happened during your Testing. It wasn’t supposed to be that way,” The Prime said, bowing his head bitterly.

  Maybe The Prime wasn’t as in control as people thought. Maybe he was being pushed around in his own position.

The thought had barely formed when Haldric blurted out, “Are all Hunters that much of an asshole, or is Godric a special case?”

Wait.

Did I just say that out loud? Inner thoughts, idiot! Inner. Thoughts.

A slow blink from the leader of the city.

Then—a smirk.

“Ahh, yes. Godric. Mythical Beast Hunter.” He exhaled through his nose, shaking his head. “They say never meet your heroes, lest you find out how much of a fraud they really are.”

A pause.

“That, was certainly the case for me.”

His eyes flickered, not just toward Haldric, but through him, past him, as if recalling something long buried.

Then, just as quickly, he sat back in his chair, his expression smoothing over, settling into something unbothered.

As if none of it had happened at all.

“Regardless, what is it that you wish to speak to me about?”

Haldric clenched his fists for a fraction of a second, then forced himself back into control.

This was his moment to regain composure.

Focus. Stick to the plan. Ignore what just happened.

He shifted his weight slightly, adjusting his stance into something professional, something that felt like he hadn’t just embarrassed himself.

And then, as if the moment before hadn’t happened at all, he finally got to the reason he came. After receiving The Prime’s stamps, he returned to his desk and spent the rest of the day sorting meaningless reports.

By the time the workday ended, Haldric’s mind was buzzing. Not with reports—but with the image of Godric’s face. The tension in his shoulders. The way his hands had curled into fists at his sides.

Something had happened in that meeting.

Something big.

But Haldric was just a clerk. A paper-pusher. Whatever this was, it wasn’t his concern.

At least, that’s what he told himself.

Still…

As he stepped out onto the streets, the weight of the day pressing against his shoulders, he felt something stir—a sensation he hadn’t realized had been missing. A quiet hum beneath his skin, an old instinct waking up.

Three weeks ago, it had slipped away, buried under routine and obligation.



But now?

Now, the pieces weren’t adding up. The whispers in the records, the tension in The Prime’s office, the missing people—it was all leading somewhere.

The truth was out there, hidden in the dark. Waiting.

And for the first time since taking this job, he felt the pull of something greater.

The thrill of the hunt.
