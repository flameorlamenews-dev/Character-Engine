Chapter 1 - The Hunter Exam





     Praew closed the book for the last time, saying goodbye to the story that raised her.

     She and her two best friends had made a pact that after the test, they would never read the story again. Because today would determine if they got to face the storm or remain stuck behind the gates forever.

     A faint hum filled the waiting hall, a steady murmur of excitement, nerves, and whispered bets between hopefuls. The walls were lit by the soft, warm glow of SolarStones embedded at even intervals along the ceiling. The stairs, the podium, the tables and desks, even the long bench they sat on had all been carved directly from the stone floor, destined to remain forever stationary, trapped and unchanging.

     Funny, she thought. Furniture has always been that way, but why does it suddenly feel so… wrong, like the rest of this dreary dungeon they masquerade as a city?

     Praew’s fingers tapped her knee, foot bouncing as she scanned the room. She wasn’t the only one tense; most of the would-be Hunters sat stiffly, whispering or staring off like they were waiting for a final meal.

She brushed a pink curl behind her ear, already feeling it frizz from the tension. With bright hair, blushing cheeks, and eyes people always said “sparkled,” whatever that meant. She apparently looked like the type to “stand aside and cheer for others.”

Yeah. Not the vibe she was going for, but she embraced it anyway. At least if she tripped over her boots, she’d look adorable doing it.

Since they’d all turned twenty this year, she couldn’t help but notice how everyone towered over her, especially the boys, with muscles they seemed to grow effortlessly. Meanwhile, tired, sick, or injured, she still trained every day without fail, only to see slower results. It wasn’t easy being a girl expected to compete with male strength.

She caught herself.

No! I will not use my height or gender as an excuse to fail. If I can’t accomplish something the others can, it’s my own fault for not training harder.

Praew looked for reassurance from her two best friends. Haldric Tosa, a pale young man with messy black hair, met her gaze with an annoyingly infectious grin. Top of the class with both sword and spear, he was easily the most likely to pass the Hunter Test.

Asher, on the other hand, was tall and lanky, with slicked-back sandy blond hair. He sat at the stone desk, still flipping through the well-worn pages of Srevlis, the Mythical Beast Hunter.

Didn’t they start reading at the same time? I hadn’t even sat down yet. So how did a muscle-head like Haldric finish before a book lover like Asher?

It took her less than a breath to figure it out. “Again, Asher?” Praew teased.

Asher didn’t even glance up. “It’s just hard to put it down. For good.”

“I know,” Praew agreed. “I almost teared up.”

“Zephyrs take me, you guys are so dramatic,” scoffed a voice behind her.

Praew recognized it instantly. Renwick lounged against the stone bench, hands tucked behind his head, long black hair tied back, his freckled nose tilted smugly toward the ceiling.

     That same condescending smirk he always wore was waiting for her.

     “Seriously? Crying over your little promise? This is why women shouldn’t be Hunters. Too emotional. It’s a kid’s book. Grow up.”

     Before Praew could reply, Asher snapped his book shut with a loud thump. “Tell me, Renwick, do you strive to be the most hated person in every room, or is that a privilege you reserve exclusively for us?” 

     Asher let out a snort. 

     “I can’t wait for Praew to become a Hunter while you get sent to the mines. Oh, and this book? It’s fourteen years old, yet it still inspires the whole bolting city. Who isn’t here because of it?”

     Renwick rolled his eyes. “It’s a bedtime story for delusional kids who want to play pretend as Hunters.” He glanced at Praew, then at Asher. “No wonder you two are obsessed with it.”

     Asher scoffed, and Haldric leaned forward, arms draped over his knees. “So what about me? Do you think I’m playing pretend as well?”

     Praew shot Renwick a victorious grin, but before the conversation could continue, the large wooden doors at the front of the chamber creaked open. 

A real Hunter stepped into the waiting hall, his presence instantly commanding the room, with a proctor in tow.

     Neither wasted time on introductions. The proctor stepped forward and spoke first.

     “You have all worked hard to reach the end of your education. For seventeen years, you trained in combat, math, science, and more. Each of you has prepared for every labor role this city might assign you. You all know this is non-negotiable. Insubordination is treason.”

“AND JUSTICE WE WILL DELIVER!” the students chanted in unison.

The proctor continued. “Stormhaven thanks you for your dedication.

“Our underground city is one of the last human sanctuaries remaining. A treacherous world lies above, where beasts, storms, and even the very stones that litter the ground can kill the strongest of men. Those who brave the surface to hunt resources for the city are called Hunters.

“Now, before your role is announced, you have the legal right to take the Hunters’ test. No matter the outcome, we thank you for your bravery and willingness to fight for your city.”

The Hunter stepped forward and cleared his throat.

“There are two parts to the test,” he announced. His voice hit like a shockwave, shaking the chamber. Dust and pebbles fell from the ceiling, pelting the hopefuls below.

How did he— but before Praew could finish the thought, the Hunter continued, quieter this time.

“The first is a knowledge exam. Pass it, and you move on to the practical. Fail, and you’re dismissed.”

Murmurs rippled through the crowd, but the Hunter ignored them and went on.

“The test will be conducted alone. Each of you will enter one at a time. If the proctor calls your name, enter the door to my right. That’s all you need to know.”

Without another word, he turned and exited through the wooden door.

A tense silence settled over the room. One by one, names were called as hopeful, would-be Hunters disappeared behind the wooden doors.

“Paew… uh… pon?” the proctor called.

Of course he got her name wrong. They always did. That’s why she went by Praew instead of Praewphan.

She rolled her eyes. What am I even doing here? This was a bad idea. I’m just going to—

Her spiral broke the moment a hand touched her shoulder. Haldric.

She turned, and both he and Asher were grinning like idiots. Without a word, they held up their hands, thumb and pinky tucked in, leaving three fingers extended.

She mirrored them. Together, they pressed their hands in a triangle of contact:

Her pointer met Haldric’s, his third touched Asher’s, and Asher’s pointer pressed against her third, all three middle fingers meeting in the center to form a triangle, the symbol of their unshakable bond and their unbreakable promise.

Haldric grinned. “No matter the weather,” he began.

“Together we reign,” they finished, voices low but a hint of resolve brewed beneath their uncertainty.

What would I do without these two?

The wooden door creaked as Praew stepped inside. Her hands were clenched into fists, but her heart was ready now.

 The room was small, suffocating. A single dim SolarStone flickered atop the desk. The Hunter sat alone with his quill poised over parchment.

“Name three Sifaralith Stones and their functions,” he commanded, barely looking up.

Praew took a deep breath. “SolarStone, EchoStone, ZephyrStone.”

The Hunter gestured for her to continue.

“SolarStones emit light or heat used for visibility, warmth, or signaling. 

“EchoStones produce loud or chaotic sounds, depending on what it recorded during its trigger. It is also how we record and to authenticate messages.

“ZephyrStones generate bursts of wind, useful for movement, propulsion, or controlling airflow.”

A small scratch of ink. The Hunter made a note.



***



Asher rolled his shoulders, keeping his posture loose. No nerves, not today.

“Three stones and their functions, list them”

“SurgeStone, BlastStone, TerraStone.”

The proctor raised an eyebrow.

“SurgeStones boosts nearby stones for a bit, then drains their charge afterwards. They’re usually saved for emergencies.

 “BlastStones generate a sonic boom with immense force. They can be used for powerful ranged attacks, stunning enemies, or breaking obstacles.

“TerraStones create tunnels or incinerate earth for shelter. It’s nearly impossible for a tunnel created this way to collapse.”

A small mark on the parchment, but nothing more.



***



Haldric stood, arms crossed, gaze unwavering. He had trained for this moment. He already knew his answer before the question left the Hunter’s lips.

“Name three-”

“ForceStone, BoltStone, NullStone.” Haldric interrupted.

The Hunter’s quill didn’t move. “Define them.”

“ForceStones can store, stack, and release impacts —enhancing strikes, breaking armor, or building traps. 

“BoltStones generate and release electricity, surging through weapons, tools, or even people.

“NullStones will dampen or deactivate the effects of other stones within range.”

The Hunter finally made a mark on the parchment.



***



“Next question”



The proctor’s eyes narrowed slightly. “Praew, was it? Explain to me how stones trigger?”

She leaned forward slightly, eager to answer.

“Every stone has a unique trigger, and they are always activated by one of the ten stone abilities. That trigger might come from another stone or an external force, but it will always align with one of the ten properties.”

The Hunter remained silent, so she continued.

“No two stones are alike. You can have two EchoStones, but their size, rarity, and trigger could be completely different. One might activate with light, another with sound. It’s why identifying a stone’s individual trigger is just as important as knowing its type.”

She hesitated briefly. “That’s also why surface-dwelling stones are the most dangerous. If a Solar-triggered EchoStone gets caught in sunlight, it could set off an entire chain reaction. A bad enough combination could take out an entire hunting party before anyone realized what was happening.”

For the first time, the Hunter’s expression shifted. Not much, but enough to note approval.

He made a mark on the parchment.



***



“Now, explain a combination technique using two of the stones you listed.”

Haldric didn’t hesitate, his voice steady.

“BoltStone and NullStone. A BoltStone triggered by pressure makes a shockingly effective spearhead.”

Silence. The Hunter’s stare was flat, unimpressed.

Haldric cleared his throat, shifting slightly. “A NullStone embedded in the weapon shaft prevents the wielder from getting electrocuted.”

The Hunter gave a short nod. “Destructive.”



***



Asher grinned, leaning back slightly in his chair. “TerraStone is too slow. If you need to dig quickly for shelter, a SurgeStone can force the TerraStone to react instantly, letting you carve a tunnel within seconds.”

The Hunter made a note. “Efficient.”



***



Praew knew the final question. She’d rehearsed her answer for months now.

The proctor’s quill hovered, waiting.

She took a deep breath. “Solar and Echo. If you have a SolarStone triggered by sound and a EchoStone triggered by light, you can make a loop. Clap once, the SolarStone flashes, which triggers the EchoStone, which makes a sound—resetting the loop. You get endless light until you cover the EchoStone.”

The Hunter’s quill hesitated before marking the parchment. “Clever”.

As Praew finished her answer, the door creaked open behind her. She turned slightly, just enough to see the proctor step inside, a black bag clutched in his hands.

Without a word, he stepped forward and placed the bag directly into the Hunter’s hands.

The Hunter accepted it without acknowledgment, undoing the clasp and peeling back the opening just enough to see inside.

A dim red glow flickered against his face.



Praew’s breath hitched. A sudden, suffocating weight pressed against her chest—unexplained emotions that weren’t hers were clawing their way inside.

Alarm. Fear. Hatred. Longing. Sadness. Defiance. 

Her vision blurred. A single tear slipped down her cheek before she could stop it.

The Hunter snapped the bag shut, his expression unreadable. He gave a small nod to the proctor, who picked the bag back up and left without a word, the door clicking softly shut behind him.

Praew blinked rapidly, dragging the sleeve of her uniform across her cheek. She opened her mouth slightly, but the Hunter didn’t give her the chance to speak.

“NEXT!”

The sudden bark of his voice made her flinch. She stood quickly, moving automatically as she walked to the door, her thoughts tangled.

It’s nothing. Just nerves.

She had built this test up in her mind for years—of course her body was shaken. That had to be it. By the time she stepped into the hall, she had already buried the thought.

The waiting hall was quieter now. The murmurs had faded, replaced by anxious, uneven breathing. A few hopefuls still whispered among themselves, but most sat still, their gazes locked onto the doors ahead. Praew clenched her hands into fists, the anticipation gnawing at her.

The heavy doors swung open.

The Hunter stepped into the hall, his presence once more demanding the attention of every person in the room. He wasted no time.

“If I call your name, you passed. The rest of you, leave.”

Silence.

Then—

“Renwick.”

The jerk beside her let out an exaggerated sigh of relief, stretching his arms. “As if there was ever a doubt,” he muttered, casting a smug glance at the others.

“Haldric.”

Haldric exhaled sharply, nodding once. His posture was stiff, but the tension in his shoulders eased slightly.

“Korven.”

A tall, broad-shouldered boy at the far end of the room straightened. His expression remained stoic, but Praew caught the flicker of satisfaction in his eyes.

“Edrik.”

Another boy, thinner, wiry, grinned, jabbing his friend in the ribs. “Told you,” he muttered, shoving to his feet.

A pause.

“Praew.”

Her breath caught.

For a moment, she didn’t move. Didn’t react. Then, slowly, she let out the breath she didn’t realize she’d been holding.

She’d done it. 

She was still in the running.

Finally—

“Asher.”

Praew glanced toward her friend. He didn’t react immediately. Just nodded, his easy confidence more subdued than usual.

The Hunter’s gaze swept the remaining hopefuls.

“You were given a chance. You failed.” His tone was impassive. “There are no second attempts.”

The weight of his words settled over the room. The ones not called slowly stood, some with their heads bowed, others with clenched jaws, holding back frustration or disappointment.

Praew didn’t watch them leave.

She couldn’t.

She was too busy processing the reality of it.

Six left.

Only six.

The Hunter wasted no time. “On your feet,” he commanded.

A few shuffled awkwardly, but everyone obeyed.

“The first test is done. You passed.” His gaze flicked between them, assessing. Measuring. “Now we see if you’re truly worth sending to the surface.”

 He turned sharply, gesturing for them to follow.

No further explanation.

No hesitation.

The six of them moved, stepping into the unknown.

The corridor leading deeper underground was long and winding, the air thick with damp stone and flickering SolarStone light. Shadows stretched along the uneven walls, and the further they went, the heavier the silence became.

Renwick, of course, was the first to break the silence. “Six left, huh? Guess that means fewer idiots to slow me down.”

Praew didn’t reply. He wasn’t talking to her anyway. He just enjoyed hearing his own voice.

The path narrowed, the ceiling pressing lower as they moved deeper into the mountain’s core. A presence settled beside her. Asher.

For a moment, neither of them spoke until Praew finally asked, “You okay?”

It took him too long to answer. “Yeah,” he muttered.

Liar.

She didn’t press further. Not now.

Ahead, the corridor widened again. The Hunter leading them came to an abrupt halt. Then, without a word, he stepped aside.

Praew barely had time to process the sudden shift before she saw it. The space beyond them was massive.

The underground training hall was enormous, far larger than she had expected. The ceiling stretched high above, rough-hewn stone arching over the expanse. SolarStones flickered overhead, their golden light barely reaching the depths of the battlefield below.

And the battlefield was nothing like she imagined.  It was a maze.

Twisting stone formations, jagged outcrops, unstable platforms. Paths wound between sharp inclines and sudden drops, the entire course built for combat, survival, and calculated brutality.

A stone platform overlooked the field with seating carved into the rock as the waiting area.

Above them all in the highest seat, watching with quiet authority was The Prime Archon, the leader of the city.

Praew swallowed hard.

I can do this!

The candidates took their seats. Praew sat alone. Haldric and Asher sat in front. Her hands clenched into fists in her lap. Her heartbeat thundered in her ears. As she tried to steady her nerves, Renwick sat next to her.  Of course it had to be the most insufferable person she had ever met. She always hated the way he would stare at her. 

This was it. The second and final test. Fail here, and you were out. Forever. 

The Hunter stepped forward, his sharp gaze sweeping the room. His voice rang out, crisp and final. “There are times when you will run into Hunters from other cities.”



A ripple of unease spread through the gathered hopefuls as the Hunter continued, “This test will simulate that.”

Praew glanced at Renwick. The Hunter’s next words made her stomach twist. “The person next to you is a Hunter from another city.”

She swallowed. She hadn’t realized she’d been holding her breath.

“There is a stone for each of you,” the Hunter continued. “Work together or independently, I don’t care. Just retrieve them both.” No further explanation or rules were given.

Praew’s stomach churned. Why does it have to be this guy?

The first pair rose, Korven and Edrik. They shared a glance, then strode down the steps into the training field.

A horn sounded. The test began.

Praew watched intently.

The two hopefuls moved as one, sprinting into the maze-like course, ducking between towering stone pillars with wordless understanding. They scanned the field, assessing threats, working in tandem. Edrik leapt onto a ledge and spotted a BlastStone embedded in the wall. He gestured to Korven, signaling caution.

Korven kicked a EchoStone into a ditch, triggering a distant explosion. A distraction. Edrik used a ZephyrStone to propel them both onto a high ridge, where he scanned the battlefield and directed their next move. They reached the pedestals together and, without hesitation, each took a stone.

Praew narrowed her eyes. They were avoiding the dangers quite effortlessly.

The Hunter gave a small nod. “Impressive communication.”

Praew exhaled slowly. This was doable.

The two returned, victorious.

Then, Asher and Haldric stood, striding side by side down the steps into the arena.

Praew felt proud knowing her two friends would pass. They were the most skilled in her graduating class.

Why does no one think I’ll succeed? Not my parents, not even my friends.

I’m skilled too! When I focus, I’m just as good as Asher.

She was sick of being teased for being clumsy. Bump into a few things, hit a few corners, get a few bruises, and suddenly everyone forgot she scored second-highest in both hand-to-hand and spear combat training.

The horn sounded, pulling her back to the test.

The two bolted forward, sprinting into the maze. Their teamwork was unmatched. Asher called out hazards, Haldric led the charge. A SolarStone embedded in a wall flared to life, reacting to their movement. Haldric threw up an arm, shielding his eyes. Asher, quicker, shoved a piece of cloth over the stone, killing its glow before it could trigger something worse.

Praew released a breath. They were doing it. Making even quicker progress than the first group.

Then they hit the obstacle.

A wide gap stretched before them, a pit of jagged stone and unstable terrain below. The stones were on the other side.

Asher made the jump first, barely catching the edge, pulling himself up. Haldric leapt next. He didn’t make it. His fingers scraped against the rock. He slipped.

“Stone me!” His legs dangled, boots kicking uselessly against the wall. “Asher—”

Their eyes met. Haldric reached out.

Asher hesitated.

His fingers twitched at his side, half-reaching, half-still. His gaze flicked to The Prime Archon watching from above, then back to Haldric. His friend’s hand was open, waiting. Asher looked torn. He could pull him up. He should pull him up.

Then his eyes darted to the stones.

And he turned away.

Praew froze. What? No. No, no, no. What are you doing, Asher!?

Haldric’s grip faltered. He scrambled, his expression shifting from shock to fury. “Asher—Asher!”

Asher grabbed both stones.

Praew’s stomach dropped.

Haldric’s arms trembled as he pulled himself up, rage in his eyes, breath coming fast and sharp. Asher wouldn’t meet his gaze.

The Hunter’s voice echoed across the chamber. “The test has ended.”

Praew’s pulse roared in her ears. She saw it—the moment it settled in Haldric’s mind. The moment he realized his friend had chosen to leave him behind.

The air was heavy.

Haldric didn’t move. His breath came in sharp, shallow bursts. His fingers curled against the rock, pressing so hard they turned white. He looked up. Asher still wouldn’t meet his gaze.

Haldric’s nails dug into his palms. His breath was ragged, shoulders rising and falling with barely contained rage. His jaw clenched so tight it ached. His fists trembled.

“That’s not fair!” His voice cracked, half-roar, half-wound. “He took my stone!”

The Hunter dismissed Haldric with a lazy flick of the hand. “It counts,” he said, using his other fist to cover a yawn.

A muscle ticked in Haldric’s jaw. His whole body was coiled, every inch of him screaming to hit something. “That’s bolting convenient. Wouldn't you agree, Asher?" He was barely holding back the urge to make Asher bleed.

Asher didn’t say a word. Didn’t look at Haldric. Didn’t even glance her way. Praew felt sick.

Her name was called. She stood. The stone steps felt heavier beneath her feet.

Renwick chuckled beside her. “Looks like teamwork’s off the table, sweetheart.”

She didn’t respond. She was too focused on not thinking about Asher, not thinking about Haldric’s face.

The horn sounded.

Renwick lunged ahead, grinning. “Try to keep up!”

Praew bolted after him. The course was a battlefield. SolarStones flared unpredictably, BlastStones trembled, waiting for a trigger. She had to move fast, move smart.

Renwick wasn’t fast, but he was cruel. “Wow,” he said, panting, “seems like you got a lot on your chest.”

She ignored him.

“Bet it’s hard running with all that weight.”

Her teeth clenched. He was too close. She needed to get ahead.

She spotted a half-buried ZephyrStone. Perfect. She leapt onto it, triggering a gust of wind, and launched forward.

For a moment, she was winning. Actually winning. A thrill shot through her. No one would ever doubt or mock her again. 

Renwick’s voice came smooth, mocking. “Damn. Not a bad view from back here, either.”

Praew hesitated. Just for a second, but that’s all it took.

She faltered. Her foot caught a stone. She stumbled.

Zephyrs take me!

Renwick flew past her, laughing. The pedestals were in sight.

She scrambled, lunged, and collided with him. They tumbled across the stone floor.

Panicked, she reached for something, anything to subdue him.

The BlastStone triggered. A shockwave tore through the field, sending her flying.

She barely registered Renwick laughing. Barely saw him snatch both stones.

He turned, smirked, and winked. “Better luck next life, sweetheart.”

Praew stumbled back into the risen seats. She barely heard the Hunter’s voice. “You must beat your competition. By any means necessary.”

She barely heard Haldric’s fists trembling. Barely noticed Asher, staring at the ground.

Her mind roared.

“Two actually managed to pass this year,” the Hunter said. “Renwick and Asher.”

Wait… just two? Why not four?

“One person had to take both stones. No city can afford to let another claim what they need to survive. We do not share. We win.”

The others looked exactly how she felt, horrified.

Praew stared at the ground in shock.

I… failed. No second chances. No appeals.

The Hunter’s words rang in her skull as he asked the students, “Could you live with yourself, knowing you only received a Common Stone, and let another city walk away with something more rare? That’s why a Hunter must always aim to claim every stone for himself.”

The weight of it settled in. Praew’s hands curled into fists. Her heart was still racing. 

And Renwick just smiled.
