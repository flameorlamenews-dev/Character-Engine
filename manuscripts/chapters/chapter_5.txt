Chapter 5 - Deafening Silence



Praew’s eyes fluttered open in the quiet, pre-morning stillness of her home. The plush embrace of her luxurious bed tempted her to linger, but she slipped out with practiced coordination.

“Ouch,” she breathed through gritted teeth, rubbing her leg where she bumped it on the corner of the bed.

Coordinated or not, without the natural dawn they spoke of in storybooks about the surface, this hour still belonged to her. No one else was crazy enough to start their day this early by choice. After quietly getting ready, careful not to wake her parents, she slipped out of the house.

As Praew moved through the familiar streets, she encountered a girl whose bubbly chatter broke the silence. The girl had a warm, inviting smile, and her dark almond-shaped eyes sparkled with an infectious energy. Her jet-black hair bounced as she walked, framing her golden-brown skin that seemed to glow even in the dim corridor light.

“Hi! You’re up early too! What’s your name? I love these quiet mornings, don’t you?” the girl chattered cheerfully, whether Praew responded or not.

Praew offered a few grunts when absolutely necessary, silently wishing the girl would take a different path. There was nothing wrong with her—it’s just that one of the main reasons Praew got up this early was specifically to avoid talking to people before breakfast.

Still, she had to admit—there was a flicker of curiosity, even amusement, at the girl’s boundless energy. Zephyrs take me, who had this much enthusiasm so early?

They reached the cafeteria, which stood empty and quiet. The girl declined breakfast with a quick wave and a bright smile, disappearing down another hallway. Praew grabbed a portable grain bar, swiftly collecting her breakfast and continuing on to class.

She stepped into the empty classroom from yesterday. In the doorway to her left sat an embedded stone desk, positioned at the front of the class for the professor. A  large slate board for writing was embedded in the wall behind the desk, and still no chairs for the students. Zephyrs take me! Are we expected to squat for every class? Praew silently complained as she walked to her spot. Rounding the desk, she yelped and jumped back in alarm. She wasn’t alone. Near the other side of the desk, dressed entirely in black shoes, shirt, socks, gloves, and a featureless mask, a figure was seated cross-legged on the floor, motionless. The mask had no eye holes or features, only a strange symbol painted on it. Could they even see out of that creepy thing?

“Um, good morning,” Praew ventured, her voice soft. The figure didn’t respond, remaining perfectly still. What was with this person? Feeling a bit awkward, Praew quietly sat down, mirroring the figure’s posture, and waited as the rest of the class began to arrive.

     Assuming it might be some kind of test, Praew decided to mimic the figure’s silence, crossing her legs and closing her eyes. 

     After what felt like forever, another student entered. They—no, she greeted the silent figure first and received no response. Was that Junya? She asked Praew what they were supposed to do. Praew simply kept her eyes closed, offering no answer. 

     Praew peeked her eye open just enough to see Junya mimicking her, sitting down to meditate. Praew and Junya caused a ripple effect as students trickled in. One by one, they sat down to meditate in silence, until the entire class was seated quietly. A chain reaction of stillness spread through the room.

     Praew’s eyes snapped open to a piercing, high-pitched screech. As she blinked away the shock, she saw the masked figure at the front, dragging a dagger across the slate board to produce that grating noise. The sound jolted everyone out of their meditation.

     The figure picked up a slender stone from a ledge beside the board and began to write. As they pressed it against the slate, the stone crumbled slightly, leaving a trail of white streaks that formed the letters. “Name: Instructor”—there was a noticeable hesitation—then they added “Shadow” in a deliberate, bold script. Below that, they wrote “Shadow Recon Instructor.” The room remained completely silent as everyone took in the name and title, the figure’s presence commanding an eerie, authoritative aura.

The masked figure’s hand moved swiftly, inscribing on the board: “From now on, any speaking during class time will result in immediate failure of this class.”

Praew glanced around, noting the wide eyes and tense postures of her classmates. Some exchanged anxious glances, while others sat perfectly still, absorbing the gravity of the rule.

The figure then inscribed the second rule: “No questions will be tolerated in this class. If there’s anything you do not understand, figure it out by any means necessary or prepare to fail the class.”

By any means necessary? Praew thought. How were they supposed to learn anything if they couldn’t even ask questions?

She shifted her weight, arms crossed, as she began tapping her fingers against her bicep, slowly at first, then faster. She wasn’t sure if this was exciting or terrifying. Probably both.

Instructor Shadow never stopped writing. Snapping back into focus, Praew continued to read.

Week 1 – Silent Movement

Week 2 – Tailing

Week 3 – Information Gathering

Week 4 – Infiltration

Each word was carved out with the same crisp clarity, no hesitation. The stone the instructor held clicked once more, then stopped.

Praew felt sweat bead on her forehead. Though she wasn’t squatting this time, her legs began to shake from the weight of something even worse—dread.

Everyone knows I’m clumsy. Why in the bolting seven stars am I in a class that specializes in everything I’m bad at? I can’t talk. I can’t ask questions. And I’m supposed to just figure it all out on my own? Zephyrs take me, well, you might as well fail me now and save yourself the trou—

A click snapped her back into focus as a hidden stone door scraped open along the far-right wall, directly beside the spot where the instructor had been meditating earlier.

The instructor beckoned for them to follow, then turned without a word and stepped into the hidden passageway. One by one, they moved after her, disappearing into the narrow tunnel beyond.

The room was massive. Dimly lit, built in the same style as the one before, just stretched out to impossible size. On the left wall stood another slate board, embedded clean into the stone like before. But it was the right side that drew her attention.

Two narrow pathways looped along the far edges of the room, but in the center was an obstacle course unlike anything Praew had ever seen.

Directly ahead, a stretch of stone ran forward, lined on both sides by unlit, embedded, SolarStones. The path began with the kind of worn, lightly textured stone she was used to seeing in the city—solid, dependable, with just enough traction underfoot. Then it shifted abruptly into rough terrain, jagged and uneven, with chunks of rock embedded at awkward angles. Past that, the stone smoothed into something dangerously polished—like the finish of a well-kept blade.

Just beyond that lay a patch of what looked like sand. She only recognized it because of her father’s work, though she’d never seen this kind of brown sand before.

The rest of the room faded into shadow. Whatever came next was swallowed by the dark.

Another ear-breaking screech snapped her focus back to the instructor.

The dagger scraped across the slate board again—just like before—pulling every eye toward it. While they were distracted, the instructor had already filled the board with a detailed outline of their upcoming test. Or perhaps it was already there.

Praew’s eyes scanned the writing:



FINAL TEST: SILENT COURSE + INFILTRATION

Objective:

Move silently through the course. If you complete it, you will be given a clue to identify a target and a specific item they carry. You must obtain this item without being caught. Completion of both tasks is required to pass this class.

Terrain Test: Four Stages

You may attempt this course once per week. You are allowed six attempts total. The sooner you pass, the more time you will have to complete the second half of the test. Time matters.

Stage 1 – Stone Path

A stretch of stone lined with embedded SolarStones—triggered by sound. It begins with the kind of rough-textured stone common in the city, shifts into jagged, uneven rock, and ends with smooth, polished stone.

Walk without making a sound. If any stone glows…Simple. Just pretend she wasn’t born with thunder in her feet.



Stage 2 – Sand Track

A narrow stretch of brown sand with hidden ZephyrStones buried throughout. The trigger is still sound—too much noise, and a gust of wind will lash through the path, throwing sand like needles across your skin.

Praew imagined coughing up grit before she even stepped in.



Stage 3 – Grass Field

Tall grass covers the area. Hidden beneath are BlastStones, control your sound. If triggered, the stones will explode beneath your feet and launch you backward. Severe injury is likely.

Great! How many bones am I going to break before I pass?



Stage 4 – Water Crossing

A shallow stream scattered with uneven stones. BoltStones lurk below the surface. One loud sound, and they’ll discharge—while you’re submerged.

That one pulled her attention. She swallowed hard. Was this a second chance or a death sentence? Only a lunatic would stay. 

She should’ve walked away. Should’ve bolted. Yet her feet just stood there—quietly defiant, like they knew something she didn’t. They didn’t speak, of course. But somehow, they still managed to say, “we’ve got this.”

And the ridiculous part? She almost believed it.



Final Test- Treason

You are being tested on your courage and your ability to move, observe, and adapt in total silence. If you pass, you will be given a clue. That clue will point you to your target, a government official or a Hunter.

You must identify the person, study their behavior, find the right moment, and steal the correct item. You may only steal once. If you are caught, just like the surface, failure may result in imprisonment—or death. The Shadow Recon Department will never protect an operative from the repercussions of their own failure. That said, they will never allow you to face judgement from a mission well done.

Though you have six weeks to complete the test, don’t forget—the sooner you succeed, the more time you’ll have to trail your assigned target. The more time you spend observing them, the better your chances of retrieving the correct item. If you wait until the sixth week to pass, you may only have days—maybe a few flashes—to study your target. That is nearly impossible—and has only ever been accomplished by one Shadow before.

“This has to be a joke.”

Jorpen, a usually quiet student with eyes that looked like they’d never known sleep, blurted out without remorse.

“You want me to risk my life walking through water? You expect me to steal from a bolting Hunter, or a government agent? And if we get caught, you won’t even protect us? You’re out of your bolting mind!”

The screech of the instructor’s knife against the slate board was louder than ever. A response was already written out for Jorpen: “You spoke. You have failed this class. Leave immediately.”

Jorpen ignored the words, turning to the others for support.

“You guys can’t seriously be considering this. Look at this! This is treason!”

He raised a clenched fist.

“And justice we will deliver!” he chanted—alone.

No one joined him.

He scanned the room slowly, locking eyes with each traitor.

The silence told him everything.

In this class, speaking meant failure. And every one of them had already made their decision. None would waste their second chance.

Jorpen took a breath, stepped forward, and froze in place. 

In the blink of an eye, the same dagger that had scraped the slate just moments ago now pressed against Jorpen’s neck.

Its tip pierced just enough to draw a single drop of blood. The class stared in shock as the blood slipped down his skin.

Silently, he raised his arms and allowed  the professor to march him out of the room.

No one dared speak while Instructor Shadow was gone. The nine students stood in awkward silence, exchanging glances while they waited.

Ten flickers later, the instructor returned in their usual silence. They entered the room and pinched the dagger between their forearm and bicep, wiping it clean across the crease of their inner elbow. Then they turned and began clearing the board.

Jorpen’s okay… right? Unease twisted in Praew's gut as she watched what looked like something a warrior might do after a kill. Glancing around at the others, she could tell—she wasn’t the only one thinking it.

The professor began writing again.

“If you wish to move silently, you must first choose the correct clothing.”

Another line followed immediately.

“Your goal is balance, clothing tight enough to muffle sound, but loose enough to hide your shape. Too tight, and it reveals your gender. Too loose, and it betrays your position.”

More scratches echoed through the room.

“People wear clothes to be seen. You wear them to disappear.”

Something about that line resonated with Praew. She paused, absorbing the meaning before continuing to read.

“Black is ideal: subtle, stylish, and it vanishes into darkness. Otherwise, choose colors that blend with your surroundings like gray or brown for cavern walls. Matching tones without drawing attention makes you harder to remember.

“If I point to you, go to the center of the stone test, stand in place, and flap your arms until I signal you to return to your spot.”

They turned and pointed at a student wearing layered, oversized wraps.

The student stepped into the center of the SolarStone path. As they began to move, lifting and lowering their arms with casual motion, the stones lit up on both sides, flaring in response to the rustling fabric.

Then, without a word, the professor stepped forward and took the same position.

They flailed their arms with exaggerated, almost mocking motion—far more reckless than the student had been, yet the stones didn’t flicker once.

Without pausing, the professor returned to the board. “Your shoes matter just as much. Soft soles on the inside aren’t enough. You need cushioning on the outside. Sound-absorbing outsoles are often the difference between life and death.”

They circled the final sentence twice 

“Silence begins before you take a step.

“We will now go over how to step. When I point at you, run to the other side of the stone test through the SolarStone path and back, as quickly and quietly as you can. Observe how much the SolarStones light up when you move.”

Professor Shadow turned and pointed at Praew.

She took off, doing her best to stay light on her feet. It didn’t help. The SolarStones flared beneath her with every step. Bright, unforgiving pulses that chased her across the path. When she reached the far side, the professor pointed again. She ran back. The stones lit up just as harshly, if not worse.

Each student followed one by one. Some sprinted, some hesitated, but the result didn’t change. Flash after flash lit up the path. Not one of them crossed without drawing light.

Once the class returned to the starting side, the professor turned back to the board.

“To run silently, lift your heel slightly, then roll your foot smoothly toward it—but never let the heel touch the ground. Each step should gently rock your foot from toe to mid-foot. Lift your toes at the end of each step, then place them down first to begin the next. This cushions impact and absorbs sound. Don’t lift your heel too high, or others may notice. It may feel awkward at first. Practice until it becomes second nature.”

The professor pointed again. Praew stepped forward, adjusting her form. She tried to focus—lifting her heel, rolling her foot just as instructed. The result was… slightly better. The stones still lit, not as explosively—until she tripped, collapsing hard in front of her classmates.

A few could barely contain their laughter, too scared to let it out in case it counted as speaking.

Good, she thought as she returned to her spot. Let them laugh. It’ll be that much more satisfying when I’m the first to pass every test.

The others followed. Some fumbled the rhythm entirely, others managed to dull the glow, but none crossed clean. Even the best attempts left a trail of flickering stone behind them.

The professor returned to the board.

“Walk to the other side and back when I point at you.”

Praew moved naturally—unadjusted—and the SolarStones lit with each step. The rest of the students were no better. The stones responded like a heartbeat, echoing every thump on the ground.

Another note followed. “Now walk on your toes. Raise your heels slightly—not enough to be noticed, just enough to reduce sound.”

She tried again, heel slightly lifted, careful to balance—but the stones still flared, just more lazily now. The others mirrored her. Some improved. Some didn’t. Not one made it across in silence.

Just when Praew began to think it was impossible, the professor stepped onto the path. They ran—quick and fluid. It looked like ordinary movement, yet not a single SolarStone triggered. As if NullStones were tied to their feet.

How did they make it look so easy?

Excitement stirred in her chest at the thought of mastering that kind of control.

The final message appeared on the slate.

“To pass each test, you must run through each path and walk back without triggering a single Sifaralith. Your first chance to attempt the test is in three days. Practice well. Class dismissed.”

By the time Praew finished reading the board, the professor was already gone.

None of the nine students said a word as they left the secret chamber. Bpen was the first to speak once they reached the hallway. “Stone me, that was intense! You guys think Jorpen’s still, you know… kicking?” No one answered. Praew just shrugged. Too nauseous to eat, she went straight home.
