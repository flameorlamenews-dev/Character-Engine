Chapter 3 - To Scratch the Surface



The gate groaned, gears screeching as the slabs of iron parted. The widening crack revealed nothing at first—only a sliver of blinding gold. The air thickened. The world beyond beckoned.

Then the gates yawned open.

A gust of wind hit Asher’s face.

His breath hitched.

Before him stretched a world he had never truly seen.

The sky yawned wide above them, endless and terrible in its beauty, swirling with deep blues and violet hues, lightning crackling across the distance like veins of raw energy. Dark clouds twisted above distant peaks, rolling over mountains that stabbed toward the heavens.

The ocean shimmered on the horizon, vast and unknowable, its surface broken by jagged spires of rock and ancient wreckage. A storm brewed in the distance, flashes of light igniting the waves like fire.

And the land—rolling hills, thick forests, crumbling ruins of an older time—breathed with life. Strange birds with iridescent feathers swooped through the trees, their cries echoing in the wind. Giant beasts moved in the far distance, barely silhouettes against the horizon. It was beautiful. It was terrifying.

  Asher stepped forward, awestruck, eyes wide—

  A hand clamped onto his shoulder. Before he could react, he was yanked back hard. A sharp click echoed beneath his foot. Then—CRACK.

  A BoltStone erupted, jagged tendrils of electricity lashing through the air, scorching the ground where he had stood a second earlier. The scent of burning earth filled his lungs. Asher’s heart slammed into his ribs. He’d almost died. Just like that. No battle. No beast. Just one bad step.

  Azel’s grip didn’t budge. The older Hunter didn’t say anything at first. He just stared at him. Then his voice cut through the air like a blade.

  “I don’t remember telling you to move.”

  Asher panted, trying to steady his breathing.

  Azel shoved him back. “This is what I meant. You get distracted, you’re dead. You admire the view, you’re dead. You think for one second that this world isn’t trying to kill you?” He pointed to the scorched crater at Asher’s feet. “This place will rip you apart.”

  Asher’s awe shattered. His hands trembled. The surface wasn’t just beautiful. It was a graveyard waiting to happen.

  “Don’t make me save you again.”

  Asher nodded, then asked, “But why is there a BoltStone right in front of the gates?”

     “Wind picks them up and scatters the stones randomly,” Azel said. “This is why the surface is so unpredictable. Now stay close!”

     Asher followed.

     They moved in silence, navigating the twisting pathways of the ruined world.

     The ruins loomed like the bones of an old kingdom: shattered walls and broken towers swallowed by vines and time. The forest whispered, branches swaying unnaturally, the distant howls of beasts rising in the wind.

     Asher paid attention now. Every step was calculated, every shadow watched. At one point, a low, guttural growl rumbled nearby.

  Asher froze, his hand shooting to his weapon.

  Azel did not react. He only murmured, “Don’t… move. Slowly… exhale.”

  Asher did.

  The growl eventually faded.

  Only later did he learn he’d nearly gotten them both killed—almost drawing his blade on a non-aggressive beast. One that, according to Azel, “can wipe out entire Hunter parties when threatened.”

     As they continued their trek, Asher made sure to follow every one of Azel’s directions without hesitation, and after two terrifying days, they finally reached their destination.

    The base was built beneath the ruins of an old fortress—high stone walls still visible above ground, their decay mirrored in the cracked stonework lining the corridors below.

     Azel gestured for Asher to follow. He did, tension curling in his chest.

     They passed through a narrow corridor, then entered a nearly empty, dimly lit hall with just three figures scattered around the room. One was drinking. One was working on a broken weapon. The last lounged with a book in hand, barely acknowledging the new arrival.

     Azel clapped his hands together, the sharp sound echoing through the hall. “Alright, let’s get introductions out of the way. You’ll be training under each of them eventually, so listen up.”

     Azel gestured first to a broad-shouldered man sprawled lazily on his back, his face half-shadowed by a tattered hood. “Durian, our tracker. If something’s hiding, he’ll find it.”

     Durian raised one finger without looking, finished the last line of a page, then finally glanced up—light brown eyes sharp beneath the hood. He gave Asher a once-over, said nothing, licked his finger, and turned the page to continue reading.

     Next, Azel pointed at a woman with cropped hair and sharp eyes, sitting on a stool at a wooden table, one leg crossed over the other and a drink in hand. “Kyra, our runner. Best legs in the fortress.”

     Kyra rolled her eyes and gave a lazy two-finger salute. “What he means,” she said, glaring at Azel, “is if you need a message or supplies moved fast, I’m your best bet.”

     Azel pointed next to a broad-shouldered man hunched over a broken weapon. “Nico, our tinker. He builds, repairs, and occasionally makes things explode when they shouldn’t.”

     Nico didn’t look up from his work. “They explode when I want them to,” he corrected.

     Azel smirked, then finally gestured to himself. “And me? I’m Azel. I run this operation, keep these lot in line, and make sure no one dies too soon.”

     The group chuckled, but Azel’s gaze flicked back to Asher, sharp again.

     “Now, son, your turn. What’s your name?”

     Before Asher could answer, Durian snorted. “You mean you don’t already know? Two-day trek to get here and you never asked the kid his name?”

     A few chuckles followed.

     Azel rolled his eyes. “Figured I’d let the kid introduce himself before you lot started making bets on whether he’d survive.”

     “That’s rich,” Durian said. “You’re in for a surprise. We’ve got quite the honored guest gracing us today.”

     Azel looked at Asher, confused. “Go on.”

     Asher hated this part. Hated being in that bolting man’s shadow. He just wanted to be seen for himself. So he squared his shoulders, lifted his chin, and spoke in a voice just a little deeper than it normally was. “Asher Rivers.”

     Silence.

     The air in the room shifted. Kyra blinked. “As in . . .  The Prime Archon’s son?”

     Asher swallowed hard. “Yeah. Why?”

     The group exchanged glances. Something unspoken passed between them—something Asher didn’t understand.

     Azel’s jovial demeanor vanished. Then, finally, he exhaled through his nose.

     “No reason,” he said.

     But Asher knew that was a lie.

     Azel turned. “Come on.” He led Asher deeper into the base, Durian and Kyra following close behind, until the narrow corridor widened into a vast holding chamber. That’s when Asher saw them. The beasts.

     They were kept inside giant enclosures, reinforced with massive slabs of stone. Many of them. Beasts he was taught could never be farmed, only hunted. Yet here they were. Eating. Breeding. Existing.

     “Beautiful, aren’t they?” Azel said with a smirk.

     Asher stared, his mouth going dry. “Impossible.”

     Durian leaned against the fence, arms crossed. “Not impossible. Just inconvenient.”

     Asher turned to him, throat tight. “We were always told these creatures couldn’t be farmed. That they’d kill each other.”

     Durian grinned, but there was something too sharp in it, too knowing. “They will. If they’re starving. Keep them fed? They’re as docile as cattle.”

     Asher’s world tilted. He looked toward Azel for reassurance.

     Azel gave a short nod.

     Asher felt his pulse throb in his skull. “Then why is there a food shortage?”

     “Wait,” Kyra cut in. “You really don’t know?”

     Asher turned to her. “Know what?”

     Kyra opened her mouth again. “There—”

     “Isn’t a way to preserve the meat,” Azel said, shooting her a glance as he cut her off.

     “If you look closely, you’ll notice the Tarnox and Faelhens here don’t look like they do in the books you read in school. They change in captivity. We don’t fully understand it, but we call it de-acclimation.

     “De-acclimated beast meat starts to rot in a flash.”

     Kyra chimed in, glancing at Azel as she spoke. “After three days, the rotten meat becomes completely poisonous to humans. One bad shipment could kill a whole Floor in Stormhaven. That’s why we don’t risk it.”

     Azel nodded. “We study ’em. Breed ’em. Eat ’em ourselves. But for Stormhaven? We can only risk bringing a few live ones close to the city, butcher them, and have the meat served at ration stations within three days.”

     Asher didn’t respond. He couldn’t.

     Azel stepped out, leaving Asher alone with his thoughts.

     He returned a flicker later with a wide grin.

     “Your face’ll get stuck that way if you keep frowning. Relax, kid. I don’t think you’ll have to worry about that any time soon.”

     Then he tossed a shovel into Asher’s hands.

     “Congratulations,” he said, mocking. “You’re a Beast Hunter now. I believe the fearsome beasts have left you some droppings to hunt.”

     The ‘droppings’ were massive, steaming piles of manure. His job? Shoveling them out of the base.

     Asher didn’t know whether to scream, or run, or vomit.

     He stared at the tool, clenched in his hands. He thought he’d won. Thought he’d beaten the test and escaped The Prime’s shadow. But he was just a glorified farmhand. The other Hunters laughed. Only then did Asher realize his father had gotten the last laugh after all.

Was this really worth it?

Asher thought back to the moment on the platform during the Hunter Test. They’d been so close. With one final pull, he and Haldric would both become Hunters. And yet Asher hesitated. 

     His father’s voice crept in: ‘You and your little friends will never become Hunters. I forbid it.’

      Asher couldn’t help but glance up. The smug grin on his father’s face told him everything he needed to know. Asher realized the ploy—they were both about to fail.

     But the three of them could’ve failed together. That wouldn’t have been so bad, would it? Twelve years ago, didn’t he decide to become a Hunter just so he could remain by Haldric and Praew's side? 

     No… that’s not exactly right, is it?

Twelve years ago.

     “You guys look so stupid,” Asher Rivers, an eight-year-old boy with tousled blond hair, called out as he watched his two friends of the same age, flail around in the training yard.

     He endured them, mostly. Suffered their noise, their play-acting, their endless games of pretend swordplay. But they were the only kids who weren’t scared of him or his father. Praew and Haldric didn’t care that the Rivers family had practically ran the city since their city ruler, The Prime Archon’s health declined. They didn’t care that Asher’s father was the Heir Archon, The Prime’s successor of Stormhaven. Maybe that made them stupid. Or maybe it made them the only real friends he had.

     Praew and Haldric were swinging wooden swords at each other with the grace of drunken soldiers after a long shift. No stance. No control. Haldric had already tripped twice. Praew held her stick with both hands like a club. Asher winced every time they struck each other—it was chaos.

     Asher didn’t play with sticks anymore. He didn’t have to. His father had arranged private sword lessons for Asher, shortly after becoming the Heir Archon last year. Real blades. Real teachers. When his father became Prime, he would name Asher as the new Heir Archon. That was how it worked. Legacy, bloodline, and purpose. Asher wasn’t a child. Not like them.

     “Hah! I win again!” Praew cheered, stepping over a winded Haldric.

     “That’s not fair!” Haldric groaned from the ground, clutching his ribs. “I tripped! Zephyrs take you—I want a rematch!”

     “Language, Haldric.” The familiar voice came from behind them.

     They all turned to see Arthur Rivers approaching, hands clasped behind his back, posture as straight as a soldier’s spear. The Heir Archon. Asher’s father.

     “Sorry, sir,” Haldric mumbled, his face turning red.

     Arthur chuckled while shaking his finger. “Now, no need to take the Lord’s name in vain, is there?”

     Then something shifted.

     A female Zephyrian Acolyte emerged from the shadow of the corridor and leaned in to whisper to Arthur, whose face tightened instantly. “WHO is coming?” he said too loudly, glancing back at the children. The acolyte leaned closer, but Asher had already started walking. Quiet steps. Curious ears. He was close enough now.

     “Then, escort the children to the sanctuary,” his father whispered. “Asher’s friends must be kept safe.”

     The acolyte nodded. “Uhh, hey, kids,” she said too lightly. “How about we go on a little adventure?”

     Praew and Haldric didn’t move at first, but Asher kept close behind, listening.

     “What? Then where is Bresdin?” Arthur hissed as they walked. “He already killed one of those beasts. Can’t he handle another?”

     “He’s still recovering from that battle,” the acolyte replied. “He’s, umm, in no condition to face another one.”

     “Blast me backwards,” Arthur cursed under his breath. “Then who do we have?”

     “Umm only Godric was willing,” she said.

     “No!” Arthur snapped. “Absolutely not! I will not have my—” he stopped, looked over his shoulder, at Asher, and lowered his voice again. “I won’t have him sacrifice himself.”

     “I’m sorry, sir,” the acolyte said. “Godric was already seen heading to the surface. We, uhh, we can’t call him back.”

     “That bolting man!” The Heir spat. Asher never heard his father curse like this before. “How is The Prime doing?” 

     “He’s already safe in the bunker. But, umm, he is not likely to last long,” the acolyte said.  “We believe it’s best that you mentally prepare. Tonight, you will likely become the next Prime Archon.”

     Arthur said nothing. No celebration or pride. He just walked in silence until they eventually arrived. The bunker was on the Third Floor—a level Asher had never seen before. Its stone corridors were colder, the SolarStones lined the walls less frequently here. It held a large shrine in the center of the room with unlit SolarStones surrounding it.

      Praew and Haldric chased each other ahead, still laughing, still play-fighting. Asher watched them, shaking his head. Children. That’s all they were. He would never let himself be seen that way. He had a name to uphold, and soon, a title.

     Asher sat in the corner while they wrestled, mind racing with what he’d overheard. What beast? What sacrifice? Bresdin, his father’s greatest rival, injured? Godric going to the surface to be sacrificed? 

None of it made sense.

     An acolyte stepped into the room and cleared his throat, loudly. “The Ex Prime, Libur Cali, has passed away peacefully.”

     Arthur Rivers stood still for a moment, then nodded.

     “You are now The Prime Archon. Please come with us. There is information that only you may know.”

     Arthur turned to leave. Asher got up to follow, only for one of the acolytes to block his path. “Not you, child. Only The Prime may enter.”

     “But I’m the next Heir Archon,” Asher argued. “Can’t I come?”

     The acolytes chuckled.

     Arthur didn’t. He turned to his son, voice suddenly sharp. “Silence! You must earn that title, Asher. You need the approval of the city and a prime contribution. Then we’ll talk.”

     “But—” Asher started only to be cut off.

     “That, is enough,” Arthur snapped. “We will speak later.” Then he was gone.

     Praew and Haldric walked up beside Asher, quiet for once. 

     “Who wants to be a bolting Prime Archon anyway?” Haldric muttered.

     “Yeah,” Praew added. “Zephyrs take him! You really wanna sit behind a desk being boring all day? Or,” she said, nudging him. “You could be a Hunter with us. That’d be way more fun, wouldn’t it?”

     A nearby acolyte turned sharply. “You mustn’t speak ill of The Prime Archon. You should know—that’s treason.”

     Everyone in the room erupted into the chant:

     “AND JUSTICE WE WILL DELIVER!”

     “I’m sorry,” Praew and Haldric both said quickly.

     But Asher smiled. No one had ever dared say anything bad about his father before. Not even once. Maybe being a Hunter wouldn’t be so bad after all. At least… it would be entertaining.

     Praew nudged his arm with a sleepy grin. “Told you, you’re one of us now.”

     Haldric shifted against the wall, stifling a yawn. “Just don’t make me have to save you too many times.”

     Asher huffed through his nose—somewhere between a laugh and a sigh.

     No one said much after that. Praew and Haldric drifted toward sleep, slumped against the wall with half-lidded eyes.

     Asher stayed seated, hands clasped in his lap, gaze lingering on the SolarStones along the walls, dim but even, barely lighting the bunker’s stonework. Then, without warning, every SolarStone in the room faded out as he inhaled.

     By the time he exhaled, the room was in total darkness. The SolarStones stayed dark for another breath. Then, just as he began to inhale again, they faded back in—gentle, like their trigger had been a soft voice filling the room.

     That was a flicker. Everyone knew the term, but Asher had never felt it like this before. In school, they’d learned that every triggered SolarStone across the world, regardless of when it activated, pulsed together, all at once, in the same rhythm: faded out, stayed dark, faded back in.

     Three breaths. That was a flicker. It happened every sixty breaths—a natural measurement of time, as the world breathed.

     He began counting. At first, to see if he could time the next one. Then, to pass the time. But as he reached the sixtieth breath again, the room dipped into darkness once more. Another flicker.

     Then another. And another. By the twelfth or thirteenth flicker, something strange began to happen.

     He didn’t need to count anymore. Asher instinctively knew how many flickers had passed. His breathing lined up on its own, moving in time with the pattern. He could feel when the next flicker was coming. Like the space between waves. Like a heartbeat.

     This was it, wasn’t it? The Flickering Rhythm? A skill all children eventually gained—when they could instinctively feel the rhythm of time?

     The light dimmed again.

     Then, on what he knew was the twentieth flicker—everything changed.

     The large, untriggered SolarStones near the shrine flared to life in a rhythmic pulse every half a breath. Sixteen times—eight breaths—where a full wash of color filled the room.

     That was a flash.

     Twenty flickers in a flash.

     Twenty flashes in a day.

     Each flash was unique, marking how late it was. One pulse for the first flash, twenty for the twentieth—signaling the start of a new day. The world’s way of marking a new phase.

     Asher closed his eyes and breathed. Time seemed to slow since gaining the RhythmStone. It no longer slipped by while he was unaware. He could instinctively count every breath, and every pause the world took. Most families saw gaining the RhythmStone as a cause for celebration. But why did he feel so… empty?

     He sat still, eyes on the wall, watching the flickers come and go. Then came the second flash. Then the third. Twelve flickers later, the door creaked open—and his father returned.

      There was nothing still about him. His robes were misaligned, his hands shaking slightly at his sides. His face was pale, save for his eyes—bloodshot red like he hadn’t blinked in a flash. Sweat clung to his brow, soaking into his collar. Whatever he’d learned behind those locked doors had shaken him to his core.

     “You, take the children home,” Arthur told the Zephyrian Acolyte from earlier, his voice shaking.

     Then he grabbed Asher’s arm and marched him through the long corridor that led to the Floor above. His steps were too fast. Asher had to half-jog just to keep pace, his shorter legs struggling to match the stride of a man unraveling.

     As they passed between Floors, Asher noticed how little he’d seen of the city before. The real city. Not just the First Floor, with its clean walls and polished halls, but the way Stormhaven truly sprawled out beneath the surface. A hollowed-out cylinder of layered stone, carved like a massive inverted tower. Three Floors deep. Each Floor wrapped in three concentric rings—stone walls so high and thick you could not hear the people on the other side. Each ring was its own sector, sealed off from the rest. Only one path in or out, one gate per wall. Each level felt like its own prison. Its own kingdom.

     The First Floor, where they were headed, was the most pristine. Home to the ruling class, the government offices, and Asher’s own quarters. The Second Floor held the schools and the Hunter office. The Third was for the market and laborers. And somewhere on the Second Floor, sealed with thick iron gates, was the one true exit to the surface.

     They had nearly reached the First Floor, nearing their home, when Asher finally spoke. He’d held the question in for as long as he could—walking in silence beside his father’s quick, purposeful stride. Asher watched as sweat beaded along The Prime’s brow, his father’s jaw clenched as he kept glancing over his shoulder, scanning corners, eyes darting as if tracking something Asher couldn’t see. 

     He’d never seen his father this shaken before.

     “Father… is something wrong?” Asher asked, just above a whisper. “You don’t seem… well.”

     Arthur stopped walking.

     He turned with a sharpness that made Asher flinch. Without a word, squeezing Asher’s arm hard enough to hurt, his father squatted low until their eyes were level. Asher could see everything now: the raw red veins in his eyes, the sweat on his temples, and something else, his breath was terrible.

     “Don’t you ever call me that in public again,” Arthur hissed. “Never again! Do you understand me?”

     Asher opened his mouth, but no words came. He just nodded, stiffly, barely able to meet his father’s stare.

    “Better yet,” Arthur said, “from now on, you address me as The Prime Archon. No matter where we are. No exceptions.”

     Asher had never heard his father speak like that before. Who was this man?

     Asher blinked back the sting in his eyes and stood straighter. A true leader doesn’t cry. A true Leader never shows weakness.

     Neither spoke as they continued walking. Asher felt something in him crack. And even then he knew, some cracks wouldn’t mend.

When they finally arrived back home, Arthur—no, The Prime Archon—said nothing at first. He walked into his quarters, dropped his robe, and changed into something more formal for the announcement he’d have to make soon. 

     A perfectly tailored white suit. The back bore a red trim that formed an intricate design of three majestic trees, each embedded with ten rubies carved into the shape of leaves.

The trees had deep roots that curled across the fabric, winding into the shape of a doorway beneath each trunk.

     Asher stood in the doorway, watching in silence, wondering what it all meant.

     That was when he saw it.

     A faint glimmer. A slight bulge beneath The Prime’s chest. The Prime Archon’s undershirt slipped for a moment as he buttoned up his tunic, and Asher saw a stone embedded in the left side of his father’s pectoral. A Sifaralith Stone. Asher didn’t know which kind, nor what it meant.

     “What is that?” he asked, before he could stop himself.

     Arthur’s hands froze on the last button. “You will never speak of this,” he said, voice low and final. “Not to anyone.”

     Asher nodded, but the image had already burned into him.

     They sat facing each other in the study. A thick circular stone desk separated the two while Arthur tried to explain why Asher couldn’t just inherit the title of Heir Archon. “It doesn’t work that way. Titles must be earned. The people have to see you. Believe in you.”

     But Asher wasn’t listening. Not anymore. He was still thinking about the stone. About his friends and how much his father had changed. “Maybe I don’t want to be the Heir Archon,” he said. “If becoming The Prime means ending up like you, I would rather be a Hunter.”

     Arthur slammed his flat palm on the stone table. “You! You foolish boy! You don’t know what you’re saying.”

     Asher and The Prime Archon both flinched, staring at the permanent palm print indented into the thick stone. The Prime only hesitated for a moment. “Do you even understand why we live underground?” Arthur barked, rising from his chair. “Do you know what’s up there right now?”

     Asher stepped back, shocked, but a leader— no, a Hunter doesn’t cower in fear. Matching his father’s outbursts, he yelled back, “Do you?! Or are you too busy hiding down here like a coward while real heroes like Godric sacrifice themselves!?

     The Prime stood there for a long moment, chest rising and falling like he’d just run through every Floor of the city. Through gritted teeth, he seethed, “You and your little friends will never become Hunters. It's too dangerous. I forbid it.”

     “You know you can’t do that,” Asher said, lowering his voice, “Once we graduate school, everyone gets to take the Hunter test. Not even The Prime Archon can stop that.”

     Arthur said nothing.

     Asher turned, heart pounding, and walked out of the room. He didn’t look back. After all, a Hunter mustn't hesitate.
