Chapter 16 - The Embedding



“I do not know the outcome, but I know one thing.”



“Let me get this straight," Godric said. "You used the Heir Archon master key to access the infirmary's classified medical records. That’s how you learned about my foot, and that’s all you actually know?”

     Haldric stared at the ceiling as blood pooled in his mouth, thick and bitter. He spat, the dark smear hitting the stone as he forced himself to sit upright. “Yeah,” he said weakly.

    His ribs burned, his jaw throbbed, and his entire body ached as if the stone itself had punished him.

    A boot nudged his leg. “Get up,” Godric said.

    Haldric blinked through the blur. Godric stood over him, arms crossed, back to that calm demeanor and the same maddening, persistent look of disappointment.

    Haldric hated that look. He tried to push himself up, ignoring the pulse of pain that followed. 

    Godric watched him struggle but made no move to help. “Bolting Archons,” he muttered, “they’re always the same. The way Praew spoke of you, I expected more. Bolting girl never did know how to trust the right people.”

    Haldric froze. 

   Godric shook his head. “She thought I was making my students disappear. Threatened me, actually. Said if anything happened to her, you’d be the one coming for her.”

    Haldric’s heart slammed against his ribs.

    Godric sighed. “I didn’t take the bolting girls, but I’ve been trying to track down who did.”

    “What?” Haldric coughed, his chest tightening. “Why didn’t you lead with that?”

    Godric scoffed. “Says the guy who hit first, asked questions later? I’ve seen a Tarnox in heat with more wisdom on a battlefield than you.”

    Sad thing is, he probably has, Haldric thought.

    Godric’s jaw tightened slightly. “Problem is, I can’t find them.” He shook his head. “I searched all of the Fourth Floor. No sign of them, except…” His voice lowered. “I could hear faint screaming from below it.”

      “Wait,” Haldric said slowly. “Who was the person you put into a coma last night? All their information was redacted.”

  Godric looked at him. “That,” he said, “was the deadliest person in the city. And I wasn’t the one who put her in that coma.”

  “Wait, then who did?”

  Godric shook his head. “I don’t know. And that’s what worries me.”

       Haldric felt a chill. Praew was caught up in something far more dangerous than he had ever realized. And if Godric had heard screams from below the Fourth Floor,

    Haldric stiffened. That could only mean…

    “There’s a Fifth Floor,” he breathed.

    Godric nodded. “I can’t find the entrance to it. There must be a hidden passageway somewhere.”

    Haldric’s pulse hammered. A secret passageway. An undisclosed location. Something clicked in his mind. A place. A possibility. A sickening certainty he didn’t want to have.

    He hoped he was wrong.

    He really, really hoped he was wrong. But, well, he wouldn’t be surprised at this point.

    He forced himself to his feet, pushing past the pain. There was no time for exhaustion. No time for hesitation. He had to move. He had to find her.

    Godric let out an exasperated sigh. “Yeah, yeah. I’ll go with you.”

    “You don’t have to—”

    “You’re only standing,” Godric cut in, “because I don’t enjoy beating up children. Do you think our kidnapper will show you the same mercy?” His towering figure looked down toward Haldric. “Don’t push your luck.” 

    Haldric didn’t argue. He just started moving.

    Please be okay, Praew!



***

The door slammed shut behind her.

The room was bright. Too bright. Praew squinted as she was shoved inside; the sudden shift from darkness made her vision blur. The room was a stark contrast to the cells outside. It was far too clean and organized. Trays lined the walls, holding neatly rowed instruments she didn’t recognize. A stairway wound along the wall to a location she couldn’t make out within the harsh lights. At first, Praew thought she was in a medical chamber, but no. It was far too quiet.

And then Praew saw her.

Junya lay, eyes wide open, strapped to a nearly vertical polished stone slab with her arms splayed out, wrists bound in thick restraints. Her ankles and neck were tied down as well, so not even her soul could run away.

But that wasn’t what made Praew’s blood run cold.

Something was sticking out of Junya’s forehead.

A Sifaralith, embedded deep, pulsing with a sickly glow. Junya herself was still.

Was she already—?

  Before Praew could finish the thought, Renwick led her to an identical stone table beside her motionless friend and strapped her down the same. Only able to turn her head side to side, Praew stared at her friend’s corpse.

  Junya was dead.

  Praew had known Junya wouldn’t leave this room alive. But knowing something and seeing it firsthand were two different things.

  She squeezed her eyes shut, but it made no difference. The sight was burned into Praew’s memory.

  And she was next.

  A voice slipped through the silence, drenched in quiet disdain.

  “Another aberration rendered useless. How profoundly unremarkable, no?”

  Praew didn’t move. She barely breathed. The voice wasn’t near her. It came from somewhere above, threading effortlessly through the haze of light streaks and rays.

  A sigh grew into a flicker of silence. Then, the silence was broken by the soft echo of descending footsteps. The sound circled her, each step closer than the last, like a predator closing in.

  “Feckless Ratrod,” the voice hissed, “this degree of incompetence is beyond untenable. What an inefficient and frivolous expenditure of my time, no?”

  A scientist came into view. He moved to the slab beside her and began unstrapping Junya’s body. With deliberate carelessness, he let the corpse drop to the floor with a thud.  

  Praew flinched as the scientist turned to her.

  “Do refrain from replicating this subject’s outcome. I’m disinclined to witness consecutive failings. You grasp that, no?”

  Praew looked away, only to find Renwick staring at her.

  “How could you do this, Renwick?” she shouted. “Do you really hate me that much? What did I ever do to you?! And Junya… she was innocent! She didn’t deserve this!”

  “Hate you?” Renwick asked incredulously. “I’m saving your life! I’m trying to make sure you don’t end up like—forget it.”

  He clenched his jaw, fists trembling at his sides, then turned and walked out of the room.

  She tried to move, but nothing gave. Each strap bit into her skin, cutting off circulation.

  Footsteps echoed again across the stone floor. Praew’s chest rose and fell too fast. She snapped her head the other way to look back at the approaching scientist.

  This was obviously a man who thought he was the smartest in the room. A man who enjoyed power over others. And right now, he was clearly enjoying this.

  Praew saw it in his smile. Too pleased.

  Once beside her, he drew in a slow breath, as if to relish the moment.

  “Predictable. The conscious mind clings to resistance even when statistical certainty dictates futility. Yet you persist. Fascinating, no?”

  His fingers trailed over her wrist, grazing the strap but not checking it. Testing.

  Praew didn’t flinch, but the scientist smirked, as if sensing her discomfort.

  “The tenacity of the lesser mind is its own tragic poetry. You will break, of course. They always do. The only variable is duration.”

  He leaned closer, eye to eye, just enough for her to feel the shift in the air. Just enough for her to smell the bitter tang of chemical oil on his skin.

  Then he grabbed her hair, inhaling like he was savoring something.

  Out of the corner of her eye, Praew caught a figure slip through the doorway behind him, clearly sneaking up on the scientist. Her pulse leapt.

  Renwick? He came back!

  The smirking madman raised an eyebrow and began turning to see what had stolen her attention.

  Trying to distract him, Praew locked eyes with the scientist and smiled. “You’re a coward.”

  The scientist froze, his smirk faltering slightly. Praew tilted her head as much as the straps allowed and gave him the most disgusted look imaginable.

  “I see you,” she murmured, voice steady and poisoned with amusement. “Men like you only touch women when they can’t fight back. I bet you don’t even know what to do with one otherwise.”

  He gripped her wrist tightly, his smile vanishing in an instant.

  “What a tedious display of defiance. The same primitive barbs, the same desperate bravado. Tiresome, no?”

  Her own grin widened. “Oh? Did I touch a nerve?”

  She saw it happen. The crack in his composure. The twitch in his jaw. The exact moment he lost control. He leaned closer, face just inches from her own.

  She smelled his pungent breath, never losing track of the shadowy figure creeping closer behind him.

  She spat directly into his eye.

  His curse barely left his lips before she slammed her head into his nose. The crack was satisfying.

  He staggered back, nose bleeding, teeth clenched.

  His face twisted as he spoke through gritted teeth, his voice full of raw contempt, absolute superiority, and final condemnation.

  “A squandered genetic anomaly, cursed with just enough sentience to be insufferable. No?”

  His hand wrapped around her throat.

  “You mistake toleration for limitation. I will rectify that.”

  “Any time now, Renwick!” she gasped.

  The scientist barely had time to react before his body went stiff.

  A splitting pressure pushed from within the creep’s throat, stretching the skin outward toward Praew. His eyes bulged, confused and panicked.

  A sword tip punched through the front of his throat, up through his jaw, and out his mouth.

  His grip went slack. His knees buckled.

  He slid off the blade, collapsing to the side, on top of Junya.

  Just as lifeless.

  Praew gasped for air, blinking up at the spot where Renwick stood.

  Only to find it wasn’t Renwick at all.

  The Prime Archon, sword in hand, sneered at the dying scientist.

  “It is absolutely deplorable monsters like you that give the rest of us a bad name,” The Prime said, his voice thick with disgust.

  Then, a breath. A shift in tone.

  “Are you alright, Praew?”

  She stared at him, swallowing hard. Her throat ached, her body was stiff and trembling, but the worst of it was over.

  She took a slow, shaky breath. “I… I am now.”

  The Prime? The Prime saved me?

  Her pulse hammered in her ears. She hesitated, then muttered, “Uh… thank you, sir.”

  He chuckled.

  “No need to thank me. How dare he think to sully the future heroes of this city?”

  “Uhh… right,” she mumbled, still trying to wrap her head around it. The Prime is saving me?

  She barely registered it before he stepped past her, moving out of sight, fiddling with something behind her head.

  “I… owe you an apology, sir.”

  “Hmm?” His voice sounded distant behind her.

  Praew took a deep breath. “I thought you were… well… an absolutely deplorable monster.”

  The Prime chuckled like someone only half-listening.

  What is he doing back there? Ohh, he must be looking for a way to release me!

  ”The straps have buckles here…”

  No response.

  She twiddled her fingers. “Uh… can you release me now?”

  A pause.

  “No, I cannot.”

  The words hung in the air—so matter-of-fact, so final.

  She swallowed hard. Alarm crept down her spine. “What…?”

  “Do you even know what’s happening here, girl?”

  His voice was measured, calm, like a man explaining basic facts to a child.

  She writhed against the straps, instinct clawing for escape, but nothing gave.

  The Prime continued without her response.

  “You probably only know about Common and Noble Stones.” His tone remained even. “But there are more. Prime. Sovereign. Mythical.”

  Another deliberate, dramatic pause before continuing.

  “There are humans who are, in essence, more beast than man. Some are born with a rare genetic mutation—a mutation that enables a particular Sifaralith Stone to be embedded into their body, much like the beasts who are born with theirs.”

  Praew’s body began to shake involuntarily. Her breathing grew difficult. She glanced at her friend’s corpse and the Sifaralith stone embedded in Junya’s skull. But… that… that wasn’t possible.

  The Prime and Renwick stepped into her line of sight. When did that bolting Ratrod return?

  She gritted her teeth. “I will kill you, Renwick! If I ever get out of here, I will make you pay! Both of you!”

  He refused to look at her as he handed a black pouch to The Prime, who then pulled out a BoltStone. Its polished surface gleamed under the dim light.

  The Prime lifted it between the three of them reverently, voice shaking as he spoke. “Exclusive to one of the ten beasts of legend. A Mythical BoltStone.”

  As if triggered by his voice, the stone began to glow bright red. The color flooded the room, overpowering the SolarStones within.

  A wave of fear and sadness overwhelmed Praew. “That glow!”

  “Yes.” The Prime nodded eagerly. His calm demeanor had begun to crack. “That red glow is proof of your compatibility with the stone. It recognizes you. But recognition is not enough.”

  He paused, staring at the tears that began running down her face.

  “You feel it, don’t you?” He continued with an excited smile. “Emotions that do not belong to you? That is the stone attempting to initiate the embedding process. The stone makes you feel the final emotions of the beast, just before its death. Do not fight it.”

  Praew stopped breathing. Her mind reeled.

  To a distant, buried memory. One she had assumed was just nerves.

  The Hunter Test. That same black bag.

  It had been glowing this same red.

  And she—she had felt this same raging storm of emotions.

  That test…

  It wasn’t about becoming a Hunter, was it?

  It wasn’t about skill at all.

  It was a screening process. A search for people. People like her.

  Her lips parted. A whisper. Barely a sound.

  “That’s impossible.”

  The Prime’s face softened. His expression became unnaturally paternal.

  “No, my dear. What’s impossible is the task you must face next.”

  He closed his hand around the Sifaralith and clasped his hands behind his back, pacing slowly before continuing.

  “You must synergize with the lingering will that the beast left within the stone. But the real trick is to match the intensity of those emotions. That is the only way to avoid ending up like your friends, who couldn’t even handle their Prime Sifaraliths.”

  The Prime shook his head in what could have been pity or disappointment.

  “You see, the rarer the stone, the slimmer the chance a human survives the embedding.

  “Common: forty percent. Noble: thirty. Prime: twenty. Sovereign: ten.”

  The Prime stopped just in front of her, gaze meeting hers.

  “Mythical, well, few believe the stones even exist.”

  Praew’s pulse roared in her ears. Her mind screamed at her to fight, to run, to do something.

  The Prime raised his arm above his head, eyes fixed upward as he admired the stone. The BoltStone glowed brighter now.

  He shook his head and sighed.

  “Yet, for the sake of our species, the risk must be taken.”

  The Prime slammed the BoltStone into her chest, just below the throat.

  Praew gasped.

  The BoltStone pulsed against her skin. Its red glow deepened to a bloody crimson that flooded the room with an intense heat. Praew tried to look down, but the straps locked her neck in place. She could just make out the stone’s new molten color between The Prime’s fingers while he continued to push.

  She screamed as the heat burned into her chest.

  Agony. Raw. Unrelenting. A shockwave of electricity ripped through her bones, searing through muscle, nerve, and skin.

  Everything faded to black.



***



  Praew found herself on the surface. The sky above was dust-swept and churning, dark with smoke.

  A middle-aged man, armored and weather-worn, stood a dozen paces ahead.

  “I’ve been waiting to bolting kill you for a long time, Boltrine!” he bellowed.

  This little rodent dares?

  Clack.

  A TerraStone struck without warning, swallowing the ground beneath her.

  The earth collapsed. Her body dropped into the mud, dragged down fast. Cold sludge closed around her limbs, locking them in place. She thrashed, but it didn’t matter. She was stuck. Only her neck and head remained above the surface.

  She cried in alarm.

  The surface faded.    



***



  Praew’s eyes snapped open.

  Even now, that dream torments me?

  But no, it was different this time. Praew had never heard the name Boltrine before, and she could finally make out the man’s face.

  Wasn’t that War—

  Lightning erupted, crackling, branching, arcing off the table, the walls, and the ceiling. The room flashed with searing light.

  The air filled with the sharp stench of burning flesh. She barely recognized that it was her own. Her vision blurred as fire seemed to melt through her.

  Nothing was spared. Electricity tore through every part of her. It started near her chest, racing down her limbs and spine until it burrowed into her very marrow.

  She continued to scream. It didn’t matter if she wanted to, it ripped out of her throat like it had been waiting.

  The glow dimmed, or was it her vision?

  Praew felt her consciousness slipping.

  This is it! I’m going to die! Zephyrs take me this is it!

  The world faded away.



***



  Boltrine found herself trapped on the surface. The wind ruffled against her exposed face and neck. She thrashed, but nothing gave.

  The man bellowed a laugh. “Even your god is on my side today.”

  What god? This dim-witted rodent thinks Haiyana’s hurl is the work of divinity?

  The man took a step forward.

  How could I be so careless?

  She thrashed more, with no luck.

  Love, where are you? I’m… I’m scared. I’m going to die aren’t I?

  She closed her eyes.

  The wind dispersed.



*** 



  When Praew came to, she kept her eyes shut tight.

  The Prime continued to push the stone against her as it melted her skin.

  “Don’t you dare give up now, Praew!” he seemed to call out from a distance.

  Wait, then who is—

  Praew opened her eyes to see no one touching the stone. It drove deeper into her chest on its own. The Prime and Renwick were climbing the stairway out of sight.

  They bolting left me! They left me to suffer and die alone!

  “I’m going to kill you bolting little rodents!” Praew screamed, her voice too loud to be her own.

  The BoltStone continued to discharge more fiercely than before.

  She thrashed and writhed, but the straps held firm. Her body arched off the slab, bones rattling from the force of the current.

  It felt like she was being peeled apart, piece by piece, burned from the inside out.

  Praew closed her eyes, fighting back tears.



***



  On the surface, the rodent began approaching with his blade lifted, his silhouette growing as he neared.

  There was no mercy in his eyes. Only greed. And that sadistic, human satisfaction.

  Boltrine shivered.

  My son, I can no longer fly with you. Please keep me close in your heart.

  My love, I fear I can no longer gaze upon your majesty, but I trust you will not forsake our pact. Blood for blood!

  Rodent, I’ll make sure he kills you!

  A majestic bird cry ripped from her throat and shot toward the sky. Cocking her head sideways to better eye the threat, she began to cast crackling streams of electricity toward him.

  The rodent dodged them effortlessly.

  But she knew he would. She was baiting him. He dodged again, but this time, his fate was sealed. A cry from above—her own echo—came crashing down on top of the rodent. The cry hit his exposed arm, successfully marking him. A yellow symbol appeared on his skin. She could sense her connection to it, and so would her mate, Boltrax.

  The man stopped just in front of her, sword raised, and without another word, he swung.

***

  Praew screamed again.

  The lightning surged more violently. It didn’t stop. It wouldn’t stop. Her body was unraveling, raw nerves singing in unbearable torment.

  She was going to die. Just like Boltrine.

  Boltrine… The thought cut through the pain.

  Every time Boltrine released electricity, she cried out first. Praew’s mind twisted, grasping at that moment.

  That was important, wasn’t it?

  That… wasn’t a coincidence.

  It was… triggered by sound!

  Her own voice was killing her, but she wanted to scream. She needed to scream.

  Her throat ached with it, her lungs desperate for release, but she clamped her jaw shut, grinding her teeth so hard she thought they’d shatter.

  The electricity weakened. Not gone entirely, but less.

  Her body still convulsed, her muscles still spasmed, her nerves still blazed with pain. But she had control now.

  She just had to survive it.

  Flickers. A flash. Or maybe forever. The pain dragged on. It never relented. It only wore her down, peeling at her will, digging in deeper, deeper, deeper. She clenched her teeth until she tasted blood.

  She held it in, silently enduring the greatest pain she had ever known.

  Then, at last, it stopped.

  All at once, the electricity died and the glow disappeared.

  A voice rang in her head, deep and ancient. “Who Dares To Desecrate Her Noble Stone? Answer This Exalted One, Or Await My Wrath.”

  Her body collapsed, limp, spent, shaking. Her chest rose. Fell. A breath. Then another.

  She was alive.

  She was ALIVE!

  And no one had come to save her.
