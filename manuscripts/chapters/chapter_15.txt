Chapter 15 - Resolve and Reunion





“I saw it in the eyes of the next generation, those who will carry the spear after me.”





     The girl was still there. Not physically; Haldric hadn’t seen her since the gate. But she lingered. The image of her stare had seeped into his thoughts, staining the back of his mind in a way that refused to fade.

  He sighed, dragging a hand down his face. Get a grip. She wasn’t real.

  He focused on the real problem. The Prime.

  The man was ruthless, calculating, maybe even manipulative. But Haldric was not a fool. The Prime was no villain. He was a coward. Why do we have to leave the planet? This is our home too. We should learn from those disgusting creatures and unite our species. If we all acclimated, there’s no way we could lose. Then maybe he would finally have a chance to court—

  The realization settled like a stone in his gut.

  Praew had been right. Stone me, Praew was right! Stormhaven was suffering. People were barely surviving. The shortages were faked for their survival. But… The Prime Believed it was a necessity.

Haldric had been a complete Ratrod to Praew, but he needed to tell her everything. She needed to know the truth.

  What she really needed—no, what she deserved—was an apology.

  His pace quickened as he made his way through the winding corridors of the lower districts, each step pressing heavier than the last. He hadn’t seen her since yesterday.

  Guilt churned in his stomach.

  She was going to be smug about it, he was sure. She’d smirk and cross her arms. “Zephyrs take me! I told you so, you stone-headed boulder!” Praew chastised in his head.

  Haldric sighed, rubbing his temple. Yeah, I deserved that.



***



  Finally, he reached her family’s home. He took a breath, steeling himself, then rapped his knuckles against the stone door. It opened almost immediately.

  Praew’s father stood in the doorway, his lined face breaking into relief the moment he saw him.

  “By the stones,” he sighed, stepping forward as if to embrace him. “You’re safe. We were so worried.”

  Haldric blinked. Worried?

  He barely had time to register it before Praew’s mother appeared beside him, hands clasped tightly. “Where have you two been?”

  Two?

  Haldric hesitated. “We… two?” His voice came slower than he meant it to.

  Praew’s father frowned. “Isn’t she with you?” He poked his head past Haldric, looking behind him. Searching.

  Haldric felt his breath stall.

  “You were the last one to see her,” her mother added, her voice laced with quiet fear. “No one could find either of you since yesterday and… Hal?”

  Haldric took a step back. Yesterday. The words landed like a punch to the chest. Praew was missing.

  His skin went cold. He hadn’t seen her since—stone me.

  “Hal, Dear!” Praew’s mother called out.

  He left without a word. His mind was already racing. Why do I have such a bad feeling about this?

  “Wait, Hal!”

  Was someone calling out to him? No. Ignore that. Focus.

  He’d been with The Prime ever since separating from Praew last night. There wasn’t enough time for The Prime to grab her and catch him snooping. What if… someone else caught her committing treason?

  He headed toward the city jail, turning over every possibility. You better not be here, Praew. He wasn’t even sure if he had authority here, but he’d try anyway.

  The guard snapped to attention as Haldric approached. He straightened his posture and spoke in the most authoritative tone he could muster, just like he’d seen The Prime do.

  “I require your arrest reports and inmate ledger. Yesterday to present.”

  “Sir?” The guard blinked.

  Haldric’s voice dropped lower, colder. “I hope you don’t intend to make me repeat myself, soldier. It would be… unfortunate to learn you deliberately ignored the Heir Archon.”

  The poor man’s eyes nearly popped out of his skull. “N-no, Heir Archon, sir! I’ll get them right away!”

  He returned quickly with the documents. Haldric read through them more than once, taking brief, petty satisfaction in seeing War Claw Bresdin’s name in there. But ultimately, the documents were useless.

  Nothing. Back to square one.

  He went to The Prime’s office, hoping for help, but the man wasn’t there. Probably still on the surface. Maybe best not to disturb him right now…

  Haldric paused in the corridor. What next?

  Praew wasn’t easy to take down. Not after her Special Forces training. He absentmindedly rubbed his throat where she had hit him last night.

  She was probably more skilled than him and Asher in every form of combat by now. It would’ve taken a serious fight to capture her. Blood. Noise. Someone would’ve heard something. Right?

  He arrived at the infirmary and used his title to get immediate access to the medical records. But there was no record of her, nor any combat-related injuries among the wounded.

  Haldric slammed the infirmary’s thick booklet shut. She’s not here either? “Bolting stone me!” he yelled. Where in the ten stones are you? It’s not like you would vanish without a fight.

  That was not the Praew he knew.

  Unless the assailants were acclimated. But of course, that information wouldn’t be in just any accessible medical records.

  Startled by Haldric’s outburst, a chirurgeon cautiously approached. “Uhh… sir? Would you perhaps like to search through the classified records?”

  “The what?” Haldric snapped back.

  “Um—I mean…” The worker fidgeted uncomfortably. “The classified records, if it pleases you. Very few are authorized to review or edit them, but we all know they exist, sir. The Prime and Heir should have their own key to unlock the room.”

  I’m an idiot.

  ”You just figured that out?” Praew snorted in his head.

  Haldric pulled the key that hung beneath his shirt and unlocked an obscured room. Moments later, he cracked the classified check-in ledger open and flipped through the recent entries. If any acclimated Hunters or Special Forces operatives had been admitted, it would be noted.

  And there it was. Two notable admissions from early this morning.

  The first?

  Patient Name: [REDACTED]

       Superior Human Anatomy: Embedded

  Profession: [REDACTED]

  Injuries: Blunt force trauma, lacerations, coma

  Cause of Injury: [REDACTED]

  Haldric stared. He’d never seen this many redacted lines on a single document. That can’t be normal, right? 

      “Not likely.” Praew agreed in his head, “keep reading.” 

      He moved to the second entry. This one actually told him something.

  Patient Name: Godric Rivers

  Superior Human Anatomy: Acclimated

  Profession: Mythical Beast Hunter

  Injuries: Dislocated shoulder, impaled foot

  Cause of Injury: Combat training

  Haldric whistled. Combat training? In the middle of the night? Unlikely. Looks like Praew was right about you too.

  He had already taken down one Mythical Beast Hunter. Now it looked like he’d have to deal with the second.

  Silverwing Godric.

  Haldric’s jaw locked, his heart pounding. This time, he wouldn’t be caught unprepared. This time, Godric would be the prey.

  As he ran home, Haldric’s thoughts spun. Upstairs. Bedroom. Floorboards. He dropped to his knees and yanked a wooden box from beneath the bed.

  His father’s combat gloves.

  Each one had a single slot in the center of the palm for a Sifaralith Stone to be embedded.

  He had stared at these gloves for years. Obsessively. Unhealthily. He knew exactly what size of stone they needed. He pulled out a second box, which held pilfered goods from the previous night.

  He never should have taken them.

  The Sifaralith Identification Program on the Fourth Floor was brutal. He’d seen it himself—how they made enslaved people handle stones, watching for a spark, a twitch, a scream. Waiting for a trigger. Knowing full well that an unknown trigger often meant death.

  Despite their origin, he had stolen a SurgeStone and a ForceStone of a Noble rank.

  He put the gloves on. His fists clenched, the gloves tightening against his skin. It was wrong. He knew that. But it was the only way to level the field. The only way to stand on even ground with men like Godric.

  His guilt didn’t matter now. Praew was missing.

  And he knew exactly where Godric would be.

  Haldric shoved the boxes back under the bed and was out the door before the guilt could catch up to him. His steps hit stone hard and fast.

  No more time to waste.

  It was time for answers.

  And time for Godric to face his wrath. 

  The training center was alive with movement. Six Special Forces recruits sparred in the open space, each clash of weapons and footwork controlled under the sharp eye of Silverwing Godric.

  Haldric didn’t slow until he set foot inside. The moment he did, everything changed.

  Combat froze to a halt as training partners hesitated mid-strike. A slow ripple of unease spread through the room.

  The look on Haldric’s face must have been frightening.

  His eyes locked onto Godric, who stood in the center of the ring, arms crossed. Unbothered.

  Haldric rolled back his sleeves. The gloves glinted, briefly exposing the stones embedded in his palms.

  A ForceStone in his right. A SurgeStone in his left.

  Haldric squeezed his left hand with as much force as his enhanced strength could muster. Triggered by force, the SurgeStone released a wave of power that washed over him. His muscles thirsted for action. 

  He could feel endless strength within every stride. He felt a sense of clarity as his pulse sharpened, his hearing improved, and time appeared to slow as his brain processed faster.

  Haldric smacked his chest with his left hand three times, a gesture that Hunters used to indicate their intent to fight.

  Silverwing Godric mirrored the gesture, but only twice.

  That’s odd. Shouldn’t it have been three to accept?

  Silence stretched between them, both knowing exactly what would happen next.

  Haldric clasped his palms together with all his strength. The contact made a surge link between his two stones.

  With a surged ForceStone, Haldric swung.

  CRACK.

       A violent, fist-sized pulse of concentrated kinetic energy hovered between them.

  Godric had casually caught the invisible force bare-handed, needing only a single step backward.

  Well… at least Haldric had thought he knew what would happen.

  He froze, too stunned to move. How did he even—

  “Your bolting form is all wrong,” Godric sighed, not even bothering to look toward Haldric. “Clench your teeth, kid.”

  Godric’s arm began to glow. A light blue symbol could be seen through his sleeve.

  What is he? Haldric braced himself, teeth gritting and eyes closed.

  Haldric felt a nudge on his outstretched hand. Then—nothing.

  He opened his eyes. “That’s it?”

  The suspended energy exploded backward.

  The shockwave rebounded, slamming into him twice as hard. His body launched backward, feet lifting off the ground.

  Then came the impact.

  Haldric’s back hit the stone wall, the force rattling his skull, air ripping from his lungs.

  His left palm slapped the wall behind him.

  The moment Haldric surged his body, the throbbing pain dulled instantly.

  Across the room, Godric straightened.

  Haldric pushed off the wall, rolling his shoulders.

  A slow brush of his sleeve. A breath.

  One step.

  Then another.

  Each movement deliberate, forcing himself forward, step by step, until he stood right in front of Godric.

  "Surprised you’re still standing," Godric said loud enough for the class to hear. "Guess you’ve grown some stones after all."

  Godric leaned in, lowering his voice to a warning. "Forfeit now. A surge crash after a head injury will drop you flat."

       “Don’t worry about me, Godric.” Haldric gave a slow, knowing grin. “How’s your foot holding up?”

  “You know too much.” Godric said in a smooth, emotionless tone. Now you’ve forced my hand.” 

  His demeanor had completely changed. He curled his fingers stiff, like the claws of a beast ready to tear flesh. His eyes widened, burning with an intent to kill.

  And he—stone me.

  Haldric’s legs began to shake uncontrollably.

  Silverwing Godric was smiling.

 Haldric’s instinct screamed to run, but no.

A prey that turns its back—

No. That wasn’t right.

A predator that flees—

Nothing. His mind went blank. 

Praew bolting needs me!

He clenched his fists and met Godric’s stare.

Godric never broke eye contact. Addressing his students, he spoke with a curt, bellowing voice. “Class dismissed.”

The trainees bolted without hesitation. Not a single one lingered, scrambling past each other, tripping over their own feet in their rush to flee.

”They probably sense danger," Praew suggested in his mind. “Stay alert!” 

  Godric cracked his knuckles. Slow. Deliberate. The sound echoed through the empty training hall. A smirk twitched at the edge of his lips.

  “A surged ForceStone? How cute. But I’m not Bresdin, kid. The Prime will need to send his Embedded if he plans to eliminate me!”

  Embedded? Haldric’s grip tightened around the stones. His body surged.

  “The Prime didn’t send me.” Haldric’s voice was sharp. “I wouldn’t want to bother him with your corpse.”

  He climbed to his feet, staggered, then charged. Fist raised, aiming straight for Godric’s face, he screamed,

  “Where is she!?”

       Praew found herself on the surface.

  The sky above was dust-swept and churning, dark with smoke. Distant cracks split the air like laughter from the gods. Before her stretched a cursed land, crater-pocked, waterlogged, and seething with hidden peril. Mud glistened under a colorless haze, masking the truth beneath its sheen. Great divots scarred the terrain where stones had fallen. They weren’t natural. 

     Monstrous chunks of blackened rock, shattered earth, and TerraStones rained intermittently from the volcano in the distance. One struck near the horizon. In an instant, a chunk of land silently vanished, swallowed by impact. The hole left behind steamed faintly. Water and sludge began to slither into its depths, disguising the wound beneath a false surface. The air shimmered with heat and danger.

  A middle-aged man, armored and weather-worn, stood a dozen paces ahead. His chest rose and fell with each breath, his blade enormous, jagged, and heavy, as if forged from the land itself. Rage warped his face. He pointed the weapon squarely at her.

  “I’ve been waiting to bolting kill you for a long time.”

  What surprised her most was that, instead of fear or anger, she only felt amusement.

  This little rodent dares?

  Then came the impact.

  A TerraStone struck without warning, swallowing the ground beneath her.

  The earth collapsed. Her body dropped into the mud, dragged down fast. Cold sludge closed around her limbs, locking them in place. She thrashed, but it didn’t matter. She was stuck. Only her neck and head remained above the surface.

  The man bellowed a laugh. “Even your god is on my side today.” He began approaching with his blade lifted, his silhouette growing as he neared.

  There was no mercy in his eyes. Only greed. And that sadistic, human satisfaction.

  “Praew,” came another voice.

  Who’s that?

  “Praew…”

  The voice didn’t come from the wind. It came from somewhere closer. Real.

  “Praew!”

  She gasped.

  Her eyes snapped open as she woke abruptly, finding herself somewhere else. That dream again?

  “Praew,” the voice said again, more clearly this time.

  She knew that voice.

  “Renwick?” she asked, sitting up fast. Her arm scraped against a wall. A sharp sting followed. She hissed, pulled back, and rubbed the scrape.

  The walls loomed close, their surface intentionally coarse. It was obviously designed to punish contact. The polished floor sloped slightly toward the door, slick with cold, sludge-tainted water. Her clothes had already drunk their fill, now clinging to her skin with the weight of filth. As she steadied herself with one hand, her palm slid against the smooth floor, the chill biting through her skin. She looked around, breath already tight in her chest.

  The room—no, the cell—was long and narrow. If she bent her elbows outward, they would graze both walls. Its length could fit two, maybe three people her size lying head to foot.

  A weak SolarStone glowed dimly from the ceiling near the gate, its light barely reaching the center. Shadows lingered at the edges, teasing her peripherals. Farther back, she spotted a second SolarStone embedded in the rear wall, unlit.

  I wonder what its trigger is.

  She tried to inhale deeper, but the air barely gave. No matter how she tried, she couldn’t seem to catch her breath.

  Her eyes fixed on Renwick, who gave her a weary smile. “You alright, Praew?”

       “Well, I was, before I saw your punchable face.”

     Renwick smiled and began to chuckle, but it broke off into a nasty coughing fit.

     Praew shifted toward him.

     Now that she looked closer, he barely resembled the boy she remembered. He was shaking, filthy, eyes wide and bruised. A long, tattered cloak hung off his frame like it had never been taken off. His hair clung to his face. His breathing was shallow.

  “Zepher’s take me! What happened?” she asked. “Where are we?”

  Renwick shook his head. “I don’t know.”

  “Alright... Do you remember how you got here?” Praew asked, determined to get answers.

  He rubbed his temples, voice low. “I was with Asher. We were about to head to the surface… then there was this blinding bright light. I couldn’t see a thing for nearly a day. The Hunters had to guide me around. When I finally got my vision back… I was here. Wherever here is.”

       Praew’s eyes widened. “Wait, Asher! Is he okay?”

  Renwick froze. His mouth parted, but no words came. His gaze dropped to the floor. A faint shake touched his lower lip. He gave a small, trembling nod.

  “Yeah. I… I think so.”

  He forced a brave smile.

       “Wait.” Her eyes widened. “The Hunter test was weeks ago! You’ve been here this whole time?”

  “Yeah. There’s no way out of our cell.” He looked away, voice quieter. “What about you? What’s the last thing you remember?”

  She closed her eyes, trying to force her way through the fog.

  “I was with… Mai Azadi. Then—” She winced, jaw tightening. “I don’t know. I can’t remember.”

  Renwick hugged his knees, his hands trembling at his sides. She studied him. He was trying so hard not to cry.

  “Renwick, did you see anyone else brought here?”

       He nodded.

     When? What happened to them? What did they look like? So many thoughts raced through her head. She was ready to blast him with questions but stone me, that look… Pure horror painted Renwick’s face.

     Praew didn’t hesitate. She scooted forward and wrapped her arms around him. “Hey, we’re going to be okay, Ren.”

  He didn’t answer at first. He just sat there, stunned. Then, slowly, he leaned forward and pressed his face against her torso, arms circling her waist. His body felt small like that, shaken, clinging, fragile.

  “I don’t think we are, Praew,” he whispered, voice muffled against her. “I think… we’re going to die here.”

  “No,” she said. “We’re going to be okay.”

  His voice cracked. “How do you know?”

  “Because.”  That was all she could think to say. It was hard to stay positive in a situation like this.

  Renwick was unconvinced. “Does anyone even know where we are? Is anyone coming to save us?” 

  She went quiet. “No,” she admitted, hoping he wouldn’t notice the tears beginning to run down her face and drip into his hair.

     Renwick’s shoulders began to convulse as he wept. She held him tighter. Praew felt his breathing grow shakier with each tremble. Her own breath broke into the same broken rhythm. Their sobs and sniffling filled the cell.

     They stayed in each other’s embrace for a full flicker.

 Renwick stopped abruptly, his shoulders freezing in place as his breathing snapped to an unnatural calm.

  He sighed. Praew’s arms loosened instinctively.

  Renwick stood, wiped his hand on his pants.

       Praew didn’t move. Her arms hung useless in the air, the warmth of the embrace still echoing in her chest

       What? 

  He stepped toward the cell door and knocked.

  Moments later, a pair of boots stopped on the other side.

  “Your orders, sir?” said the guard hidden from view.

     That voice… wait. Orders?

     Praew stared at Renwick’s back, confused. 

     Sir? 

     A chill ran down her spine. Her mind screamed.

     As if to confirm her fears, Renwick spoke. “No one else is coming to rescue her. We’re in the clear. Let’s prepare her for the doctor.”

     The cell door clicked. Only now did Praew notice the strange handle, bulbous and rounded like a giant toe, jutting out from the center. Renwick gripped it and gave it a twist. It turned with a stubborn, grinding resistance.

     Renwick looked back and gave Praew a wink before walking out of her cell.

     The cell door screeched shut.

  From behind the bars, Praew watched in silence. Renwick sniffed the air and grimaced.

  “Our guest reeks,” he said, shaking his head. “One might say we’re being unaccommodating.”

  The guard gave a short nod.

  That’s when she saw it. Some sort of weapon at the guard’s side. At first glance, it looked like a sword hilt, but instead of a blade, a rounded rod extended from it. Embedded near the base was something unmistakable: a BoltStone.

  Clink! 

     The guard struck the weapon against the outer handle of her cell. Electricity crackled across the shaft. Sparks hissed as the charge surged through the odd hoor handle.

  On her side of the handle, an embedded ZephyrStone flared to life. 

  A blast of wind shot out, catching her square in the chest. How did I miss that!? The SolarStone embedded in the far wall burst to life, lighting up the space behind her.

  The force alone wouldn’t have been enough to throw her, but the slick floor made it worse. Still kneeling, she began to slide backward, unable to stop herself as her knees scraped across the stone. The gust pushed her toward the back of the cell.

  “You wish!” she shouted, throwing her arms out, bracing both palms against the walls. I’m not playing your games, you bolting Ratrod!

  Rough stone bit into her palms, but she clenched her jaw and refused to budge. She planted her right foot forward, sole flat to the floor like a man about to propose. Only this vow was a promise to fight. 

     Another clink!

     Her shoulders and arms throbbed. She kept shoving against both walls, joints screaming, like she was all that prevented them from closing in.

  Renwick didn’t speak. He simply lifted a bucket and hurled its contents through the bars.

  The soapy water struck her with a sharp slap, the ZephyrStone amplifying the speed of the throw. Cold soaked her front, clinging to her clothes, trailing into her hair. Tears escaped from the corners of her eyes, not from pain, but from the wind lashing her face. She could have turned away, but she didn’t.

  She stared straight at Renwick.

  Coward.

  The gust died. The ZephyrStone dimmed. Her arms trembled. Her palms burned. She rubbed them briefly, glaring at Renwick through soaked strands of hair.

  Of course, he only smiled and gave the guard a nod.

  Clink.

  The weapon struck the handle again. Another burst of air slammed into her. This time, she lost her grip before she could catch herself. Her back hit the far wall, spine first. Knocking the air out of her. 

     Once the wind faded, the soapy water began to trickle towards the cell exit.

 Heat enveloped her from the SolarStone just above. It was the only comfort she found.

 She didn’t move. Right foot braced, left knee folded beneath her, arms crossed in front of her face. She stayed like that, anticipating another gust of wind. Praew was too tired to do anything else. So bolting tired.

 The cell door creaked open. Heavy cuffs clicked shut around her wrists, stone restraints embedded with a NullStone for some reason.

     The guard, dressed in all black with a familiar-looking mask, stalked toward her. That looks like Ghost Operations… but that can’t be. They’re not allowed to touch me. Not while I’m under Mai’s protection.

  “Mai Azadi is going to kill you,” Praew said.

  No response.

  The guard hauled her to her feet and guided her out.

     Praew was greeted by Renwick at the cell exit.

     The hallway was wide, flanked by stone cells. To her right, only a closed door. But to the left, she could make out at least two more cells on each side before the gloom swallowed the rest. Each one seemed separated by a thick slab of stone, maybe wide enough to fit five of her laid flat, head to toe. The spacing kept every prisoner isolated.

 “Did you have a pleasant bath?” Renwick asked, smirking.

 “Yes. Thank you for getting the stench of lying Ratrod scum off of me,” Praew shot back.

     Renwick gasped mockingly. He turned casually down the hall, arms laced behind his head as if they were out for a stroll. The guard shoved Praew forward to follow.

  “You wound me again, Praew. Only asking if Asher was okay, never asking about me? That’s true cruelty. When I heard you were coming, I put in quite the effort to make sure you received a warmer welcome than I ever did.”

  He shook his head, as if disappointed.

  Definitely still Renwick. He always loved to hear himself talk, that bolting Ratrod.

       A faint whimper drifted through the hall. Praew’s ears twitched.

  “Mommy?” a girl’s voice called. Fragile. Tear-soaked. “Mommy, where are you?”

     Praew’s blood chilled. She rushed to the cell opposite her own, pressing her face to the bars.

  Empty. Not even a shadow, only silence lingered. The echo of that cry lingered in her skull like a memory she didn’t own.

     Had she imagined it?

     Renwick hadn’t even paused.

  “You see,” he continued, “I never lied to you. I told you exactly how I arrived here. Almost died too. But after my recovery, well, let’s just say I found the doctor quite agreeable. Especially when I learned we shared similar tastes.”

     Praew’s eyes began to water. What is that smell? A charred, sickly scent crept up her nose, flaring as it climbed.

     Renwick chuckled. “Yeah, you’ll get used to the smell. The doctor’s work may be deadly, but it’s nothing short of brilliant. How could I not support such ambitious research?”

     A wet, hacking cough echoed from the next cell on her left. It rasped through the air, thick with phlegm, like whoever made it was barely clinging to life.

     Praew slowed instinctively to peer inside. No one was there. The cell was empty like before, yet the sound continued. It seemed to come from right behind the bars.

     Another cough followed. Then a low, guttural moan. Gasping breaths turned to choking and gagging.

     Is this really just in my head? But why do the cells look empty? What’s happening?

     The guard shoved her forward to keep moving. As she did, the choking ended in a long exhale. The cell went silent.

     That rotting scent had grown unbearable. A gush of salty saliva submerged her tongue. She spat and began breathing through her mouth instinctively to keep from retching.

     Renwick kept walking, unconcerned.

     He glanced back at her, voice laced with mockery. “I heard you made some new friends recently.”

  Praew snapped her focus toward him with a glare.

  “Aww, don’t look at me like that,” he said, giggling like a bolting moron.

  He stopped just before the final two cells. Turning toward her, he tilted his head and smiled faintly. “You should thank me. After all, I’m the reason your friends are still here.

  “Well… mostly.”

  Praew’s heart leapt. Her friends were here. They were okay.

  The guard opened the last cell and pushed her inside and locked it behind her. 

     Near the entrance, Junya sat with her hair matted, back hunched, arms tucked under her knees and legs arched in front of her. Her face was barely visible as she stared at the floor like a corpse.

    Zephyrs take me. She looks so broken.

    “Junya, are you okay?” Praew’s voice was a gentle whisper in the stale air.

    Without moving her head, Junya’s eyes flicked toward Praew and then snapped away.

    Is she angry? Praew feared an accusation might be hidden in her friend’s silence. Or perhaps it was her own buried guilt beginning to surface. Was training more important than protecting those you care for?

    Praew took a hesitant step forward.

    “Hey, Junya, I’m sorry it took so long to find you. I—”

    But Junya cut her off sharply, head snapping up to meet Praew’s eyes with a glare.

    “Now you pretend to be Praew? Get out of my head!” She clutched her hair at the roots, as if ready to rip out two fistfuls. “You’re not real!” she screamed. “Don’t you dare pretend to be her! She can’t be here. She can never come here!”

    Her voice trembled as she let go of her poor hair and bit down on her finger, trying to wake herself from a dream.

    “I’m real, Junya. I’m here.”

    Praew moved closer and tried to wrap her arms around her friend, but Junya flinched away.

    “No, no, no! You can’t be! Don’t be real, please!” Junya scooted backward until she neared the furthest wall of the cell. “If you’re real, then… then you’re as good as dead.” She broke off, gasping for breath. “And that will be my fault too. Please, don’t be real!”

    Praew followed slowly, her voice soft and reassuring.

    “Junya, it’s going to be okay. I’m going to get you—”

    She trailed off, the words dying on her lips as her eyes caught sight of something behind Junya.

    “What is that?”

    Stepping closer, Praew finally saw the source of the awful smell. A bloated, ruthlessly burnt corpse slumped in the shadows, the remnants of clothing identical to hers and Junya’s. Her stomach turned as the truth took shape in her mind.

    “Wannii!” Praew screamed.

    The cell door opened.

    “Ahh, what a tear-jerking reunion,” Renwick cackled behind her. “To be fair, I did say mostly.”

    She heard footsteps stalk toward her from the doorway.

    “You’re sick, Renwick! How… how could you?” Praew hissed through her tears.

    She spun and launched a fist at the approaching steps. The guard took the blow square in the mask, cracking it.

    “Ouch! And here I thought you were saving that kind of love for me,” Renwick called from the hallway, his voice thick with cruel amusement.

    Grabbing Praew with one hand, the guard lifted her with unnatural strength and threw her into Junya. Both slammed into Wannii’s corpse.

    Dazed, Praew watched as the guard yanked Junya out from behind and hurled her forward. In that cruel display of dominance, Junya’s hands and knees slammed into the floor with a slap.

     Sitting up to kneel, Junya’s hands came together, palms pressed tight like a desperate prayer. She then rubbed her hands back and forth frantically, her body trembling with each motion. “Please! Please don’t take me! Wannii needs me… I’m all she has!” she cried, voice breaking as tears streamed down her dirt-streaked cheeks.

     Ignoring her, the guard seized Junya by the arm, jerking her upward and began dragging her toward the cell’s exit. 

    Pushing herself up from the floor, Praew felt Wannii’s gooey, half-rotted skin smear along her arm and both her palms, leaving a cold, slimy trail, like blood tofu slithering down the throat.

     Staggering forward, she awkwardly caught Junya’s wrist with both her shackled hands and pulled. The slime on Praew’s hands coated Junya’s homemade protection bracelet, making it slick. As the guard pulled on Junya’s other arm, the bracelet rolled down her wrist and slipped over her hand. Praew’s grip finally broke when the bracelet slipped off Junya’s fingers and launched into the air.

    Praew’s feet slipped out from under her, and she landed on the ground hard.

    The guard dragged Junya toward the cell exit.

    Praew scrambled to get back up, but the door slammed shut in her face.

    “No, please! I’ll be good! Please don’t take me!” Junya’s voice strained with panic.

    A pillar blocked Praew’s view until something clattered into sight. The guard’s mask skidded across the floor and stopped just short of her cell.

    “Please, please—Wannii? You’re okay! But… but how? I thought—” Junya’s voice was cut off by the slam of a distant stone door.

    Praew stood there, shaking. She picked up the bracelet lying on the floor. A counterfeit BoltStone for protection. Yet it had done nothing for Junya when she needed it most.

    No… I failed to protect Junya when she needed me most. 

     Bracelet still in her hands, she slipped it on as a reminder of her own failure.
