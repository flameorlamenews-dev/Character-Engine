Chapter 4 - Stone Face

Present Day – Ravtra, Second Week of Ferta

Four days after the Hunter Test



Praew wouldn’t hesitate again. It had only cost her—oh, nothing major. Just all her hopes and dreams. Still, her new job wasn’t terrible.

It was way worse.

“Focus, Praew.” Her new bestie, drill instructor Stone Face, spoke in a low, emotionless growl, like metal grating stone.

     She held the squat. Legs burning. Back straight. There were no chairs in this class, only pain. He paced the room, and they were required to follow him with their heads. A test of focus, apparently. Turn the wrong way, and he’d single you out. Lucky her.

     Who did this guy think he was? The Prime Archon?

     He stood at the front of the training hall now, arms behind his back. When had he even gotten there?

     “You have a six-week training camp,” he said. “There are three classes but you will attend a lecture on only one subject per day. Today is Sifaralith combat, tomorrow will be Shadow Intelligence, the following day will be on Ghost Operation. That’s how you will spend the first three days of every week.”

     The room wasn’t quiet. Groans, grunts, and panting escaped the lips of the ten recruits in waves. Muscles shook but no one dared complain.

     “The remaining three days are reserved for tests. If no test is scheduled, you will use that time for personal training.” He paused. “I advise you to use that time wisely.”

     Stone Face let the words hang.

     “To qualify for Special Forces, you must pass two of the three classes. If you fail two…” His gaze moved across the room. “You’ll be reassigned as a soldier, guard, or sent to the mines.”

     Great, as if this wasn’t stressful enough already!

     “Any questions?” Stone Face asked.

     Sweat slipped into Praew’s eyes. She didn’t blink.

     Behind her, Junya whispered, “I want to die.”

     Bpen spoke up. “To be honest, sir, I can’t feel my legs. Can we take a break?”

     The instructor nodded, then barked loud enough to wake the surface: “Stand at attention!”

     Everyone stood. Sighs of relief rolled across the room like a wave.

     “Free! Finally free!” Wannii shouted.

     The instructor’s eyes swept over the class shaking his head in obvious disappointment. 

     That— “bolting Stone Face! If you’re going to torture us, you should at least have the decency to tell us your name!”

     The room went silent as the whole class stared at Praew jaws agape.

     “What?”

     The instructor loomed over her. “Stone faced? How… original.”

     Praew finally understood her mistake. “I’m Sorry sir, I…I didn’t mean to say that out loud.”

     “You meant to only think it?” The instructor asked.

     “I—didn’t mean— “ she started to protest but the instructor cut her off.

     “I don’t give a bolting horn what you Ratrods call me,” he bellowed, loud enough to shake the walls—although his eyes never left Praew. “So long as you don’t mind joining her after class for an extra 5-flicker squat.” He let that sink in, then turned to the rest of the room. “If the rest of you want to leave on time, you’ll call me… let’s see…” He scratched his chin, voice dipping to a murmur. “Professor Stone has a nice ring to it.”

     Then, louder again: “You lot can call me Professor Stone.”

     “If there are no more interruptions… let’s begin,” he said, lowering himself into the only chair and desk stationed at the front of the class. He placed the tips of his fingers on some kind of stack, but from where Praew stood, she couldn’t make it out.

     “Each of you, come up and take one paper. Then return to your spot and get back in a squat formation.”

     A chorus of sighs and groans followed—frustration, exasperation, and more than a little reluctance.

     Praew stepped forward with the others, took her paper, and read it as she walked back. To her surprise, it was neatly laid out—a simple breakdown of each week’s lesson, with notes on whether there’d be a test.



Week 1 – Sifaralith Classifications. No test.

Week 2 – Stone Durability & Spiral Decay. No test.

Week 3 – SurgeStone Mechanics. No test.

Week 4 – NullStone Mechanics.

Test – Combat display to be fitted for a weapon

Week 5 – Combat Applications. No test.

Week 6 – Final Evaluation: Combat against me, a Hunter.



“As you can see,” he continued, “we begin today with classifications.	

“You all know of Sifaraliths. Smooth, circular stones with a crystal embedded into a carved design on both faces. That design and its color let us identify the type. But the trigger? Good luck.”

     He tapped the stone desk. “Everything you know about stones falls under what we call Core Stones. And that’s only one of three kinds. Trust that you’ll learn the other two.”

     “Beast Stones come next. They come in all shapes and sizes—literally. These stones are Embedded into the bodies of surface Beasts. That’s what gives them power. The embedding can usually be found in a specific appendage, depending on the species. Take Ratrods. Every single one has a SolarStone Embedded into its horn. Their trigger? ZephyrStones. Wind. One breeze and those horns light up.”

     “When a Ratrod dies, its body—flesh, bones, everything—crumbles to dust exactly one day later. But not the Embedded part. That remains.”

     He glanced around. “We call that crumbling. You’ll understand why soon.”

     “Some of you are probably wondering how we get food from beasts like that. Simple. If the Embedded appendage—like the horn—is removed within that one-day window, the rest of the body decomposes normally. Same rate as a human.”

     He stood and pulled something from beneath the desk, a Ratrod horn and some kind of claw to show the class. “That’s why Beast Stones look different. You might find a horn, a claw, a leg bone, a fang, a beak—whatever part the beast was Embedded with. These things litter the surface. And that’s part of what makes it so dangerous.”

     “They’re one and a half times stronger than Core Stones. But even they break. When they do, they dissolve and reform into a circular Core Stone of the same type, rarity, and trigger. This is also called crumbling.”

     Praew raised her hand. “Wait… if Beast Stones are stronger, why don’t we craft weapons out of those instead?”

     Stone didn’t miss a beat. “Would you rather have a metal spear where you can replace Core Stones as needed, or a one-trick bestial spear that crumbles without warning? Plus, it’s nearly impossible to find a craftsman skilled enough—and willing enough—to waste time on a temporary weapon.”

     He paused. “Now, Nature Stones.”

     “They look like Core Stones. But they shimmer like glass. Never try to use them.”

     “Nature is alive. And it doesn’t accept our control. These stones are Embedded into plants, trees, metals, stone, and more. They usually trigger on instinct. But if an external force tries to activate them? If someone tries to manipulate the trigger? Somehow, they always know. And they’ll either refuse—or trigger at you instead.”

     “No one understands how they know. But they do. When a plant—”

     A sharp voice cut through the room as Wannii stood up. “Zephyrs take me! We’re all stuck down here! What’s the point in learning any of this? It’s not like we’re ever gonna see the surface anyways!”

     “Says who? Your previous teachers?” Instructor Stone asked curtly. “You’re not children anymore. Your emotional outbursts will not tolerated. You will join Praew after class.” He stepped out from behind the desk. “Everyone, stand up while I address the Tarnox in the room.”

     The room fell quiet as he slowly locked eyes with each student before speaking again. “You all wanted to be Hunters, right? Grew up knowing you were going to be the one. You dedicated your lives to train for it. You did everything you could to prepare for that special day—the Hunter test—and still, you failed. Guess what? You’re not special. That’s the same story as every other foolish kid who failed this year. Time to get over it.”

     He let the silence linger before continuing. “I want you all to ask yourselves—why did you want to be a Hunter? Was it for the adventure?” He looked at Wannii. “To protect the ones you loved?” He looked at Praew. “Because someone inspired you?” He looked at Bpen. “Or was it for the fame and glory?” He turned to Junya. 

     “You’ll never have the title of Hunter. But that’s all it is—a bolting title. Here, you’ll earn something far more valuable: purpose. And the chance to truly protect this city. There’ll be times you go to the surface, visit other cities, collect resources, even fight beasts—anonymously. It’s not about the glory. It’s about what you do with the opportunity you’ve been given.”

     He paused one last time. “Out of everyone who failed the Hunter test, you ten showed the most promise. Make no mistake, you’ve been given a second chance. It’d be wise not to squander it.”

     I can… still see the surface? Praew blinked. Emotions she’d buried started to crawl their way out—hope, freedom, the thought of breathing real air. A single tear slid down her cheek. She wiped it away before anyone noticed… hopefully.

     Instructor Stone’s voice cut through again. “The next person to have an outburst will fail my class. If you understand, get back into a squat position.”

     Everyone dropped back down—no groans, no complaints, no gloom. Just silence. 

     “Now, where was I?” Professor Stone asked.

     “When a plant… sir,” Wannii said, surprisingly attentive.

     “Ah, yes.” He nodded. “When a plant or structure Embedded with a Nature Stone is destroyed, the stone remains. These stones can be nullified—but never surged. You can carry them, but that’s it. The only real use is planting them—giving them a chance to grow back into what they were.”

     He stepped behind the desk. “We won’t go into planting. That’s advanced. But I will say this—” His voice dropped. “Embedded nature remembers. If you try to manipulate it, or harm it, the thing might hold a grudge. And nature’s wrath can be dangerous enough that the only way to earn its forgiveness…” He leaned forward. “Is to destroy it.” He paused. “Once it’s nothing but a Nature Stone again… it forgets. Even after it’s replanted.

     “That will be all for today. I want each of you to seriously think about everything you learned—and more importantly, think about your future. Meet back here, same time tomorrow to begin your Shadow Intelligence training. Your instructor will be waiting for you.”

     He turned toward the door. “Class dismissed.”

     Praew stood up and stretched her legs, groaning under her breath. Junya rolled her ankles with a wince. “Ugh… my legs are killing me.”

     “I know,” Praew said, giving a half-laugh. “I thought we were gonna be stuck like that forever.”

     Bpen let out a low whistle. “I still can’t believe what he said. That we’re actually going to the surface.”

     Wannii gave a short nod as they walked. “Didn’t think we’d ever hear that.”

     Praew smiled. “Right? Maybe this class won’t be so bad after all.”

     “Praew. Wannii.” Instructor Stone’s voice cut across the room like a blade. They froze. “I don’t recall dismissing either of you.” He didn’t look up from the Beast Stones on his desk. “Get back into a squat position.”

     Praew sighed and muttered “Well, I’ve been wrong before,” 

     Junya and Bpen chuckled, then waved as they headed out. “See you at the ration station.”

Looks like her new best friend, Drill Instructor Stone-Face, had an unfortunately good memory.



***



   Praew’s legs trembled as she continued to squat. She was going to collapse. She was sure of it. Beside her, Wannii grunted quietly through clenched teeth. Her knees shook just as badly.

“Enough,” the instructor said at last. “You’re dismissed.”

They both groaned in relief and dragged themselves to their feet.

Wannii leaned on Praew’s shoulder as they hobbled toward the hallway. “Stone me,” she muttered. “My legs are gone.”

Praew gave a weak laugh. “If this is day one, we’re not gonna make it to day six.”

They followed the scent of warm food toward the nearest ration station. It wasn’t large—just a squared-off space with stone walls, simple benches, and a single narrow window carved into the far wall where trays were handed out.

Each citizen received an identification card every year—name, age, occupation, and their ration portion etched into it. Every meal required a ration slip, a thin strip of paper matching the info on their card. No slip, no food—unless you had enough standing to have someone collect it for you.

This was Praew’s first time in a private ration station. Only ten people were there—the students, a quiet distributor waiting behind the window with hands outstretched for her and Wannii’s slips. Trays of room-temperature portions sat behind the distributor, clearly cooled off in their tardiness.

Junya waved them over. She sat with Bpen at the far table, already halfway through her meal.

Wannii, getting her food first, slumped onto the bench with a sigh. Praew followed, grabbing a tray before joining the others. Grain mash, bitter-looking vegetables, and a tough strip of something that might have been meat, littered her tray. Rations were determined by role—and they were all trainees, no matter how sore their muscles felt.

“Oh! you two are still alive?” Junya asked.

“Barely,” Wannii groaned. “He made us squat for, like, forever.”

“I can’t feel my knees,” Praew added, poking at her mash. “I think they liquefied.”

Junya grinned. “Well, at least you gave us a head start.”

There was a short pause as they all started eating.

Then Junya spoke again. “But seriously… who is that guy?”

“I don’t know,” Bpen said, setting his spoon down. “But I’ve seen that face before.”

Praew froze, spoon halfway to her mouth.

“It was about six years ago,” he said. “Do you guys remember that incident? When a beast broke into the city?”

The rest of the table went still. Not waiting for a response, he continued.

“Do you guys remember that incident from six years ago? When that beast broke into the city’s Second Floor?”

Junya blinked. “Wait… that was real?”

“It was real, all right,” Bpen said. “It had black scales, long horns that curved forward, a thick bushy tail. It was lean—muscular. Steam poured from its snout every time it breathed. The thing moved like it was carved out of rage.”

Praew tilted her head. “That was a Tarnox, wasn’t it?”

“Yeah. That’s what they said.”

He looked down at his tray, voice steady but tense. “It rampaged through everything. And for some reason, it charged straight toward a child. Everyone ran—even the kid’s own parents abandoned him. But then… suddenly, he was there.”

Bpen glanced up, eyes locked.

“Our instructor. He stepped in, calm as anything. As the beast charged, a blue sphere of light appeared between its horns. The sphere grew as it charged forward but Professor Stone reached out with one hand, grabbing it by the horn, twisted, and redirected it. Sent the thing charging headfirst into the bolting wall. It slammed hard and dropped—knocked itself out cold.”

He paused.

“Then the professor looked back at the kid, asked if he was okay, and just… grabbed the beast by the horn and dragged it back up to the surface. Like it weighed nothing.”

Wannii stared. “That can’t be real. How could anyone be that strong?”

Bpen’s voice dropped, softer but clearer than before. “I don’t know. But I will never forget that face… because I was that kid. And that’s the uncarved truth.”

The table stared at him in silence. Only then did Praew realize—the whole room was quiet. All ten students were watching Bpen, eavesdropping on the story.

“What?” Praew asked loudly, causing everyone to look away, suddenly whispering to each other and pretending they hadn’t been listening.

Her friends still stared at Bpen in silence, so Praew spoke first. “You must’ve been so scared.”

“Well, I—” Bpen fidgeted. “You know, I mean… it is what it is, ya know? …I’m fine.”

Noticing his discomfort, Praew shifted the topic just slightly. “Just think—one day, you’ll be the one standing between a beast and a civilian. I have to admit… I admire your bravery.”

Bpen couldn’t hide his smile, holding his head a little higher. “Yes, well—I mean, we all will soon, don’t you think?”

The others nodded. They continued to eat in silence for a while, until Junya whispered, “The portions are way smaller than they were in general education.”

The others agreed. Wannii added, “They announced the food shortage got worse recently.”

Praew looked up. “If that’s true, then why not accept more Hunters each year to get more food?”

The table went quiet. No one answered. Then Junya leaned in and whispered, “Because the food shortage is fake.”

Wannii instantly hushed her, hissing, Are you crazy?” She looked toward the distributor’s window. “Saying something like that could get you killed!”

Junya just rolled her eyes. The rest of dinner passed in silence, heavy with thoughts of Professor Stone, the Tarnox, and the food shortage.

They eventually said their goodbyes and went their separate ways. Praew waited a little longer before leaving—just so she could walk home alone.

She lived on the Third Floor, in the slums of the outer ring. It was something she’d never told any of her friends except for Haldric. It wasn’t that she was ashamed. She just knew they would pity her. And there was nothing she hated more than pity.

Her parents worked hard, and she couldn’t have been prouder of them. The best part about her new job was knowing that, one day soon, she could move them to the First Floor. From the slums to upper class. It was a privilege granted to all Special Forces and Hunters. That alone was reason enough to give this everything she had.

As Praew walked, her thoughts circled back to the food shortage. It wasn’t just the daily rations that had been reduced lately. It was the supplementary slips too, the ones people used to buy what were considered non-essentials: everything from snacks to clothes, tools, bedding, and furniture.

Regardless of what The Prime thought, those slips mattered. Not just for comfort, but for survival. And for Praew’s parents, they meant everything.

Her father worked as a stone sculptor, smoothing out walkways, shaping doorframes, sometimes carving benches or storage blocks when someone could afford them. Her mother was a woodworker. She crafted smaller things—handles, shelves, utensils, and, once in a while, something decorative if someone had slips to spare. But lately, fewer people did.

When supplementary rations dried up, so did the orders. And no matter how hard her parents worked, they couldn’t flourish. Eventually, her family had to move from the Second Floor middle ring to the Third Floor slums.

She reached the door of their home and pressed her hand to the stone, letting the stress of the day slip off her skin. From the outside, the house looked like nothing—just another shallow-cut home along the outer cave wall. But inside? Inside, it felt like stepping into a different world.

The floor was smooth, shaped by her father’s careful hands. The walls were carved with delicate patterns—winding plants, leafwork, and soft curves he called “flowers.” Real wooden chairs with soft cushions surrounded a table her mother had built, its edges curved and inlaid with darker wood in swirling patterns.

She’d been inside Asher’s home before. The Prime Archon may have had a larger home, but not even he had a more beautiful one.

This was the most beautiful place in the city, and it was her little secret.

Her father, Raksa, looked up from his spot near the wall, dust still clinging to his arms from work. “There’s our little pebble,” he said with a smile. “Well? Did they finally put you in charge yet, or are they still figuring out how brilliant you are?”

Her mother, Malai, stepped in from the other room, brushing her hands against her skirt. “Why are your shoes still on? And how was your first day?”

Praew chuckled as she leaned down to unlace her boots. “It’s not really a job yet. Six weeks of classes and tests first. They call it ‘Specialized training.’”

“Oho,” Raksa said, raising his brows. “So you’ve got six weeks of being yelled at by angry old men, then?”

“Basically,” she muttered, stretching her shoulders.

Malai studied her face, then softened. “You look ready to collapse. Go wash up and get some sleep, little pebble.”

Praew turned toward her room, then paused. “Hey Mae, Papa… I heard the rations were reduced,” she said quietly. “What are yours like?”

Her parents exchanged a look before Malai answered. “Oh? We didn’t even notice,” she said lightly. “Nothing to worry about. We’re still getting plenty.”

Raksa nodded beside her. “Don’t you have enough on your plate? Focus on your studies,” he said with a quiet chuckle.

Praew hesitated—just a moment—then nodded and got ready for bed.

Feeling clean and refreshed, she picked up the wooden handle of a device shaped like a hairbrush. She’d come up with the idea when she was ten, after studying Sifaraliths for the first time. Her parents surprised her with a working model on her eleventh birthday.

It had a smooth wooden grip and a stone head, inlaid with two carefully embedded Sifaraliths—stacked one behind the other. A SolarStone triggered by force, just strong enough to create heat from the light. And behind it, a ZephyrStone, triggered by the SolarStone, calibrated to blow a steady stream of warm air.

She smacked the side of the handle a few times against her palm—just enough to activate the trigger.

The device sizzled gently in her hand. Heat built across the surface. A moment later, a soft stream of warm air flowed through her hair.

Praew never understood why her parents refused to sell the design to the engineering or research branches of Stormhaven. Surely a device like this would earn them a mountain of supplementary rations. Every time she brought it up, they’d just smile and say, “It’s not ours to sell.”

 Once she stepped into her room and shut the curtain behind her, the quiet settled around her.

Her bedroom was even larger than her parent’s. The bed stood in the center like a work of art on display—an elegant blend of stone and wood, its arched frame carved with smooth precision. Thick bedding rested on top, layered with soft blankets and perfectly fluffed pillows. It looked impossibly luxurious compared to anything outside these walls.

The frame displayed carvings of winding floral patterns, gentle vines and petals etched in perfect symmetry. On the walls, her father had shaped an entire mural of the surface world—trees rising toward a carved sky, gentle hills marked with grass and water, and in the center, a radiant SolarStone etched into the ceiling like a sun frozen in time.

Just above her headboard, tucked into the curve of the arch, was her favorite part, a small carving that read, “We love you always -  Mae & Papa.”

Praew kneeled on the bed, her fingers brushing the words—her nightly ritual. After all, her parents had sacrificed so much to give her this life.

She slipped beneath the covers and closed her eyes, dreaming of the day she could do the same for them.
