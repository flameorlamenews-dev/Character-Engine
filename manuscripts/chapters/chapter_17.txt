Chapter 17 - Recorrection Program 



“Change is on the horizon. Who are we to stand in its way?”









Haldric couldn't believe his eyes. Carved out of the walls before him was a large balcony that circled a radiating chamber below.

And in that moment, he hated being right. 

The entrance to the Fifth Floor? It had been in The Prime’s bolting office this whole time.

When Haldric had been caught snooping before, it was only because The Prime had entered through a passage concealed behind some moving wall contraption in his office. At the time, Haldric had been too consumed by the shame of being discovered to question it. Too humiliated to notice what mattered.

Haldric and Godric crouched to sneak to the ledge of the balcony and peeked their heads just enough to look down. 

  Below, six masked statues ringed the chamber, half lost in the shadows. Near the center, in a spill of gore, blood, and bile, two bodies lay on the floor piled on one another beside a nearly upright stone slab, both bodies were twisted unnaturally.

  Haldric let out a silent sigh of relief to discover neither body was Praew’s.

  On the other side of the slab was the bolting Ratrod himself, The Prime Archon, completely unbothered by the gore before him.

  A wafting draft hit Haldric’s nose, causing his breakfast to surge up his throat and rest in his mouth. He shuddered and forced it back down.

  The Prime was a bolting sick man.

  He should have known. No, he had always known. Praew, Note, even The Prime himself had warned him in their own ways.

  Was the title of Heir truly worth it? Or, if he were honest, had he simply wanted a father so badly that he clung to denial? He’d wandered the city, pretending to search for Praew, while she needed him most. And for what? Just to avoid facing the truth?

  Stone me, The Prime could have just finished murdering people when he caught me snooping last night. 

  But those bodies below looked fresher. 

  If I’d suspected him sooner, could I have saved these two? One was a girl around his age. Praew will never forgive me if this was one of her missing friends.

  Haldric shook his head. Now is not the time for this! A true Hunter saves the living before mourning the dead. I need to find Praew!

  Asher’s voice popped into his head. “That bolting traitor! Please don’t let my father get away with this, Haldric!”

  Haldric gritted his teeth. Shut up, Asher! Are you really any better? Or did you forget the saying? ALL Rivers run cold.

  A figure entered the room below, pulling Haldric from… whatever that was.

  Was that Renwick?

  “What in the seven stones are you doing!?” Renwick yelled.

  Haldric flinched. How did he spot us?

  “Renwick,” The Prime said, voice flat, “I showed you the mural. When mankind grows fangs, claws, and scales, three will rise. Their hands will decide which force endures: the Beasts, the children of men, or the living breath of the world itself. The first life they choose to strike down will bind their fate. From that moment on, they will hunt the rest of that kin until none remain.

  “Now,” The Prime continued, “correct me if I’m wrong, but were we not threatened no more than a flash ago?”

  He pointed to something on the other side of the stone slab, just out of view. “Truly, would you risk our entire species over something as fleeting as unrequited feelings?”

  What unrequited feelings? Haldric didn’t know if he wanted his guess to be right or not.

  “Don’t you dare!” Renwick snapped, brandishing a sword toward The Prime.

  Where did he get the weapon from? Is he going to kill The Prime?

  The Prime didn’t seem to care, turning his back on Renwick to fiddle with whatever he’d pointed to earlier.

  Renwick raised the sword to swing.

  “Renwick,” The Prime said lazily, “drop the sword, stand down, and stand guard outside.”

  Renwick went unnaturally stiff, dropping the sword with a clang. “Yes, sir.” He turned and disappeared out the door below.

  Stone me, what just bolting happened?

  Godric started rounding the balcony to get a better view, so Haldric began to follow.

  “That bolting ratrod just doomed us all!” Godric hissed, staring at the stone slab before crouching below the railing a moment later. Haldric tried to peek his head up, but Godric pulled him back down. “Go home, Heir. Some things can not be unseen.”

  “No,” Haldric said. “I must… I have to see it for myself.”

  Godric nodded and let go of his shoulder.

  They found her.

  Praew was strapped against a slanted stone slab, nearly standing. Her wrists, ankles, and neck fixed in the same brutal restraints described in the medical records he had skimmed earlier. 

  Her head hung forward, lifeless. Blood streaked the front of her shirt, trailing down and pooling beneath her feet. Her skin looked colorless in the harsh light.

  Before Haldric could process what this meant, something else caught his eye.

  That…wasn’t a medallion, was it?

  Below her throat, something embedded in her chest reflected the light of the SolarStones.

  “Don’t panic,” Asher pleaded in his head. “If she’s bound, she might still be alive. Look for signs of life.”

   He’s right, Haldric thought.

  But she was way too still. Too limp. He was too high up to see whether she was breathing.

  And by the stones… all that blood.

  A rush of panic seized his chest.

  Praew’s head weakly turned to the side.

  His feet were already moving when Godric’s arm shot out, catching him before he could take another step. “Hold.”

  Haldric nearly shoved him off. “Move, stone you!” 



  “Look!” Godric hissed.

  Haldric followed his gaze, then stopped cold. Those six masked statues he had glanced over earlier… weren’t statues at all. Unnaturally still, masked figures stood watching the room below.

  They weren’t guards, nor were they Hunters. There was something else about them. Something wrong. 

  “Embedded Special Forces,” Godric growled quietly.

  Haldric’s pulse roared in his ears. He already knew what they were. Godric had told him earlier: some humans could fuse with Sifaraliths just like the beasts, and somehow that made them stronger.

  Godric growled. “No way through them.”

  Haldric’s teeth clenched. So what? Was he supposed to just stand here while Praew bled out? Every instinct screamed at him to go now, fight now, save her now — but Godric seemed certain they wouldn’t win.

  “Not yet, at least,” Godric whispered. “We need a distraction. Something big enough to lure them away.”

  Haldric’s mind raced. He shook his head in frustration. The Master Key around his neck brushed his chest.

  He grabbed his shirt, fingers wrapping around the key beneath it.

  Then it clicked.

  The Third Floor prisoners.

  Without hesitation, he turned to Godric. “I have an idea… if I can get to the Third Floor.”

  Godric nodded, but the fury on his face was… well, terrifying.

  I’ll take that as approval.

  “Then what are you waiting for?” Godric hissed. “I must reach her parents’ home to capture an Echo of their voices. So make your distraction right at the next flash. Do not let anyone see you. Then go home pretending like you know nothing.”

  His voice lowered.

  “Do you understand? Deny any involvement.”

  Haldric nodded. “I understand. But do you have another EchoStone I could use?”

  Godric rolled his eyes and pulled out both an EchoStone and a NullStone. “Here. These two are useless to me. They are both triggered by sound, so be careful.”

  Haldric nodded again, but before he ran off, he hesitated — looking back at Praew. “What will happen to her? It’s not like she can just hide in the city forever…”

  “I will take her to the surface,” said Godric. “I have connections with another city. She will be safe until you become the next Prime. Then she can return to her family.”

  Haldric nodded, only to hesitate again. “Please tell Praew… that… that I love her.”

  Godric stared at Haldric. “I’m not going to bolting say that, Heir. And would you get going? We don’t have all day!”



***



    Haldric moved quickly through the corridors of the city, footsteps light but urgent, the echo of them pressing at his ears. He kept brushing at his shirt as if to reassure himself the master key didn’t abandon him before becoming an accomplice in the highest level of treason.

     What if The Prime found the stones in the office? No. He’d hid them well. It would be impossible. And even if it wasn’t, it didn’t matter.

     The Prime had betrayed him first. Why Praew? Why would Arthur Rivers ever do that to her? No. Why would he do that to anyone?



     The image of Praew strapped against the slab clung to him like a weight around his ribs. Her head limp. Her clothes stained with blood. Her skin too pale in the harsh light. He swallowed hard. He couldn’t rid himself of what he saw.

     “This, Haldric, is your own fault,” The Prime accused in his head. “Foolish, you chose to believe my lies over the truth your friend made you witness. How unfitting of an Heir Archon.”

     He stumbled to a halt. Hearing Praew, Asher, and that woman in white was already unsettling. But The Prime’s voice invading his mind?

     Haldric shuddered before forcing himself back into motion.

     Praew found the truth, and I attacked her for it. Then when she needed me most, I wasted too much time pretending The Prime was innocent and unaware.

     He reached the stairwell leading to the Third Floor. The air was colder here, thicker. Empty. Perfect for what he needed. He descended with quick, controlled steps, gripping the railing once to steady himself before heading toward the prison hall. 

    His own words echoed in his head. “You’re not as smart as you pretend to be.”

    He tightened his hands until his palms ached. Praew’s voice, the way it cracked when he yelled at her, replayed with painful clarity. The hurt in her eyes. The way she flinched when he snapped.

     Haldric turned a corner sharply, nearly scraping a wall. His lungs were frantic by the time he spotted a guard up ahead, leaning on his spear, half asleep. Haldric slowed just enough to pass without drawing attention, keeping his head down like any restless Admin on an errand.

     He moved past the guard, heartbeat pounding, the rhythm matching each thought striking him one after another.

     He remembered Praew walking away, refusing to look back. That was the last moment he saw her before she vanished.

     The SolarStones along the prison walls flickered, dimming for the last time before the next flash.

     When has time ever passed so fast?

     The silence wrapped around him. No guards in sight. Luck or fate, he didn’t care.

     He stopped in front of the first cell.

     The flash began.

     Haldric reached beneath his shirt, pulled out the key, and hesitated.

     When will I see her again? What if I never do? What if I never get to say I’m sorry or tell her how I feel?

     Shaking his head, Haldric shoved the key into the first lock.

     He moved fast, with steady hands and a set mind. The Archon key fit every lock, just as promised. 

     Cell after cell swung open. Murderers. Thieves. Smugglers. Traitors. It didn’t matter. Some were infamous. Some were dangerous and… was that one Embedded?

     Haldric didn’t care.

     They poured into the hall in a frenzy, a mass of ragged bodies colliding with each other as they bolted for freedom. Shouts rose. Footsteps pounded. Someone laughed. Someone screamed. The guards didn’t stand a chance.

     Good.

     Cell after cell. Lock after lock. Hinges groaned. Bodies shoved past him, the air thick with heat and breath. The SolarStones flickered as the next flash crept closer.

     A voice called out from a cell he had already unlocked.

  “You think this changes anything? Some wounds never heal… and yours won’t either, not while you stand beside him.”

     Haldric froze. He looked back.

     War Claw Bresdin.

     Not so legendary now.

     The fallen Beast Hunter stood in the hall, unshaken amidst the chaos, rubbing the stump where his right hand used to be.

     No time for riddles.

     “I have no time for the cryptic nonsense of a crumbling relic,” Haldric called out.

     Without another word, Bresdin turned and walked away through the storm of bodies.

     Haldric shook his head, unlocking the last cell before pushing into the chaos as well.

     He kept his head low as he slipped out of the prison hall, the Archon key still warm against his chest. He didn’t look back. The echo of the last cell door creaking open rang in his ears like a private victory. That should be enough of a distraction.

     A muffled scream drifted from a path to his right.

     Let’s see how The Prime deals with this mess. The thought almost made him smile.

     He cut across the outer ring walkway, slipping past shadowed corridors and sidestreets as he headed toward the Third Floor’s central market. The heavy air felt lighter than it had all day. He imagined Praew exploring the surface like she had always dreamed of. He imagined seeing her again someday. Once he became Prime, he could welcome her back. But until then, he could relocate her family and keep them safe. When she learned of his consideration, he could finally make things right, fixing, and maybe even advancing, their relationship.

     Another shout echoed somewhere behind him. He was just glad the distraction was working.

     Someone rushed past him, brushing his shoulder hard enough to turn him half a step. He blinked, annoyed. If they knew how disrespectful they had been to their future Prime…

     Haldric continued walking until he stepped into the wide market square at the heart of the Floor. The stalls should have been active at this hour, crowded with shady vendors trying to sell questionable goods. Instead, several stalls had been knocked onto their sides. Supplies were scattered across the stone floor. A spilled jug left a wet streak that soaked the edge of Haldric’s boot.

     He slowed, confusion finally tugging him out of his thoughts.

     A prisoner stumbled out from between two fallen stalls, dragging a woman by her hair. A child clung to the woman’s shirt, crying out to her. The woman’s nails dug into the man’s hand as she tried to escape his grasp, voice breaking as she begged for mercy. The prisoner yanked harder. The child began to hit the prisoner’s leg. “Let go of my mommy!”

     Haldric’s feet stopped.

     “No,” the prisoner said. “This is your mother’s fault for reporting me, boy.”

     The prisoner backhanded the child. The boy fell to the floor, stunned silent. The woman twisted, desperate, reaching for him.

     “Hey!” Haldric yelled. “Let her—”

     The prisoner slit her throat before he finished the sentence.

     Blood sprayed across the stones in a sharp, violent burst. The woman collapsed in front of the child. The boy froze, covered in his mother’s blood, his small hands trembling as he clutched her clothes.

     The spreading pool reached Haldric’s boots. He couldn’t breathe. He couldn’t look away from the open eyes staring up at nothing.

     The prisoner laughed once and ran deeper into the chaos.

     Haldric didn’t move.

     The child’s sobs echoed across the entire square.

     Note’s voice swept through him. “The most dangerous thing to her right now is you, Haldric.”

     His oath to the Prime stabbed guilt into him sharper than any blade. “Every decision will be for the betterment of the city.”

     This is what I call betterment for the city? This… destruction?

     He looked at the woman’s vacant eyes. He looked at the child shaking above her.

     His voice barely rose above a whisper.

     “What have I done?”



***



Praew barely had the strength to lift her head, but she saw it—the tremor in The Prime’s fingers, breath, and shoulders. Even the way he looked at her changed. No longer a test subject, but as if she was something… divine.

     Praew felt nauseous. What?

     The Prime gasped a long, shuddering, almost reverent breath. “Finally,” his voice was soft. Fragile. “After fourteen years.”

     He was staring at the stone embedded in her chest.

     “Finally.”

     His hands hovered over it, close enough for Praew to feel a chill radiate from his palm. Or was it from the thing he’d forced into her? His disgusting fingers twitched. He almost touched it. Almost. Then he pulled back. Like he wasn’t worthy.

     The Prime sighed. His lips parted, whispering so softly she almost didn’t hear, “Our savior descends upon the unworthy to free us from our plight.”

     Praew’s heart raced. Zephyrs take me! He— he thinks I’m some kind of savior?

     His fingers curled into a tight fist, and for a moment she thought he was crying. Then he wiped his eyes, and the trembling vanished.

     The Prime reached into his coat.

     Praew swallowed hard as he pulled two stones free, a BoltStone and… an EchoStone?

      He used two fingers to slide them against each other in the palm of his hand. Slow. Deliberate. Like he was working a salve between his fingers

     “Ahh,” The Prime said, “you must be wondering what happens next.” He clasped his hands behind his back, the stones still in his grasp.

     “A BoltStone to your membrane in steady pulses makes the mind more impressionable,” he said.

     What?

     He wants— “to… control my thoughts?”

     The words slipped out before she could process them.

     “No, not exactly,” The Prime murmured, his tone maddeningly calm. “More like… guiding your emotions.”

     Guiding? My…emotions?

     She blinked. She wouldn’t believe something so ridiculous if it wasn’t for the Sifaralith stone sticking out of her bolting chest. She looked down at the stone and instantly found difficulty breathing. The stone rose and fell with her chest as she gasped for air. “You can’t do that. You can’t.”

     “I am sorry,” he said softly, “but I must.”

     Her heart raced. Her limbs strained against the restraints. No!

     “Please— don’t—”

     “Peace, Noble one. You will still be you,” The Prime continued, voice gentle like he was comforting a child.  As if he hadn’t already tortured her mentally and physically.  

     “I, unlike someone, do not believe in altering personalities. For I am no beast. I hold myself above such depravity.”

     He cast a sharp, disdainful glance at the scientist’s corpse sprawled over poor Junya’s limp body.

     Her pulse roared, breath shattering.

     This wasn’t happening.

     “You will just be more inclined to act as our guardian.”

     Guardian? Noble one? “Zephyrs take me! I’m not some guardian deity!” 

     Her body shook as her thoughts fractured. A fresh wave of fear surged up her throat.

     “Please,” she whispered. “Don’t do this, please—”

     “This,” The Prime began, “is a necessity.  I cannot have that much power go unchecked, now can I?”

     He moved behind her head, lifting the restraint.

     “Well,” he said, “this is for the best.”

     Cold pressed against her neck as something clicked into place.

     “Now,” The Prime said, completely emotionless. “You will feel a strong vibration within your head. Clear your mind and relax your body. Do not fight it. Resistance will only make the process … unpleasant.”

     “Why?” Praew gasped, her body jerking against the restraints. “Must everything be decided for me?!” Why can’t we choose anything in our own lives? Why are you so afraid of us?”

     “No, Noble one,” The Prime said. “I fear the story that begins with my end, and the end you are bound to begin.”

     The first pulse hit. A low, drumming thrummed inside her skull.

No!

Boom. Ka-boom. Boom. Ka-boom-boom. Boom. 

She clenched her jaw. 

I will not! 

Boom. Ka-boom. Boom. Ka-boom-boom.

Boom. Ka-boom. Boom. Ka-boom-boom. Boom. 

Let you! 

Boom. Ka-boom. Boom. Ka-boom-boom.

Boom. Ka-boom. Boom. Ka-boom-boom. Boom. 

Control… 

 Everything faded to black.

A distant voice called out to her.

Mother? Why was she here? Where was here?

She couldn’t shake the feeling that she was forgetting something… something important. Something more important than her own life. She needed to remember…

Her mother and father wrapped her in a much-needed hug. “Remember, little pebble,” her father said fondly. “You must leave the city for now. Go on that adventure you always dreamed of. Don’t worry about us. We’re fine.”

“Yes,” her mother agreed. “It sounds like a storm is brewing. Just never let the storm stop you. And only listen to others if your heart agrees.”

“We love you,” they said together.

“I love you too,” Praew said with a sniffle, burying her face in her parents— in her—

What was I doing again?

Ah. right. That’s what I needed to remember.

“Ratrod! You blasted Ratrod! You have doomed us all!”

Was that… The Prime?

“If we are, you can only blame your bolting self, Arthur.”

Strange. For a flicker, it almost sounded like… Godric. But it couldn’t be. Why would The Prime and Godric be in my home?
